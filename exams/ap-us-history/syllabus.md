# AP US History — Syllabus

Nine chronological periods defined by the College Board. Use these as quiz scope. For each unit, the **Key concepts** list enumerates the major developments a unit quiz must cover; a comprehensive quiz should touch most of them. These are the source of truth for quiz coverage — not just the one-line theme.

| Unit | Period       | Theme                                                               |
| ---- | ------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 1    | 1491–1607    | Pre-Columbian societies, contact, Columbian Exchange                |
| 2    | 1607–1754    | Colonial America, regional development, transatlantic trade         |
| 3    | 1754–1800    | French & Indian War, Revolution, Constitution, early republic       |
| 4    | 1800–1848    | Jeffersonian era, market revolution, Jacksonian democracy, reform   |
| 5    | 1844–1877    | Manifest Destiny, sectionalism, Civil War, Reconstruction           |
| 6    | 1865–1898    | Gilded Age, industrialization, immigration, the West                |
| 7    | 1890–1945    | Progressive Era, WWI, 1920s, Great Depression, New Deal, WWII       |
| 8    | 1945–1980    | Cold War, civil rights, Great Society, Vietnam, 1970s crises        |
| 9    | 1980–present | Conservative resurgence, end of Cold War, globalization, post-2000  |

## Unit 1 · 1491–1607 — Key concepts

- Diversity of pre-Columbian societies north of Mexico: Mississippian mound cultures (Cahokia), Pueblo/Ancestral Puebloan dry-farming in the Southwest, Pacific Northwest rank societies built on salmon and cedar, Eastern Woodlands maize-bean-squash horticulture, Great Plains hunter-gatherers, Great Basin foragers, Arctic maritime peoples
- Mesoamerican and Andean states: Aztec/Mexica tributary empire at Tenochtitlán, Inca administration
- European motives for Atlantic expansion: bypassing Italian/Muslim middlemen for Asian luxury goods, Reconquista crusading zeal (completed 1492), competition among Iberian monarchies, Renaissance humanism
- Maritime technology: caravel, lateen sail, astrolabe, compass, improved cartography; Portuguese school at Sagres
- Iberian exploration: Prince Henry, da Gama, Columbus (1492), Treaty of Tordesillas (1494), Magellan, Cortés (fall of Tenochtitlán 1521), Pizarro
- Columbian Exchange: New World → Old (maize, potato, tomato, tobacco, cacao, syphilis); Old → New (wheat, rice, sugar, cattle, pigs, horses, smallpox, measles, influenza)
- Demographic catastrophe in the Americas; virgin-soil epidemics; population collapse in central Mexico (~25M to ~1M over 80 years)
- Spanish colonial institutions: conquistadors, *encomienda*, *repartimiento*, *mita*, hacienda, *requerimiento*, casta system, Catholic mission system
- Labor transitions: Indigenous forced labor giving way to African chattel slavery; early Atlantic slave trade, Portuguese Cape Verde / São Tomé sugar models
- Ideological and legal debate over Indigenous humanity: Bartolomé de Las Casas, Juan Ginés de Sepúlveda, Valladolid debate (1550–51), New Laws of 1542
- Silver economy: Potosí (1545), Mexican silver, the "price revolution" in Europe, global circulation via Manila galleon
- Regional impact in what is now the US: Spanish Florida (St. Augustine 1565), New Mexico (Santa Fe 1610), horse frontier on the Great Plains (post-1680 Pueblo Revolt diffusion)
- First English attempts: Roanoke (1585, 1587, "lost" 1590), Jamestown (1607) as the closing event of the unit

## Unit 2 · 1607–1754 — Key concepts

- Chesapeake: Jamestown, Virginia Company, John Smith, starving time, John Rolfe and tobacco, headright system, House of Burgesses (1619), first Africans at Point Comfort (1619), Maryland (Cecil Calvert, Act of Toleration 1649), indentured servitude → chattel slavery transition
- New England: Pilgrims/Plymouth (1620, Mayflower Compact), Massachusetts Bay Colony (1630, Winthrop "City upon a Hill"), Puritan covenant theology, town meetings, Cambridge Platform; New Haven and Connecticut (Hooker, Fundamental Orders 1639); Rhode Island (Roger Williams, Anne Hutchinson)
- Middle Colonies: New Netherland → New York (1664), Pennsylvania (William Penn 1681, Quaker pacifism and toleration), New Jersey, Delaware; ethnic and religious pluralism (Germans, Dutch, Scots-Irish)
- Lower South: Carolina proprietors, rice and indigo, enslaved African majority in coastal SC; Georgia (Oglethorpe 1733) as debtor and buffer colony
- Slavery: shift from indentured to racialized chattel slavery after Bacon's Rebellion; slave codes (Virginia 1705); Stono Rebellion (1739); Atlantic/Middle Passage; triangular trade; task vs. gang system regional variation
- Native American relations and conflicts: Powhatan wars, Pequot War (1636–38), King Philip's (Metacom's) War (1675–76), Beaver Wars, Pueblo Revolt (1680, Popé), Yamasee War, Tuscarora War
- Bacon's Rebellion (1676): Nathaniel Bacon vs. Governor Berkeley, backcountry vs. tidewater, consequences for slavery and class
- Salem witchcraft trials (1692)
- Halfway Covenant (1662) and Puritan religious decline
- First Great Awakening (1730s–40s): Jonathan Edwards, George Whitefield, New Lights vs. Old Lights, religious pluralism, forerunner of revolutionary egalitarianism
- Enlightenment in the colonies: Benjamin Franklin, deism, scientific societies, literacy
- Atlantic economy and mercantilism: Navigation Acts (from 1651), Board of Trade (1696), salutary neglect, triangular trade, cash-crop dependence, consumer revolution
- Imperial wars: King William's War (1689–97), Queen Anne's War (1702–13, Treaty of Utrecht), King George's War (1744–48) — prelude to the French and Indian War
- French Louisiana and New France: fur trade, *coureurs de bois*, Jesuit missions, alliances with Algonquian and Huron; Spanish borderlands (Florida, Texas, New Mexico, California missions later)
- Colonial politics: salutary neglect, colonial assemblies, governors, "rights of Englishmen" ideology

## Unit 3 · 1754–1800 — Key concepts

- French and Indian War / Seven Years' War (1754–63): Albany Congress/Plan (1754), Washington at Fort Necessity, Braddock's defeat, British victory under Pitt, Treaty of Paris (1763), expulsion of Acadians, British debt
- Post-war imperial policy: Proclamation of 1763, Pontiac's Rebellion, standing army in America
- Taxation and resistance: Sugar Act (1764), Stamp Act (1765, Stamp Act Congress, Sons/Daughters of Liberty, "no taxation without representation," virtual vs. actual representation), Declaratory Act (1766), Townshend Acts (1767), Boston Massacre (1770), Tea Act and Boston Tea Party (1773), Coercive/Intolerable Acts (1774), Quebec Act
- Continental Congresses: First (1774, Declaration of Rights and Grievances, Continental Association), Second (1775, Olive Branch Petition, Continental Army, Washington as commander)
- Ideological roots: Enlightenment (Locke natural rights, Montesquieu separation of powers), classical republicanism, "country" opposition thought, Thomas Paine *Common Sense* (1776)
- Declaration of Independence (1776): Jefferson, grievances, natural rights
- War for Independence: Lexington & Concord (1775), Bunker Hill, NY campaign, Trenton/Princeton, Saratoga (1777) turning point, French alliance (1778), Valley Forge, Yorktown (1781); role of women, enslaved people (Dunmore's Proclamation, Book of Negroes), Native allies
- Treaty of Paris (1783)
- State constitutions and revolutionary-era reforms: religious disestablishment, primogeniture abolished in some states, gradual abolition in the North, Northwest Ordinance (1787)
- Articles of Confederation (1781–89): accomplishments (Land Ordinance 1785, Northwest Ordinance 1787), weaknesses (no taxation/commerce power, unanimous amendment)
- Shays' Rebellion (1786–87) and calls for stronger government
- Constitutional Convention (1787): Virginia Plan vs. New Jersey Plan, Great (Connecticut) Compromise, Three-Fifths Compromise, slave trade clause, fugitive slave clause; Madison as "father"
- Ratification debate: Federalists (Madison, Hamilton, Jay — *The Federalist Papers* 1787–88) vs. Anti-Federalists (Brutus, George Mason, Patrick Henry), demand for a Bill of Rights (ratified 1791)
- Washington's presidency: Hamilton's financial program (assumption of state debts, Bank of the United States, tariff, excise/whiskey tax), strict vs. loose constitutional construction, Jefferson-Hamilton split, Whiskey Rebellion (1794), Proclamation of Neutrality (1793), Jay's Treaty (1794), Pinckney's Treaty (1795), Farewell Address (1796)
- Native conflict and policy: Northwest Indian War, Battle of Fallen Timbers (1794), Treaty of Greenville (1795)
- Emergence of first party system: Federalists vs. Democratic-Republicans
- Adams' presidency: XYZ Affair (1797–98), Quasi-War, Alien and Sedition Acts (1798), Virginia and Kentucky Resolutions (1798–99, Jefferson and Madison, nullification doctrine)
- Revolutionary social legacy: republican motherhood, companionate marriage, early abolition in North, free Black communities, religious pluralism
- Election of 1800 ("Revolution of 1800")

## Unit 4 · 1800–1848 — Key concepts

- Jeffersonian era: Revolution of 1800, limited government ideology, Louisiana Purchase (1803), Lewis and Clark (1804–06), Barbary Wars, Embargo Act (1807), Non-Intercourse Act
- Marshall Court: *Marbury v. Madison* (1803, judicial review), *Fletcher v. Peck* (1810), *McCulloch v. Maryland* (1819), *Dartmouth College v. Woodward* (1819), *Gibbons v. Ogden* (1824), *Worcester v. Georgia* (1832)
- War of 1812: causes (impressment, Orders in Council, War Hawks — Clay/Calhoun, Tecumseh's confederacy, Battle of Tippecanoe), events (burning of Washington 1814, Fort McHenry, Battle of New Orleans 1815), Hartford Convention (1814) and Federalist collapse, Treaty of Ghent
- Era of Good Feelings and the American System (Clay): Second Bank of the US (1816), protective Tariff of 1816, internal improvements debate
- Monroe Doctrine (1823)
- Market Revolution: transportation (National/Cumberland Road, Erie Canal 1825, steamboats — Fulton, railroads from 1830s, telegraph — Morse 1844), manufacturing (Lowell/Waltham system, interchangeable parts — Whitney, putting-out system), commercial agriculture (cotton gin 1793 and the cotton boom), banking and credit
- Immigration wave: Irish Catholic and German immigrants 1830s–40s; nativist response (Know-Nothing precursors)
- Expansion of slavery and the Missouri Compromise (1820): 36°30′ line, Tallmadge amendment controversy
- Growth of the "peculiar institution": internal slave trade, domestic slave breeding, plantation economy, slave culture (religion, family, music), resistance — Gabriel's Rebellion (1800), Denmark Vesey (1822), Nat Turner's Rebellion (1831), tightened slave codes; pro-slavery apologetics (Calhoun "positive good," Fitzhugh)
- Jacksonian democracy: expansion of white male suffrage, spoils system, national nominating conventions, rise of the Democratic Party and the Second Party System (Democrats vs. Whigs), election of 1828
- Andrew Jackson's presidency: Bank War and Bank Veto (1832), "pet banks" and Specie Circular (1836), Nullification Crisis (Tariff of Abominations 1828 → South Carolina nullification → Compromise Tariff and Force Bill 1833), Indian Removal Act (1830), Trail of Tears (1838, Van Buren), Five Civilized Tribes, Seminole Wars; "King Andrew" Whig critique
- Panic of 1837
- Second Great Awakening: revivalism, Charles Grandison Finney, burned-over district, perfectionism; Mormons (Joseph Smith 1830, Book of Mormon, Nauvoo, martyrdom 1844, Brigham Young's Utah migration); Millerites; Unitarianism, Universalism
- Reform impulse: temperance (American Temperance Society, Maine Law 1851), education reform (Horace Mann, common schools), asylum and prison reform (Dorothea Dix), Sabbatarianism, peace societies
- Utopian communities: Shakers, Oneida (Noyes), Brook Farm, New Harmony (Owen), Fourierist phalanxes
- Transcendentalism: Emerson ("Self-Reliance," "The American Scholar"), Thoreau (*Walden*, "Civil Disobedience"), Fuller
- Abolitionism: early colonizationists (ACS, Liberia), radical immediatism — William Lloyd Garrison and *The Liberator* (1831), American Anti-Slavery Society (1833), Frederick Douglass, Harriet Tubman, Sojourner Truth, David Walker's *Appeal*, Underground Railroad, Grimké sisters, split over women's role (1840)
- Women's rights: cult of domesticity / "separate spheres," female academies, Seneca Falls Convention (1848) and Declaration of Sentiments (Stanton, Mott), *Lily* (Amelia Bloomer)
- Antebellum culture: Hudson River School, penny press, early baseball, minstrelsy, Irving, Cooper, Poe

## Unit 5 · 1844–1877 — Key concepts

- Manifest Destiny as ideology: John O'Sullivan (1845), "Young America," racial and religious justifications, Oregon fever
- Expansion episodes: Texas revolution (Alamo 1836, Republic of Texas), Texas annexation (1845), Oregon boundary (54°40′ vs. 49th parallel, 1846 treaty), Mexican-American War (Polk, Rio Grande casus belli 1846, Scott/Taylor campaigns, Mexico City), Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo (1848) and Mexican Cession, Gadsden Purchase (1853)
- California Gold Rush (1849) and statehood debate
- Mormon migration to Utah (1847) and the "Deseret" project
- Wilmot Proviso (1846) and the sectional crisis over slavery in the territories
- Compromise of 1850 (Clay, Douglas): California free, Fugitive Slave Act, popular sovereignty for NM/UT, end of DC slave trade; Northern resistance (personal liberty laws, Anthony Burns case)
- *Uncle Tom's Cabin* (Stowe 1852)
- Kansas-Nebraska Act (1854, Stephen Douglas): popular sovereignty, repeal of Missouri Compromise, Bleeding Kansas, Lecompton Constitution, Pottawatomie massacre, Sumner-Brooks caning (1856)
- Collapse of the Second Party System: Whigs destroyed; Republican Party formed (1854) as an anti-slavery-expansion coalition; Know-Nothing (American Party) nativist surge
- *Dred Scott v. Sandford* (1857): Black citizenship denied, Missouri Compromise declared unconstitutional
- Lincoln-Douglas debates (1858), Freeport Doctrine
- John Brown: Pottawatomie (1856), Harpers Ferry raid (1859) and Southern reaction
- Election of 1860 and secession: four-way race (Lincoln, Douglas, Breckinridge, Bell); South Carolina secedes (Dec 1860); Confederate States of America (Feb 1861); Crittenden Compromise fails; Alexander Stephens's "Cornerstone Speech" (1861); secession declarations explicitly cite slavery
- Civil War military narrative: Fort Sumter (April 1861), First Bull Run, Union strategy (Anaconda Plan, blockade, Mississippi corridor, capture Richmond), Antietam (Sept 1862), Vicksburg/Gettysburg (July 1863), Chattanooga, Grant's Overland Campaign (1864), Sherman's March to the Sea (1864), Appomattox (April 1865), Lincoln's assassination (April 14, 1865)
- Wartime politics: Lincoln's leadership, suspension of habeas corpus, Copperheads (Vallandigham), New York Draft Riots (1863), Confiscation Acts; foreign policy (Trent Affair, preventing British/French recognition); Emancipation Proclamation (Jan 1, 1863), United States Colored Troops / 54th Massachusetts, Gettysburg Address, Second Inaugural
- Women in the war: Sanitary Commission, Clara Barton, nurses, widows, Confederate bread riots
- Republican wartime economic program: Homestead Act (1862), Morrill Land-Grant Act (1862), Pacific Railway Act (1862), National Banking Acts (1863–64), greenbacks, high protective tariffs
- Confederate home front: states' rights paradox, inflation, class tension
- 13th Amendment (1865)
- Presidential Reconstruction: Lincoln's 10% Plan and Wade-Davis Bill (vetoed), Freedmen's Bureau (1865), Andrew Johnson's lenient Reconstruction, Black Codes (1865–66), Memphis and New Orleans riots (1866)
- Congressional/Radical Reconstruction: Civil Rights Act (1866), 14th Amendment (1868), Military Reconstruction Acts (1867), Tenure of Office Act (1867), Johnson's impeachment and acquittal (1868), 15th Amendment (1870)
- Freedpeople's politics and institutions: Black congressmen (Hiram Revels, Blanche Bruce), ~2,000 Black officeholders in the South, Black churches, HBCUs (Howard, Fisk), schools, land hunger and "forty acres and a mule" failure
- White resistance and violence: Ku Klux Klan, White League, Red Shirts; Enforcement Acts (1870–71); Colfax Massacre (1873)
- Supreme Court retreat: *Slaughter-House Cases* (1873), *U.S. v. Cruikshank* (1876), *Civil Rights Cases* (1883)
- Economic aftermath in the South: sharecropping, tenant farming, crop-lien system, debt peonage
- Panic of 1873 and Grant-era scandals (Credit Mobilier, Whiskey Ring)
- Liberal Republican revolt (1872) and Northern exhaustion
- Compromise of 1877: Hayes-Tilden disputed election, withdrawal of federal troops, formal end of Reconstruction

## Unit 6 · 1865–1898 — Key concepts

- Second Industrial Revolution technology: Bessemer steel, electricity (Edison, Tesla, Westinghouse), petroleum refining, telegraph and telephone (Bell), typewriter, sewing machine; scientific management (Taylor later)
- Corporate consolidation: vertical integration (Carnegie Steel), horizontal integration and the trust (Rockefeller's Standard Oil), interlocking directorates and finance capitalism (J.P. Morgan), Vanderbilt and railroads
- Ideology: Social Darwinism (Sumner, Spencer), Horatio Alger's rags-to-riches, Gospel of Wealth (Carnegie 1889), Social Gospel (Rauschenbusch, Gladden), countervailing critiques (Henry George's *Progress and Poverty* 1879, Edward Bellamy's *Looking Backward* 1888)
- Railroads: transcontinental (Union Pacific + Central Pacific, 1869), Chinese and Irish labor, federal land grants, time zones, rebates and long-/short-haul abuses
- Regulation: Interstate Commerce Act (1887) and ICC, Sherman Antitrust Act (1890) and its early anti-labor use (*In re Debs* 1895)
- Labor: Great Railroad Strike (1877) and federal troops, Knights of Labor (Powderly, inclusive across skill/race/gender but collapsed after Haymarket), Haymarket affair (Chicago 1886), American Federation of Labor (Gompers 1886, skilled craft unionism), Homestead Strike (1892, Carnegie/Frick/Pinkertons), Pullman Strike (1894, Debs, federal injunction, Cleveland's use of troops); Molly Maguires
- New Immigration: shift from northern/western to southern/eastern Europe (Italians, Poles, Jews, Greeks, Slavs); Chinese and later Japanese on the Pacific Coast; Ellis Island (1892), Angel Island; push and pull factors
- Nativism and restriction: American Protective Association, Chinese Exclusion Act (1882, first national-origin ban, extended and made permanent in 1902), Immigration Act of 1891, literacy test proposals
- Urbanization: tenements (dumbbell), Jacob Riis's *How the Other Half Lives* (1890), streetcar suburbs, skyscrapers, department stores, mass-circulation newspapers (Pulitzer, Hearst), Coney Island
- Political machines and bosses: Tammany Hall (Tweed), immigrant patronage networks; Pendleton Civil Service Act (1883) after Garfield assassination
- Reformers at the urban margin: Settlement-house movement — Hull House (Jane Addams and Ellen Gates Starr, 1889), Lillian Wald, Florence Kelley; Salvation Army; WCTU (Frances Willard)
- The West: Homestead Act (still operative), sodbusters, bonanza farms, cattle kingdom and cow towns (Chisholm Trail), mining rushes (Comstock Lode), Mormon consolidation in Utah and statehood (1896); closing of the frontier (1890 Census) and Frederick Jackson Turner's frontier thesis (1893)
- Indigenous dispossession: Plains Indian Wars — Sand Creek (1864), Red River War, Little Bighorn (1876), Nez Perce/Chief Joseph (1877), Apache (Geronimo); reservation policy; Dawes Severalty Act (1887) and allotment; Carlisle Indian Industrial School and assimilation; Ghost Dance and Wounded Knee Massacre (1890); Helen Hunt Jackson's *A Century of Dishonor* (1881)
- New South: Henry Grady's "New South" ideology, industrial aspirations (textiles, steel at Birmingham), persistence of cotton monoculture and tenancy; redemption of Southern state governments by Democrats
- Racial regime: lynching (Ida B. Wells's anti-lynching crusade), disfranchisement via Mississippi Constitution (1890) — poll taxes, literacy tests, grandfather clauses, white primaries; Jim Crow segregation in custom and law; *Plessy v. Ferguson* (1896); Booker T. Washington's Atlanta Compromise (1895) and Tuskegee
- Agrarian discontent and Populism: Grange (1867) and "Granger laws," Greenback Party, Farmers' Alliances (Northwestern and Southern, Colored Farmers' Alliance), subtreasury plan, formation of the People's (Populist) Party and Omaha Platform (1892, free coinage of silver at 16:1, graduated income tax, direct election of senators, government ownership of railroads, telegraphs), Panic of 1893, "Coxey's Army" (1894), William Jennings Bryan's "Cross of Gold" speech and 1896 election against McKinley (realigning), Gold Standard Act (1900)
- Gilded Age national politics: close partisan balance, high turnout, patronage, tariff as the central federal issue (McKinley Tariff 1890), limited presidential initiative
- Women's movement: NAWSA (1890, Stanton/Anthony/Catt), Western state suffrage (Wyoming 1890), women in higher education and the professions
- Overseas turn at the decade's end (transition to Unit 7): Hawai'i overthrow (1893) and annexation (1898), Cuban revolt, USS *Maine* (1898), Spanish-American War (1898), Teller and Platt Amendments, Treaty of Paris (1898)

## Unit 7 · 1890–1945 — Key concepts

### Empire and foreign policy, 1890s–1917

- Ideological roots of empire: Alfred Thayer Mahan's sea power, Josiah Strong, "White Man's Burden," closing frontier
- Spanish-American War (1898): yellow press (Hearst, Pulitzer), *Maine*, Cuba and the Philippines, Teller and Platt Amendments, Treaty of Paris; Anti-Imperialist League (Mark Twain, Carnegie, Bryan)
- Philippine-American War (1899–1902), Emilio Aguinaldo
- Open Door Notes (John Hay, 1899–1900) and Boxer Rebellion (1900)
- Theodore Roosevelt's "Big Stick": Panama Canal (independence 1903, canal 1914), Roosevelt Corollary (1904), Russo-Japanese mediation, Great White Fleet, Gentlemen's Agreement (1907)
- Taft's Dollar Diplomacy, Wilson's moral diplomacy and interventions in Mexico (Veracruz 1914, Pershing vs. Villa 1916), Haiti, Dominican Republic

### Progressive Era

- Muckrakers: Lincoln Steffens *The Shame of the Cities*, Ida Tarbell on Standard Oil, Upton Sinclair *The Jungle* (1906), Jacob Riis, *McClure's Magazine*
- Progressive intellectual sources: Social Gospel, pragmatism (William James, John Dewey), efficiency/scientific management, experts
- Municipal and state reform: commission and city-manager government, Wisconsin Idea (La Follette), initiative/referendum/recall, direct primary
- National Progressive legislation: Pure Food and Drug Act and Meat Inspection Act (1906), Hepburn Act (1906, ICC with teeth), Mann-Elkins Act (1910), Federal Reserve Act (1913), Clayton Antitrust Act (1914), Federal Trade Commission (1914), Keating-Owen child labor act (1916, later struck down), Adamson Act
- Progressive amendments: 16th (income tax, 1913), 17th (direct election of senators, 1913), 18th (Prohibition, 1919), 19th (woman suffrage, 1920); Prohibition support (WCTU, Anti-Saloon League, Volstead Act)
- TR's Square Deal: 1902 coal strike arbitration, trust-busting (Northern Securities), Newlands Reclamation Act (1902), Gifford Pinchot and conservation, national parks/forests, Antiquities Act
- Election of 1912: TR's Bull Moose Progressive Party ("New Nationalism") vs. Wilson's "New Freedom" vs. Taft vs. Debs; Wilson wins
- Women's suffrage endgame: Alice Paul and the National Woman's Party, Silent Sentinels, Carrie Chapman Catt's "Winning Plan," 19th Amendment (1920)
- African American activism: Niagara Movement (1905, Du Bois), NAACP founded (1909), Urban League (1911); lynching persists (Wells-Barnett)
- Labor radicalism: IWW / "Wobblies" (1905, Haywood), Lawrence textile strike (1912); Eugene Debs and the Socialist Party of America

### World War I

- Causes and US neutrality: militarism/alliances/imperialism, Wilson's early neutrality, trade with Allies, U-boat warfare, *Lusitania* (1915), Sussex Pledge, preparedness movement
- US entry (April 1917): Zimmermann Telegram, renewed submarine warfare, "world safe for democracy"
- Home front: Selective Service Act, War Industries Board (Baruch), Food Administration (Hoover), Committee on Public Information (Creel), Liberty Bonds, anti-German sentiment; Espionage Act (1917) and Sedition Act (1918); *Schenck v. United States* (1919, "clear and present danger")
- First Great Migration: Black southerners to northern cities, race riots (East St. Louis 1917, Chicago 1919)
- Military: American Expeditionary Force (Pershing), Meuse-Argonne, armistice (Nov 11, 1918)
- Wilson's Fourteen Points, Paris Peace Conference, Treaty of Versailles and League of Nations; Senate rejection (Henry Cabot Lodge's reservationists, Irreconcilables)
- First Red Scare: Bolshevik fear, Palmer Raids (1919–20), Sacco and Vanzetti (executed 1927), anarchist bombings, steel strike (1919), Seattle general strike

### 1920s

- Republican ascendancy: Harding's "return to normalcy," Teapot Dome scandal, Coolidge, Hoover
- Economic prosperity and its limits: assembly-line mass production (Ford Model T, $5 day), consumer credit, advertising (Bruce Barton), electrification, radio, cinema, stocks-on-margin; agricultural depression persists
- Cultural conflict: Jazz Age, flappers, new morality, urban vs. rural tensions
- Harlem Renaissance: Langston Hughes, Zora Neale Hurston, Countee Cullen, Claude McKay, Duke Ellington, Louis Armstrong, Cotton Club
- Lost Generation: Hemingway, Fitzgerald (*The Great Gatsby*), Eliot, Stein
- Fundamentalism vs. modernism: Scopes "Monkey" Trial (1925, Bryan vs. Darrow), fundamentalist movement
- Immigration restriction: Emergency Quota Act (1921), National Origins Act (1924, quotas based on 1890 census, barring most Asians and South/East Europeans)
- Second KKK: anti-Black, anti-Catholic, anti-Jewish, anti-immigrant, national reach
- Women after suffrage: professional and clerical work, continued wage gap, Equal Rights Amendment proposal (1923, Alice Paul)
- Marcus Garvey and UNIA Black nationalism
- Prohibition's results: speakeasies, bootlegging, organized crime (Capone), uneven enforcement
- Foreign policy: Washington Naval Conference (1921–22), Kellogg-Briand Pact (1928), Dawes Plan (1924), high tariffs (Fordney-McCumber 1922), Good Neighbor Policy (FDR later)

### Great Depression and New Deal

- Causes of the Depression: stock speculation, bank fragility, underconsumption, agricultural weakness, international debt cycles, Federal Reserve missteps; 1929 crash
- Hoover's response: limited federal action, Reconstruction Finance Corporation (1932), Hawley-Smoot Tariff (1930, backfire), public works; Bonus Army (1932)
- Human cost: Hoovervilles, Dust Bowl (Great Plains, *The Grapes of Wrath*, Okies to California), 25% unemployment, bank runs
- FDR and the First New Deal (1933–35): Hundred Days, Emergency Banking Act, bank holiday, Glass-Steagall Act / FDIC, CCC, AAA (production controls; struck down 1936), NIRA/NRA (Blue Eagle; struck down *Schechter* 1935), TVA, Civil Works Administration, HOLC, FHA, Securities Act / SEC, end of gold standard
- Second New Deal (1935–38): Works Progress Administration (WPA — arts projects, Federal Writers' Project), Social Security Act (1935, old-age pensions, unemployment, ADC), Wagner Act / National Labor Relations Board (1935), Fair Labor Standards Act (1938, minimum wage, 40-hour week, child labor ban), US Housing Act, revenue/"soak the rich" acts
- Critics: Huey Long ("Share Our Wealth"), Father Coughlin, Dr. Francis Townsend; business critics (American Liberty League); Communist Party and Popular Front; court-packing plan (1937) backlash and "switch in time"; "Roosevelt Recession" (1937–38)
- Labor upsurge: Committee for Industrial Organization → Congress of Industrial Organizations (CIO, John L. Lewis), sit-down strikes (Flint GM 1936–37), organizing mass-production industries
- Dust Bowl policy: Soil Conservation Service, shelterbelts
- Indian New Deal: Indian Reorganization Act (1934, Collier)
- African Americans and the New Deal: shift to the Democratic Party, Eleanor Roosevelt advocacy, Mary McLeod Bethune and the "Black Cabinet," discrimination within programs
- Political realignment: New Deal coalition (urban workers, immigrants, African Americans, South, intellectuals)

### World War II

- 1930s isolationism: Nye Committee (1934–36), Neutrality Acts (1935, 1936, 1937), *America First Committee*, Lindbergh
- Aggression abroad: Japanese invasion of Manchuria (1931) and China (1937), Nanking; Italian Ethiopia (1935); Hitler's Germany and the Munich Conference (1938); Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact (1939); war in Europe (Sept 1939)
- Moves toward intervention: Cash and Carry (1939), Destroyers for Bases (1940), Lend-Lease (March 1941), Atlantic Charter (Aug 1941), undeclared Atlantic naval war
- Pearl Harbor (Dec 7, 1941); US entry
- European Theater: North Africa, Italy, D-Day (June 6, 1944), Battle of the Bulge, Yalta Conference (Feb 1945), V-E Day (May 1945)
- Pacific Theater: fall of the Philippines, Bataan Death March, Battle of Midway (June 1942), island-hopping, Iwo Jima, Okinawa, Manhattan Project (Oppenheimer, Los Alamos), Hiroshima and Nagasaki (Aug 1945), V-J Day
- Wartime diplomacy: Casablanca, Tehran, Yalta, Potsdam; disputes over Eastern Europe and Poland; planning for United Nations; Bretton Woods (1944, IMF/World Bank)
- Home front economy: War Production Board, Office of Price Administration, rationing, massive federal spending ends the Depression, full employment, wage controls, savings
- Women and the workforce: "Rosie the Riveter," WAVES/WACs, postwar expectation to leave paid work
- African Americans: Executive Order 8802 (1941, FEPC) under A. Philip Randolph's March on Washington threat, Double V campaign, Detroit race riot (1943), Tuskegee Airmen
- Mexican Americans: Bracero Program (1942–64), Zoot Suit Riots (Los Angeles 1943)
- Japanese Americans: Executive Order 9066 (1942), internment camps (Manzanar, Tule Lake), *Korematsu v. United States* (1944), 442nd Regimental Combat Team
- Jewish refugees and the Holocaust: restricted admissions, St. Louis (1939), WRB (1944), liberation of camps
- End of war planning: Nuremberg Trials, Tokyo Trials, GI Bill (1944)

## Unit 8 · 1945–1980 — Key concepts

### Origins of the Cold War and early containment

- Post-1945 geopolitics: Soviet dominance in Eastern Europe, atomic monopoly briefly, decolonization beginning
- Ideological framing: George Kennan's "Long Telegram" (1946) and "X Article," containment; Churchill's "Iron Curtain" speech (1946)
- Truman Doctrine (1947, Greece/Turkey), Marshall Plan (1948, European Recovery Program), Berlin Blockade and Airlift (1948–49)
- Institutions: National Security Act (1947, CIA/NSC/DoD), NATO (1949), later SEATO and CENTO; UN created (1945)
- "Loss of China" (Mao 1949), Soviet A-bomb (1949); NSC-68 (1950) and militarization of containment
- Korean War (1950–53): UN intervention, Inchon, Chinese entry, 38th parallel stalemate, MacArthur relieved, armistice

### Second Red Scare / McCarthyism

- HUAC and Hollywood Ten (1947), loyalty oaths, Truman's Loyalty Program
- Alger Hiss case (Chambers, perjury conviction 1950), Rosenbergs (executed 1953)
- Joseph McCarthy's Wheeling speech (1950), anti-Communist hearings, Army-McCarthy hearings and censure (1954)
- McCarran Internal Security Act (1950)

### Postwar affluence and consent politics

- GI Bill, suburbanization (Levittown), interstate highways (1956), automobile culture, baby boom
- Consumer economy, television, rock and roll, Kinsey reports
- Eisenhower's "Modern Republicanism": expansion of Social Security and unemployment insurance, Interstate Highway Act (1956), Department of Health/Education/Welfare (1953)
- "Organization Man" (Whyte), *The Affluent Society* (Galbraith 1958), early countercurrents: Beats (Ginsberg, Kerouac), *Catcher in the Rye*, Betty Friedan later
- Warning of the military-industrial complex (Eisenhower 1961)

### Cold War in the Eisenhower and Kennedy years

- New Look and massive retaliation (Dulles), brinkmanship, CIA covert operations (Iran 1953 / Mossadegh, Guatemala 1954 / Árbenz), Suez Crisis (1956), Eisenhower Doctrine (1957), Sputnik (1957) and NDEA/space race, U-2 incident (1960)
- Kennedy's New Frontier: Peace Corps, Alliance for Progress, flexible response; Bay of Pigs fiasco (1961), Berlin Wall (1961), Cuban Missile Crisis (Oct 1962), Limited Test Ban Treaty (1963)
- JFK assassination (Nov 1963)

### Civil rights movement

- *Brown v. Board of Education* (1954, Thurgood Marshall, NAACP) overturning *Plessy*; Southern Manifesto and "massive resistance"
- Emmett Till (1955), Montgomery Bus Boycott (1955–56, Rosa Parks, Martin Luther King Jr., SCLC 1957)
- Little Rock Nine (1957) and federalized National Guard; Civil Rights Act of 1957
- Sit-ins: Greensboro (Feb 1960); SNCC founded (1960)
- Freedom Rides (1961, CORE), James Meredith and University of Mississippi (1962)
- Birmingham campaign (1963, Bull Connor, children's crusade), "Letter from Birmingham Jail," George Wallace's "segregation forever"
- March on Washington (Aug 1963, "I Have a Dream")
- Mississippi Freedom Summer (1964), Freedom Democratic Party, murder of Chaney/Goodman/Schwerner; Civil Rights Act of 1964 (Title II public accommodations, Title VI federal funds, Title VII employment / EEOC)
- Selma-to-Montgomery march and Voting Rights Act of 1965; Watts riot (Aug 1965)
- Black Power turn: Malcolm X and the Nation of Islam (assassinated 1965), Stokely Carmichael and SNCC, Black Panthers (Oakland 1966, Newton/Seale), Kerner Commission (1968), Chicago '68 convention, MLK assassination (April 1968) and urban uprisings
- *Loving v. Virginia* (1967), Fair Housing Act (1968)

### Great Society and the liberal apogee

- Lyndon Johnson: "War on Poverty" (Economic Opportunity Act 1964, VISTA, Job Corps, Head Start, Community Action)
- Medicare and Medicaid (1965), Elementary and Secondary Education Act (1965), Higher Education Act, Department of Housing and Urban Development (1965), National Endowments for the Arts and Humanities (1965), PBS/NPR
- Immigration and Nationality Act of 1965 (ending national-origin quotas)
- Warren Court: *Brown* (1954), *Gideon* (1963), *Miranda* (1966), *Griswold v. Connecticut* (1965), *Engel v. Vitale* (1962)
- Rights-based liberalism and the "rights revolution"

### Other social movements

- Second-wave feminism: Betty Friedan's *Feminine Mystique* (1963), Equal Pay Act (1963), National Organization for Women (1966), Title IX (1972), *Roe v. Wade* (1973), ERA passed by Congress (1972) and stalled ratification by 1982 (Phyllis Schlafly's STOP ERA)
- Chicano movement: Cesar Chavez and Dolores Huerta, United Farm Workers, Delano grape boycott (1965), Brown Berets, Chicano Moratorium (1970)
- American Indian Movement (AIM): Alcatraz occupation (1969–71), Trail of Broken Treaties (1972), Wounded Knee II (1973); Indian Self-Determination and Education Assistance Act (1975)
- Gay rights: Stonewall uprising (1969), Gay Liberation Front, gay pride movement
- Asian American movement, Puerto Rican Young Lords, disability rights (Rehabilitation Act 1973)
- Environmentalism: Rachel Carson's *Silent Spring* (1962), Earth Day (1970), Environmental Protection Agency (1970, under Nixon), Clean Air Act (1970), Clean Water Act (1972), Endangered Species Act (1973)
- Counterculture: hippies, communes, drugs, sexual revolution, Woodstock (1969), Altamont

### Vietnam War

- Origins: French colonial rule, First Indochina War, Geneva Accords (1954), Ngo Dinh Diem, domino theory
- Escalation: Tonkin Gulf Resolution (1964), Operation Rolling Thunder (1965), ground troop commitment under Johnson
- Turning points: Tet Offensive (Jan 1968), My Lai Massacre (1968, revealed 1969), LBJ declines to run (March 1968)
- Nixon: Vietnamization, Cambodia bombing and incursion (1970), Kent State and Jackson State (1970), Pentagon Papers (1971), Christmas bombing, Paris Peace Accords (Jan 1973), War Powers Act (Nov 1973), fall of Saigon (April 1975)
- Antiwar movement: SDS and Port Huron Statement (1962), teach-ins, draft resistance, Chicago 1968, Moratoriums (1969), Kent State reaction; hawks vs. doves; veterans against the war (Kerry)

### Nixon, the 1970s crises, and Carter

- Nixon's foreign policy: détente with the USSR, SALT I (1972), opening to China (1972), Kissinger
- Domestic Nixon: New Federalism, Southern Strategy, law-and-order, EPA/Clean Air/OSHA, wage and price controls (1971), dollar off gold (1971)
- Watergate (1972–74): break-in, Saturday Night Massacre (1973), *U.S. v. Nixon* (1974), resignation (Aug 1974), Ford's pardon
- Economic troubles: 1970s stagflation, Bretton Woods collapse, OPEC oil embargo (1973) and long gas lines, second oil shock (1979), deindustrialization/Rust Belt, affirmative action debates (*Regents of the University of California v. Bakke* 1978)
- Carter presidency: human-rights diplomacy, Panama Canal Treaties (1978), Camp David Accords (1978), SALT II, Iranian Revolution and Hostage Crisis (1979–81), Soviet invasion of Afghanistan (1979), Carter Doctrine, "malaise" speech (1979), Three Mile Island (1979), grain embargo and Olympic boycott (1980)
- Rise of the New Right: evangelicals (Moral Majority 1979), anti-tax revolt (California Proposition 13, 1978), deindustrialization backlash

## Unit 9 · 1980–present — Key concepts

### Reagan Revolution and conservative resurgence

- 1980 election: Reagan defeats Carter; Moral Majority (Jerry Falwell), Phyllis Schlafly, religious right mobilization
- Reaganomics / supply-side: Economic Recovery Tax Act (1981), Kemp-Roth cuts; deregulation; air traffic controllers / PATCO strike broken (1981); federal deficits balloon; Volcker's Fed tames inflation; 1981–82 recession, recovery by 1984
- Reagan's culture war and welfare critique: "welfare queen" narrative, War on Drugs, Anti-Drug Abuse Act (1986), crack/cocaine disparity; AIDS epidemic and the government's slow response (ACT UP)
- Supreme Court turn: O'Connor, Scalia, Rehnquist chief (1986); *Webster v. Reproductive Health Services* (1989); *Casey v. Planned Parenthood* (1992)

### End of the Cold War

- Reagan's arms buildup and Strategic Defense Initiative (1983, "Star Wars"); KAL 007 (1983); U.S. Lebanon deployment and Beirut bombing (1983); invasion of Grenada (1983)
- Iran-Contra affair (1986–87): arms-for-hostages to Iran, illegal diversion of funds to Nicaraguan Contras
- Reagan-Gorbachev summits: Reykjavik (1986), "tear down this wall" (1987), INF Treaty (1987)
- 1989 revolutions: Solidarity in Poland, fall of the Berlin Wall (Nov 1989); Tiananmen Square (June 1989)
- George H.W. Bush: Panama intervention (1989), German reunification (1990), Persian Gulf War against Iraq (1990–91, Desert Storm, coalition), dissolution of the Soviet Union (Dec 1991), START I (1991); "new world order"
- Americans with Disabilities Act (1990), Clean Air Act Amendments (1990), Clarence Thomas / Anita Hill hearings (1991), Rodney King beating and Los Angeles riots (1992)

### 1990s: Clinton, globalization, and culture wars

- 1992 election: Clinton defeats Bush and Ross Perot; generational turnover
- "Don't Ask, Don't Tell" (1993); Family and Medical Leave Act (1993); Brady Bill (1993); failed health-care reform (1993–94)
- NAFTA ratified (1993), WTO created (1995), globalization accelerates (offshoring, "China shock"); Battle of Seattle (1999)
- 1994 Republican Revolution and Contract with America (Newt Gingrich); government shutdowns (1995–96)
- Welfare reform / PRWORA (1996), Defense of Marriage Act (1996), Telecommunications Act (1996)
- Dot-com boom and consumer internet (browser, ecommerce, 1995–2000)
- Oklahoma City bombing (1995), Waco (1993), rise of militia/right-wing paramilitary
- Balkans: Bosnia (Dayton 1995), Kosovo (1999), Rwanda genocide (1994) and US nonintervention
- Lewinsky scandal and Clinton's impeachment and acquittal (1998–99)
- Immigration debates: California Proposition 187 (1994), IIRIRA (1996), border buildup
- Columbine (1999), fears of school violence

### 2000s: 9/11, War on Terror, and the financial crisis

- 2000 election and *Bush v. Gore*
- George W. Bush: tax cuts (2001, 2003), No Child Left Behind (2001)
- September 11, 2001 attacks (al-Qaeda, Osama bin Laden); USA PATRIOT Act (2001), Department of Homeland Security (2002), Guantánamo Bay detention (2002), enhanced interrogation debate, Afghanistan War (2001–), Iraq War (2003– invasion, WMD claims, insurgency, Abu Ghraib, surge 2007)
- Hurricane Katrina (2005) and federal response failure
- 2008 financial crisis: subprime mortgages, Lehman collapse (Sept 2008), TARP bank bailout, auto bailout; Great Recession

### Obama presidency

- 2008 election: Barack Obama as first Black president; Tea Party backlash (2009)
- American Recovery and Reinvestment Act (2009), auto industry rescue; Affordable Care Act / "Obamacare" (2010) and *NFIB v. Sebelius* (2012); Dodd-Frank financial reform (2010)
- Foreign policy: withdrawal from Iraq (2011), Osama bin Laden killed (2011), Arab Spring, Libya (2011), Syria civil war, Iran nuclear deal (JCPOA 2015), Paris Climate Agreement (2015), Cuba reopening (2015); drone warfare
- Social change: *Obergefell v. Hodges* (2015, same-sex marriage), Black Lives Matter (founded 2013 after Trayvon Martin; Michael Brown/Ferguson 2014; Eric Garner), DACA (2012); repeal of Don't Ask Don't Tell (2011)

### 2016–present: Polarization and crises

- 2016 election: Trump defeats Clinton; populist right, "Make America Great Again"
- Trump administration: tax cuts and Jobs Act (2017), withdrawal from TPP, Paris, Iran deal; border wall, family separation, travel ban; conservative Supreme Court appointments (Gorsuch, Kavanaugh, Barrett), shifting Court rightward
- First impeachment (2019–20, Ukraine)
- COVID-19 pandemic (2020–), CARES Act, remote work and schools, vaccine rollout, mass death
- 2020 election, January 6, 2021 Capitol attack, second impeachment
- Biden administration: Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (2021), Inflation Reduction Act (2022), CHIPS and Science Act (2022); withdrawal from Afghanistan (2021)
- Supreme Court turn: *Dobbs v. Jackson* (2022) overturning *Roe v. Wade*; affirmative action struck down (*SFFA v. Harvard*, 2023)
- Ongoing themes: climate change politics, AI and platform regulation, Russia's invasion of Ukraine (2022), Israel-Hamas war (2023), immigration and border politics, racial reckoning after George Floyd (2020)

- **Document-Based Question (DBQ)** — essay using 7 provided documents.
- **Long Essay Question (LEQ)** — thesis-driven essay without documents.
