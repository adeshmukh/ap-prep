Multiple choice

"All the power and sway is got into the hands of the rich, who by extortious advantages, having the common people in their debt, have always curbed and oppressed them in all manner of ways… We appeal to the country itself what and of what nature their oppressions have been, or by what cabal and mystery the designs of many of those whom we call great men have been transacted and carried on."

— Adapted from Nathaniel Bacon, "Declaration of the People of Virginia," 1676

Bacon's grievances in the excerpt most directly reflect which tension in late seventeenth-century Virginia society?

  1. A growing rift between enslaved Africans and free Black landowners over access to tidewater plantations.
  2. Resentment of former indentured servants and small frontier farmers against a tidewater planter elite that monopolized land and political office.
  3. Conflict between Anglican clergy and dissenting Puritan congregations over established church taxes.
  4. Disputes between royal customs officers enforcing the Navigation Acts and colonial smugglers evading duties.

Correct: the answer about former indentured servants and frontier farmers resenting the tidewater elite.

Bacon's Rebellion drew its support from freed indentured servants pushed onto the Indian frontier by a headright system that concentrated the best tobacco land in the hands of Governor Berkeley's inner circle. The option about Africans versus Black landowners is anachronistic for 1676 and misreads the coalition (Bacon's force included both white servants and enslaved and indentured Africans). The Anglican-versus-Puritan option describes New England, not Virginia. The Navigation Acts option is the wrong axis of conflict — Berkeley, not Bacon, was the royal enforcer; the rebellion was not primarily about customs duties.

Historians most often cite Bacon's Rebellion as a turning point because planters responded by

  1. dismantling the headright system and redistributing tidewater plantations to landless freedmen.
  2. accelerating the shift from European indentured servitude to lifelong, racially defined chattel slavery as their main labor source.
  3. embracing Puritan congregational government as a model for Virginia's county courts.
  4. agreeing to strict royal enforcement of the Navigation Acts to protect Virginia's tobacco monopoly.

Correct: the answer about accelerating the shift to racial chattel slavery.

After 1676 Chesapeake planters increasingly preferred enslaved Africans — whose status was lifelong and heritable — over indentured servants who became armed, land-hungry freedmen. The slave codes of 1682 and 1705 hardened this racial line. The headright-redistribution option is the opposite of what happened: elites kept their land. Puritan congregationalism never spread to Virginia's Anglican establishment. The Navigation Acts option confuses causes — the rebellion was not resolved through tighter imperial trade enforcement.

Which earlier development most directly set the stage for the grievances Bacon expresses in the excerpt?

  1. The headright system, which granted 50 acres per imported servant and concentrated land in the hands of wealthy planters who could pay passage for many laborers.
  2. The Mayflower Compact, which established self-government based on the consent of adult male settlers.
  3. The Half-Way Covenant, which loosened church-membership requirements for the grandchildren of original settlers.
  4. The Dominion of New England, which consolidated northern colonies under a single royal governor.

Correct: the headright-system answer.

The headright system rewarded whoever paid a servant's Atlantic passage with 50 acres — so rich planters who imported dozens of servants accumulated vast estates, while servants who survived their indenture found the best land already claimed. That structural inequality drove former servants onto the contested frontier that sparked Bacon's war. The Mayflower Compact and Half-Way Covenant are New England developments unrelated to Virginia land tenure. The Dominion of New England came after Bacon's Rebellion (1686) and applied only to the northern colonies.

"We must consider that we shall be as a city upon a hill. The eyes of all people are upon us; so that if we shall deal falsely with our God in this work we have undertaken… we shall be made a story and a by-word through the world."

— Adapted from John Winthrop, "A Model of Christian Charity," delivered aboard the Arbella, 1630

Winthrop's sermon most clearly expresses the Puritans' understanding that their Massachusetts Bay settlement was

  1. a proprietary colony chartered to a single noble family seeking quitrents and personal profit.
  2. a covenant community whose moral conduct would serve as a religious example for Reformed Christianity in Europe.
  3. a commercial enterprise modeled on the Virginia Company, focused on exporting a cash crop to English markets.
  4. a refuge of broad religious toleration in which all Protestant denominations would worship freely.

Correct: the covenant-community / religious-example answer.

Winthrop frames the colony as bound by a covenant with God, watched by "the eyes of all people" — a model of reformed society rather than a private estate or a cash-crop venture. The proprietary answer describes Maryland or Pennsylvania, not Massachusetts Bay. The Virginia Company answer confuses Winthrop's errand with the Chesapeake tobacco economy. The broad-toleration answer describes Rhode Island or Pennsylvania; Massachusetts Bay enforced religious conformity and expelled dissenters like Roger Williams and Anne Hutchinson.

Which later seventeenth-century development most directly challenged the vision of a unified, godly community that Winthrop describes?

  1. The Mayflower Compact's assertion of self-government at Plymouth in 1620.
  2. The Half-Way Covenant of 1662, which extended partial church membership to unconverted descendants and signaled declining rates of conversion.
  3. The Great Migration of Puritan families to New England in the 1630s.
  4. The arrival of Quaker missionaries in Pennsylvania in the 1680s.

Correct: the Half-Way Covenant answer.

By 1662 ministers worried that the second and third generations were not reporting conversion experiences, so the Half-Way Covenant allowed their children to be baptized without full communion — an implicit admission that the "city upon a hill" was losing its spiritual intensity. The Mayflower Compact predates Winthrop's sermon and reinforced, not challenged, covenant ideals. The Great Migration supplied the colony Winthrop described. Quaker missionaries in Pennsylvania did not threaten Massachusetts Bay's internal religious cohesion.

Document 1. "Shipped aboard the Mary Anne, bound for Bristol, 340 barrels of Carolina rice on account of Mr. T. Drayton, planter of Goose Creek, being the produce of his negroes working by task on the inland swamps…"

— Adapted from a Charles Town merchant's ledger, 1738

Document 2. "Whereas the late insurrection of the negroes at Stono hath manifested the great danger of permitting slaves to assemble, to learn to write, or to go abroad without tickets, Be it therefore enacted…"

— Adapted from the South Carolina Negro Act, 1740

Taken together, the two documents best support which conclusion about low-country South Carolina by the 1730s?

  1. Carolina planters were moving away from enslaved labor toward European indentured servants because of the Stono Rebellion.
  2. An export economy built on enslaved Africans' rice cultivation produced both staggering planter wealth and harsh legal codes designed to control a Black majority.
  3. The Navigation Acts had destroyed Carolina's Atlantic trade and forced rice planters to sell primarily in French Caribbean markets.
  4. Quaker settlers dominated low-country politics and pushed for gradual emancipation after Stono.

Correct: the answer pairing rice wealth with harsh legal control of a Black majority.

Document 1 shows rice profits flowing through Charles Town to Bristol on the backs of task-laboring enslaved workers; Document 2 shows the colony tightening its slave code immediately after Stono (1739). Together they describe the plantation-complex / slave-society pattern of the Carolina low country — not a retreat to indentured labor (the opposite happened), not a collapse of Atlantic trade (rice exports boomed within the British imperial system), and not Quaker political dominance (Quakers were concentrated in Pennsylvania, not South Carolina).

The "task" labor arrangement referenced in the merchant's ledger differed from the Chesapeake gang-labor system in that it

  1. assigned each enslaved worker a daily quota, giving them limited control of their remaining time once the task was complete, and was tied to the specialized skills demanded by tidal rice cultivation.
  2. freed enslaved workers from field labor and reassigned them to urban artisanal trades in Charles Town.
  3. required enslaved workers to labor in coordinated groups under a white overseer from sunrise to sunset, as on Virginia tobacco plantations.
  4. paid enslaved workers a monthly wage in colonial currency once they had completed their contracted "task" of seven years.

Correct: the daily-quota / rice-cultivation answer.

Rice required specialized knowledge — much of it brought from West Africa — and the task system let planters exploit that expertise by setting a daily quota per worker. The urban-artisan answer misdescribes the plantation setting. The gang-labor description is exactly the Chesapeake contrast the question asks about, not the Carolina task system. The wage / seven-year-contract answer confuses chattel slavery with indentured servitude, which by the 1730s had largely been displaced in the Carolina low country.

Before 1754, the practice often called "salutary neglect" is best described as

  1. a formal parliamentary policy that exempted the thirteen colonies from the Navigation Acts in exchange for quitrents paid to the Crown.
  2. an informal pattern in which British ministries left most enforcement of trade regulations to colonial assemblies and customs officers, allowing colonial self-government and smuggling networks to flourish within a mercantilist framework.
  3. a Puritan theological movement urging colonists to withdraw from Atlantic commerce to preserve their spiritual purity.
  4. a treaty between Britain and France guaranteeing neutral trade zones in the Ohio River valley during the early eighteenth century.

Correct: the informal-enforcement answer.

Salutary neglect was not written law but an administrative habit under Walpole and his successors: the Navigation Acts remained on the books, yet lax enforcement let colonial assemblies grow in power and merchants to trade (and smuggle) relatively freely. The "formal exemption" answer inverts reality — mercantilism stayed officially in force. The Puritan-withdrawal answer invents a theology that did not exist. The Anglo-French treaty answer is fabricated; there was no such neutral-trade treaty in the Ohio valley.

Free response

SAQ 1. Using your knowledge of the period 1607–1754, answer (a), (b), and (c).

(a) Briefly describe ONE specific difference between the economies of the Chesapeake colonies and the New England colonies in the seventeenth century.
(b) Briefly explain ONE specific cause of that difference.
(c) Briefly explain ONE specific way this regional difference shaped the development of colonial society by 1750.

Rubric

  • (a) 1 point for a specific, accurate contrast (e.g., Chesapeake export staple vs. New England mixed economy; plantation labor vs. family farms / shipping).
  • (b) 1 point for a specific cause (soil and climate; settler motives — profit vs. Puritan community; headright system; West Indies slave-trade access).
  • (c) 1 point for a specific later consequence that clearly follows from (a) — e.g., entrenched chattel slavery and a planter elite in the South; urban merchant class, denser towns, and literate Puritan culture in New England.

Model answer

(a) The seventeenth-century Chesapeake economy was organized around a single export staple — tobacco grown on large plantations — while New England developed a diversified economy of small family farms, fishing, shipbuilding, and merchant shipping centered on Boston.

(b) This difference was driven largely by settler motives and geography. Virginia and Maryland attracted profit-seeking planters who used the headright system to acquire bottomland suited to tobacco; New England was settled by Puritan families seeking to build covenant communities on rockier, less staple-friendly land, which encouraged diversified household production and Atlantic trade.

(c) By 1750 the Chesapeake had become a rigidly stratified slave society in which a small planter elite dominated politics while enslaved Africans performed most field labor, whereas New England had developed a denser network of port towns, a sizable merchant class, and comparatively widespread literacy rooted in Puritan congregational life — shaping very different political cultures going into the imperial crisis.

Short DBQ. Using the three excerpts below and your knowledge of the period, write a focused response (roughly a thesis plus two to three paragraphs) to the following prompt:

Evaluate the extent to which cultural and intellectual movements in the British North American colonies between 1700 and 1754 challenged traditional sources of authority.

Document A. "The God that holds you over the pit of hell, much as one holds a spider, or some loathsome insect, over the fire, abhors you… there is nothing between you and hell but the air."

— Adapted from Jonathan Edwards, "Sinners in the Hands of an Angry God," 1741

Document B. "In my travels through the colonies I have seen thousands weep under preaching, irrespective of parish, sect, or learned minister, and sober congregations turn itinerant, judging their settled pastors by the fruits of the new birth rather than by ordination."

— Adapted from a letter attributed to George Whitefield, 1740

Document C. "The jury, finding that Mr. Zenger printed what was true, though it reflected on the Governor, returned their verdict of Not Guilty, to the great applause of the people assembled in the court."

— Adapted from an account of the trial of John Peter Zenger, New York, 1735

Rubric

  • Thesis (1 pt): historically defensible claim that takes a position on the extent of challenge to authority (e.g., "To a significant extent, religious revival and Enlightenment-influenced public debate undermined deference to established clergy and colonial officials, though traditional hierarchies in politics and slavery remained intact.").
  • Contextualization (1 pt): situate the period (e.g., maturing mercantilist empire, salutary neglect, established churches, growing literacy, print culture).
  • Document use (up to 3 pts): use all three excerpts to support the argument, describing content accurately — e.g., Edwards on emotional revivalism, Whitefield on itinerancy and lay judgment of clergy, Zenger on press criticism of a royal governor.
  • Outside evidence (1 pt): at least one specific fact beyond the documents — e.g., the split between "Old Lights" and "New Lights," Enlightenment influence (Locke, Franklin), the spread of newspapers, growing colonial assemblies under salutary neglect.
  • Complexity / sourcing (1 pt): nuance — e.g., revivalism challenged clerical authority but often reinforced patriarchal and racial hierarchies; Zenger's verdict did not change law but shifted public expectation; sourcing by purpose/audience of at least one document.

Model answer (abbreviated)

Thesis: Between 1700 and 1754 the First Great Awakening and an expanding colonial print culture challenged religious and political authority in significant ways — lay converts judged their ministers, and jurors defied a royal governor — but these movements did not fundamentally dislodge the established planter elite, imperial officials, or the institution of slavery, so the challenge was real yet partial.

Context and documents: Within a British imperial system of salutary neglect and established churches, revivalists like Edwards (Doc A) used emotional, fear-driven preaching to push ordinary colonists to seek a personal "new birth," cutting around the scholarly authority of settled Congregational ministers. Whitefield's itinerancy (Doc B) taught audiences from New England to Georgia to evaluate clergy by the "fruits" of conversion rather than by ordination — producing the Old Light / New Light split that fractured established denominations. At the same time, the Zenger trial (Doc C) showed a New York jury protecting printed criticism of a royal governor, foreshadowing a press culture increasingly willing to police officials.

Complexity: Yet these challenges had limits. Slave codes hardened after Stono (1739); tidewater and low-country elites retained control of assemblies; and Whitefield himself defended slavery even as he preached to enslaved audiences. Enlightenment ideas (Locke, Franklin's Junto, the expanding newspaper network) reinforced a culture of public argument, but deference to imperial authority would not truly break until the crisis after 1763. The period therefore produced meaningful but uneven challenges to traditional authority — laying intellectual groundwork for the revolutionary era rather than overturning colonial hierarchies in its own right.
