Unit 1 · 1491–1607

Pre-Columbian Societies, Contact, and the Columbian Exchange

Eight stimulus-driven MCQs and two free-response prompts covering Indigenous diversity in the Americas, European motives for exploration, biological and cultural exchanges, and the emergence of Spanish colonial labor systems.

8 multiple choice 2 free response AP-level

Multiple choice

"The reason the Christians have killed and destroyed such an infinite number of souls is that they have been moved by their desire for gold and their wish to enrich themselves in a very short time. The Indians, a people so humble, so patient, and so easily subjected, they have treated not as beasts — for beasts they would have treated better — but as the excrement in a public square."

— Adapted from Bartolomé de Las Casas, A Short Account of the Destruction of the Indies, 1552

"The Spanish have a perfect right to rule these barbarians of the New World… who in prudence, skill, virtues, and humanity are as inferior to the Spanish as children to adults, or women to men… In fact, there is as great a difference between them as there is between savagery and forbearance, between violence and moderation."

— Adapted from Juan Ginés de Sepúlveda, Democrates Alter, c. 1547

The disagreement between Las Casas and Sepúlveda shown in the excerpts most directly concerned which of the following questions?

  1. Whether the Spanish Crown should allow Protestant missionaries into its American colonies.
  2. Whether Indigenous peoples of the Americas possessed natural rights that limited Spanish authority over them.
  3. Whether African slavery should replace Indigenous labor in the Spanish colonies.
  4. Whether Spain should withdraw entirely from the New World and cede it to Portugal.

Correct: Whether Indigenous peoples possessed natural rights that limited Spanish authority.

The Valladolid debate (1550–1551) centered on whether Native Americans were rational beings entitled to human dignity (Las Casas) or "natural slaves" in an Aristotelian sense whose conquest was justified (Sepúlveda). The Protestant-missionaries option is anachronistic — Spain was militantly Catholic and excluded Protestants from its empire entirely. The African-slavery option conflates a related but distinct debate; Las Casas briefly endorsed African labor as a substitute before renouncing the idea, but that was not the question at Valladolid. The Spain-cedes-to-Portugal option confuses the Treaty of Tordesillas with the rights debate.

Sepúlveda's argument in the second excerpt most directly drew on which intellectual tradition?

  1. Enlightenment theories of natural equality articulated by John Locke.
  2. Aristotelian and medieval scholastic arguments about "natural hierarchy" among peoples.
  3. Protestant Reformation theology emphasizing the priesthood of all believers.
  4. Indigenous Mesoamerican cosmologies recorded by Spanish friars.

Correct: Aristotelian and scholastic arguments about natural hierarchy.

Sepúlveda explicitly drew on Aristotle's Politics and its doctrine of the "natural slave," filtered through Catholic scholasticism. Lockean natural equality postdates the debate by a century and a half and is the opposite of Sepúlveda's claim. Protestant theology was rejected by the Spanish Crown and played no role in Sepúlveda's case. Indigenous Mesoamerican cosmologies were the object of Spanish study, not the foundation of Spanish legal philosophy.

The labor system that Las Casas denounced in the first excerpt was most directly associated with which institution?

  1. The encomienda, which granted Spanish settlers the labor and tribute of Indigenous communities in exchange for nominal protection and Christianization.
  2. The Atlantic chattel slave system organized around the Middle Passage.
  3. The headright system used by the Virginia Company to recruit English laborers.
  4. The French coureurs de bois fur-trading partnerships with Algonquian peoples.

Correct: The encomienda system.

Las Casas was himself a former encomendero, and his indictment of Spanish greed and cruelty targeted the encomienda specifically. The Atlantic chattel slave option is wrong because in 1552 the mass African slave trade to the Americas was still nascent and is not what Las Casas is describing here (he writes of "Indians"). The headright system is English and seventeenth-century. The coureurs de bois are seventeenth-century French traders — wrong empire, wrong period, and based on exchange rather than coerced tribute.

Chart description (paraphrased): A bar chart titled "Estimated population of central Mexico, 1518–1605" shows the Indigenous population falling from roughly 25 million in 1518 to about 16.8 million in 1532, 6.3 million in 1548, 2.7 million in 1568, and approximately 1.0 million in 1605. A footnote credits the figures to the Berkeley school demographers Sherburne Cook and Woodrow Borah.

— Adapted from Cook & Borah, The Population of Central Mexico in the Sixteenth Century, 1960

The demographic trend shown in the chart is most directly explained by which of the following?

  1. The Mexica practice of large-scale human sacrifice in the fifteenth century.
  2. The introduction of Old World pathogens, especially smallpox, to populations with no prior immunity.
  3. The voluntary migration of Indigenous peoples out of central Mexico toward the Andes.
  4. A long-term climatic cooling that reduced maize yields across Mesoamerica.

Correct: The introduction of Old World pathogens, especially smallpox.

Epidemiological devastation — smallpox, measles, typhus, and influenza — is the overwhelming driver of the collapse. Warfare, overwork under the encomienda, and famine contributed, but none remotely approach the demographic scale of virgin-soil epidemics. Mexica sacrifice did not remove millions of people over generations and is a stereotyped distractor, not a demographic explanation. There was no mass migration to the Andes. Climatic cooling during the Little Ice Age affected Europe more than central Mexico and cannot account for a 96% drop.

Which consequence in the Atlantic world is most directly linked to the demographic collapse shown in the chart?

  1. The gradual abolition of the encomienda and the return of land to Indigenous cabildos.
  2. Spanish and Portuguese turn toward importing enslaved Africans to fill labor shortages in mines and plantations.
  3. An English decision to avoid New World colonization until the eighteenth century.
  4. The collapse of the transatlantic silver trade between Potosí and Seville.

Correct: The turn toward importing enslaved Africans.

As the Indigenous labor force collapsed, the Spanish and Portuguese increasingly relied on enslaved Africans — a shift the College Board explicitly frames as a defining transition of Unit 1. The encomienda was reformed by the New Laws (1542) but continued de facto; land was not restored to Indigenous councils in any systematic way. The English did colonize in the early seventeenth century (Jamestown 1607). The silver trade actually boomed in this period, fueled in part by this new labor regime; it did not collapse.

Which Old World crop or animal, introduced through the Columbian Exchange, most transformed the lifeways of Indigenous peoples on the Great Plains by the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries?

  1. Wheat, which supplanted maize as the staple grain of Plains societies.
  2. The horse, which enabled mobile bison hunting and new forms of military and economic power.
  3. The pig, which underpinned a free-range meat economy among Pueblo farmers.
  4. Citrus fruit, which became a central trade good in the fur economy.

Correct: The horse.

Spanish horses — especially after the 1680 Pueblo Revolt dispersed herds northward — revolutionized Plains life, making peoples like the Lakota, Comanche, and Cheyenne mounted bison hunters and military powers. Wheat never displaced maize among Plains peoples. Pigs were a Spanish and English import but were never central to Pueblo agriculture, which remained maize-, bean-, and squash-based. Citrus was not significant in North American Indigenous exchange networks.

Which of the following best illustrates the diversity of pre-Columbian societies in what is now the United States around 1491?

  1. Most peoples north of the Rio Grande lived in small, nomadic bands with essentially identical subsistence strategies.
  2. Mississippian mound-builders sustained stratified urban centers like Cahokia through intensive maize agriculture, while Pacific Northwest peoples built hierarchical societies on salmon and cedar, and Pueblo peoples practiced irrigated desert farming.
  3. Nearly all Indigenous peoples north of Mexico had adopted a pastoral economy based on domesticated livestock.
  4. The Iroquois Confederacy dominated the entire continent politically until European contact.

Correct: The answer contrasting Mississippian, Northwest, and Pueblo societies.

By 1491 the Americas hosted enormous ecological and political diversity: Cahokia at its height housed tens of thousands; Northwest peoples (Chinook, Kwakwaka'wakw, Haida) built rank societies on abundant marine resources; Pueblo farmers in the Southwest practiced dry-farming and irrigation. The claim of uniform nomadic bands ignores Mississippian urbanism and Pueblo sedentism. A pastoral livestock economy is incorrect — the Americas had few domesticable large mammals, which is precisely why horses later transformed the Plains. The Iroquois Confederacy was influential in the Northeast but never controlled the continent.

Which combination best explains the surge of late-fifteenth-century Iberian overseas exploration?

  1. A Europe-wide religious consensus that forbade trade with the Islamic world and required new routes.
  2. The desire for direct access to Asian luxury goods, the spread of the caravel and improved navigation, and a Reconquista-era crusading zeal that tied monarchy to Catholicism.
  3. A Scandinavian monopoly over North Atlantic fishing that pushed Iberians southward.
  4. A demographic boom in Iberia that made overseas settlement the only outlet for surplus population.

Correct: The combination of Asian luxury demand, caravel/navigation advances, and Reconquista crusading zeal.

The conventional "God, gold, and glory" formula fits Iberian expansion: desire to bypass Venetian and Ottoman middlemen for spices and silk, the technological enabling of the caravel, astrolabe, and magnetic compass, and the militant Catholicism forged by the Reconquista, completed in 1492. A religious consensus forbidding trade with Muslims is incorrect — Mediterranean trade with Muslim states continued, and motives were economic/strategic rather than a blanket ban. Scandinavian fishing did not "push" Iberia anywhere; the North Atlantic cod fishery was a parallel development. Iberia's population was not booming — plague had reduced it — so demographic pressure is a weak explanation.

Free response

SAQ. Using your knowledge of the period 1491–1607, answer (a), (b), and (c).

(a) Identify ONE specific way in which pre-Columbian societies in the Americas differed from one another in their political or economic organization.

(b) Explain ONE specific cause of European exploration and colonization of the Americas in the period 1491–1607.

(c) Explain ONE specific effect of the Columbian Exchange on either Indigenous peoples in the Americas OR peoples in Afro-Eurasia in the period 1491–1607.

Rubric

  • (a) 1 point for a specific, accurate contrast between two identifiable pre-Columbian societies (e.g., Aztec urban/tributary empire vs. mobile Algonquian bands; Mississippian stratified mound cities vs. egalitarian Great Basin foragers; Pueblo irrigation farming vs. Northwest salmon-based rank society).
  • (b) 1 point for a specific cause with a clear explanation — not a bare phrase. Acceptable causes include the search for a western route to Asian spice and silk markets, the crusading zeal cultivated by the Iberian Reconquista, the development of the caravel and improved navigation, or competition for prestige among Atlantic monarchies.
  • (c) 1 point for a specific effect with explanation. Acceptable: virgin-soil epidemics (smallpox, measles) causing catastrophic Indigenous population loss; introduction of maize and potatoes to Europe/Africa driving population growth; the horse transforming Plains Indigenous societies; the silver flow from Potosí reshaping the global economy.

Model answer

(a) The Mexica (Aztec) Empire of central Mexico was a hierarchical, urban, tributary state ruled from Tenochtitlán, with a professional priesthood, specialized artisans, and a population in the hundreds of thousands supported by chinampa agriculture and tribute from conquered city-states. By contrast, most Algonquian-speaking peoples of the Eastern Woodlands lived in kin-based villages of a few hundred, combining maize-bean-squash horticulture with seasonal hunting and without the centralized bureaucracy that characterized the Mexica.

(b) One cause was the desire of European monarchs and merchants to obtain direct access to Asian luxury goods — spices, silk, and porcelain — without paying Venetian or Ottoman intermediaries. After the Ottoman consolidation of the eastern Mediterranean in the mid-fifteenth century, Portugal pushed down the African coast seeking a sea route to India, and Spain sponsored Columbus's 1492 voyage hoping to reach Asia westward; both efforts aimed at capturing the profits of the Asian luxury trade for their own crowns.

(c) The Columbian Exchange devastated Indigenous populations through the introduction of Old World pathogens. Smallpox, measles, influenza, and typhus spread to populations with no acquired immunity, killing an estimated 50–90% of Indigenous peoples across the Americas over the sixteenth century. In central Mexico the population fell from roughly 25 million in 1518 to about 1 million by 1605, shattering Indigenous political structures, opening land to Spanish settlement, and generating the labor shortages that drove the Spanish and Portuguese to import enslaved Africans.

Short DBQ. Using the three excerpts below and your knowledge of the period, write a thesis-driven response to the following prompt:

Evaluate the extent to which Spanish colonization of the Americas in the sixteenth century was shaped by religious motives versus economic motives.

Document 1. "I… took possession of the said island for their Highnesses… and to all I gave the name of San Salvador, in recognition of His Heavenly Majesty, Who marvelously hath given all this… These people are very unskilled in arms… with fifty men they can all be subjugated and made to do what is required of them."
— Adapted from Christopher Columbus, Journal of the First Voyage, 1492

Document 2. "By the authority of Almighty God… we donate, grant, and assign to you and your heirs… all islands and mainlands discovered and to be discovered, toward the west and south… with this proviso, that none of the islands and mainlands found… be in the actual possession of any Christian king or prince."
— Adapted from Pope Alexander VI, Bull Inter caetera, 1493

Document 3. "By 1580 the mines of Potosí were producing more than half of the silver entering the Atlantic economy. The mita labor draft required Andean villages to send a seventh of their adult men each year into the shafts; many never returned. The Crown's royal fifth on this silver underwrote Habsburg armies from Flanders to the Philippines."
— Adapted from a modern historian's survey of colonial Latin America, 2005

Rubric (abbreviated, DBQ-style)

  • Thesis (1): A historically defensible claim that takes a position on the relative weight of religious vs. economic motives (e.g., "Although religion provided rhetorical and legal cover, economic motives — especially silver and coerced labor — were the dominant driver").
  • Contextualization (1): Situate Spanish colonization within the Reconquista, the Treaty of Tordesillas, and/or the global search for an Asian trade route.
  • Document use (2): Use all three documents to support the argument; describe content accurately and connect it to the thesis.
  • Sourcing (1): For at least one document, explain how point of view, purpose, audience, or historical situation affects its meaning (e.g., Columbus writes to his royal patrons and has incentive to advertise both piety and easy conquest).
  • Evidence beyond the documents (1): Cite at least one specific fact not in the documents — e.g., the Valladolid debate, the encomienda, Cortés's fall of Tenochtitlán (1521), Cook & Borah's demographic estimates.
  • Complex understanding (1): Show that religion and economics were interlocked rather than opposed (e.g., papal authority legitimized economic conquest; requerimiento ritualized both).

Model answer (condensed)

Although Spanish colonization of the sixteenth-century Americas was framed in the language of Catholic mission, economic motives — above all the pursuit of precious metals and coerced labor — did more than religion to determine where the Spanish went, how they governed, and whom they exploited. Religion gave the enterprise its legal scaffolding and rhetorical gloss; silver and sugar made it pay.

Spanish expansion emerged from the Reconquista, completed in 1492 with the fall of Granada, which fused crusading zeal with royal consolidation. The Treaty of Tordesillas (1494) partitioned the non-Christian world between Spain and Portugal under papal authority. In this context, religious legitimation and economic ambition were inseparable from the outset. Document 2, Alexander VI's Inter caetera, exemplifies how papal grants "donated" whole hemispheres to the Crown — but note the purpose: a pope mediating between Catholic monarchs competing for commercial empire. The religious frame served an economic and geopolitical agenda.

Columbus in Document 1 moves in a single passage from naming an island "San Salvador" to estimating how easily its people could be "subjugated and made to do what is required of them." Writing to royal patrons who had just funded him, Columbus has every incentive to perform both piety and profitability; the document's point of view thus reveals how seamlessly religious language and labor extraction traveled together. The requerimiento, read aloud (often in Spanish) to Indigenous peoples before conquest, similarly wrapped violent dispossession in the language of Christian invitation.

By the late sixteenth century, as Document 3 shows, Potosí's silver — extracted through the mita labor draft imposed on Andean communities — had become the economic foundation of the Spanish Empire, funding Habsburg armies from the Netherlands to the Philippines. The encomienda in Mexico and the Andes reveals the same logic: Indigenous laborers were nominally entrusted to encomenderos for Christianization in exchange for tribute and forced labor, but in practice the system produced Las Casas's denunciations in A Short Account of the Destruction of the Indies (1552) and the Valladolid debate over whether Indigenous peoples were even fully human. The Crown's willingness to reform the system only at the margins (the New Laws of 1542) — and the parallel rise of the transatlantic African slave trade once Indigenous labor collapsed — shows that maintaining a coercive labor supply for mines and plantations trumped missionary concern when the two conflicted.

Religion, then, was not a cover story but a genuine motive — Spanish friars baptized millions and built a vast missionary infrastructure — yet the shape of colonization followed the silver and the labor. The religious and economic motives were intertwined, but when they pulled in different directions, as in the encomienda, it was the economy that usually won.
