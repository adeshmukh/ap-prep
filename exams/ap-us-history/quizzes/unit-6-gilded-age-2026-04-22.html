Multiple choice

"This, then, is held to be the duty of the man of wealth: to set an example of modest, unostentatious living, shunning display or extravagance; to provide moderately for the legitimate wants of those dependent upon him; and, after doing so, to consider all surplus revenues which come to him simply as trust funds, which he is called upon to administer … in the manner which, in his judgment, is best calculated to produce the most beneficial results for the community."

— Adapted from Andrew Carnegie, "Wealth" (later "The Gospel of Wealth"), 1889

Carnegie's argument in the passage most directly reflects which Gilded Age intellectual current?

  1. The Social Gospel movement's insistence that churches redistribute property to the poor.
  2. A blending of Social Darwinism with a moral duty of the rich to steward wealth for public benefit.
  3. Populist demands for government ownership of railroads and telegraphs.
  4. Marxist critiques of capitalist accumulation circulating among immigrant labor radicals.

Correct: A blending of Social Darwinism with a moral duty of the rich to steward wealth for public benefit.

Carnegie accepted William Graham Sumner's Social Darwinist view that concentrated wealth was the natural outcome of competition, but he added a paternalistic "trusteeship" duty — the rich should administer surplus wealth for the community (libraries, universities). The Social Gospel answer is close but focused on churches and structural poverty relief rather than private philanthropy by industrialists. The Populist answer pushes the opposite policy. The Marxist answer rejects private wealth altogether, which Carnegie defends.

Carnegie's own steel empire is most often cited as an example of which business practice?

  1. Vertical integration — controlling iron ore, coke, transport, and mills under one firm.
  2. Horizontal integration — buying out rival steel producers to form a single trust.
  3. Interlocking directorates among unrelated banks and insurance companies.
  4. A holding-company structure designed to evade the Sherman Antitrust Act.

Correct: Vertical integration — controlling iron ore, coke, transport, and mills under one firm.

Carnegie Steel was the archetype of vertical integration: owning the mines, the Henry Clay Frick coke works, Great Lakes shipping, and the rails into Homestead. Horizontal integration (buying up rivals) better describes Rockefeller's Standard Oil. Interlocking directorates belong to J.P. Morgan-style finance. The holding-company/Sherman Act dodge describes Standard Oil of New Jersey after 1889, not Carnegie Steel.

A labor leader writing in 1892 would most likely respond to Carnegie's passage by pointing to which event as evidence that Carnegie's "trusteeship" rhetoric was hollow?

  1. The Great Railroad Strike of 1877, which President Hayes broke with federal troops.
  2. The Homestead Strike, in which Carnegie Steel locked out workers and Pinkertons fought the Amalgamated union.
  3. The Pullman Strike, which Eugene Debs led against the sleeping-car company near Chicago.
  4. The Haymarket affair, in which a bomb thrown at Chicago police discredited the Knights of Labor.

Correct: The Homestead Strike, in which Carnegie Steel locked out workers and Pinkertons fought the Amalgamated union.

The 1892 Homestead Strike directly involved Carnegie's firm: Frick's lockout, the armed battle with Pinkerton agents on the Monongahela, and the subsequent destruction of the skilled-steelworkers' union are the natural rebuttal to Carnegie's self-image as benevolent trustee. The Pullman Strike (1894) and Great Railroad Strike (1877) involved different industries, and Haymarket (1886) centered on Chicago anarchists, not Carnegie's works.

"We meet in the midst of a nation brought to the verge of moral, political, and material ruin. Corruption dominates the ballot-box, the Legislatures, the Congress … The fruits of the toil of millions are boldly stolen to build up colossal fortunes for a few. … We demand the free and unlimited coinage of silver and gold at the present legal ratio of 16 to 1."

— Adapted from the Omaha Platform of the People's (Populist) Party, 1892

The economic grievance most directly animating the "16 to 1" demand was:

  1. Rising industrial wages that were eroding farmers' relative standard of living.
  2. Deflation under the gold standard, which raised the real burden of farm mortgages and crop debts.
  3. Tariff reductions that were allowing cheap European wheat to flood American markets.
  4. The federal income tax of 1894, which fell disproportionately on cotton and wheat producers.

Correct: Deflation under the gold standard, which raised the real burden of farm mortgages and crop debts.

Restricting the money supply to gold produced decades of falling prices; a farmer who borrowed at high post-war prices had to repay in dollars that bought more grain every year. Free silver would inflate the currency and ease debt. Industrial wages were not the Populists' main target; tariff politics cut the other way (Populists generally disliked the high McKinley Tariff but that was not the silver issue); and the 1894 income tax actually aligned with Populist demands before being struck down.

Which later development is the best evidence that Populism reshaped national politics even after the party itself collapsed?

  1. Theodore Roosevelt's 1901 ascension to the presidency after McKinley's assassination.
  2. Progressive-Era adoption of the direct election of senators, the income tax, and regulation of railroads.
  3. The Gold Standard Act of 1900, which formally put the U.S. on gold.
  4. William Jennings Bryan's 1896 loss to William McKinley in a realigning election.

Correct: Progressive-Era adoption of the direct election of senators, the income tax, and regulation of railroads.

Many Omaha Platform demands — the graduated income tax (16th Amendment), direct election of senators (17th Amendment), expanded railroad regulation — became federal law in the 1910s, demonstrating Populism's long reach. The Gold Standard Act was the defeat, not the legacy, of Populism. Roosevelt's accession is a contingency, not a policy continuity. Bryan's 1896 loss is the fall of the movement, not evidence of its influence.

"Our cities fill up with the servile peasantry of Southern Europe — illiterate, priest-ridden, and unused to free institutions — while on the Pacific the Chinaman, working for wages no white man could accept, undercuts the honest American mechanic. A republic cannot long survive such an infusion unless Congress acts to restrict it."

— Adapted from a nativist newspaper editorial, 1885

Which federal action most directly reflected the logic of the editorial?

  1. The Dawes Severalty Act (1887), dividing tribal lands into individual allotments.
  2. The Chinese Exclusion Act (1882), which barred the entry of Chinese laborers.
  3. The Emergency Quota Act (1921), setting per-country ceilings based on 1910 census shares.
  4. The Pendleton Civil Service Act (1883), requiring competitive exams for federal jobs.

Correct: The Chinese Exclusion Act (1882), which barred the entry of Chinese laborers.

The editorial's racialized attack on "the Chinaman" lines up almost verbatim with the congressional debate that produced the 1882 Chinese Exclusion Act, the first federal law to bar a nationality. The Dawes Act addressed Indian policy, not immigration. The 1921 quota act matches the editorial's logic on Southern Europeans but comes a generation too late for Unit 6. Pendleton dealt with patronage, not immigrants.

Jane Addams and the Hull House settlement movement would be best characterized as a response to which of the following trends cited in the editorial?

  1. Farmers' declining political influence in the face of urban ethnic machines.
  2. The rapid concentration of Southern and Eastern European immigrants in crowded urban neighborhoods.
  3. The expansion of Chinese labor in Pacific Coast railroads and mines.
  4. The collapse of federal Reconstruction enforcement in the urban North.

Correct: The rapid concentration of Southern and Eastern European immigrants in crowded urban neighborhoods.

Hull House, opened in Chicago's Near West Side in 1889, aimed at the Italian, Greek, Russian Jewish, and Bohemian tenement population — precisely the "new immigrants" the editorial denounces. It offered English classes, childcare, and political advocacy, rejecting the nativist framing. The Chinese question played out largely on the Pacific Coast, far from Addams's work. Reconstruction policy and rural political decline are unrelated to settlement houses.

In Plessy v. Ferguson (1896), the Supreme Court upheld a Louisiana law requiring "equal but separate" railway cars. The decision is best understood as:

  1. A narrow ruling that applied only to interstate railroads and left state segregation untouched.
  2. A constitutional sanction for state-mandated racial segregation that accelerated the spread of Jim Crow laws.
  3. A reversal of Dred Scott v. Sandford that reaffirmed the Fourteenth Amendment's protections.
  4. A judicial endorsement of Booker T. Washington's Atlanta Compromise approach to race relations.

Correct: A constitutional sanction for state-mandated racial segregation that accelerated the spread of Jim Crow laws.

Justice Brown's majority opinion read the Fourteenth Amendment to allow "separate but equal" accommodations, giving Southern states a green light for the comprehensive Jim Crow codes, literacy tests, poll taxes, and grandfather clauses that disenfranchised Black voters by 1900. The decision actually licensed intrastate segregation; it did not repudiate Dred Scott (the 13th–15th Amendments had done that); and while Washington's 1895 Atlanta speech urged accommodation, the Court did not cite it as legal doctrine.

Free response

SAQ 1. Using your knowledge of the period 1865–1898, answer the three parts below.

(a) Briefly describe ONE specific way the federal government promoted industrial and westward expansion during the Gilded Age.

(b) Briefly explain ONE specific way that workers or farmers organized in response to the economic changes of this period.

(c) Briefly explain ONE specific way that African Americans or American Indians experienced a loss of rights or autonomy between 1877 and 1898.

Rubric

  • (a) Identify a concrete federal action — Pacific Railway Acts, Homestead Act (1862, still operative), protective tariffs (McKinley Tariff, 1890), use of federal troops to break strikes, or court/Sherman Act interpretations favoring capital — AND tie it to industrial/western growth.
  • (b) Identify a named organization or movement (Knights of Labor, AFL, Farmers' Alliance, Populist Party, specific strike) AND explain what it sought or did.
  • (c) Identify a named policy, court decision, or episode (Plessy v. Ferguson 1896, Dawes Act 1887, Wounded Knee 1890, poll taxes/literacy tests in 1890 Mississippi Constitution) AND explain the loss of rights or autonomy.

Model answer

(a) The Pacific Railway Acts granted transcontinental railroads enormous alternating sections of public land and federal loan bonds, subsidizing the Union Pacific and Central Pacific in completing the first transcontinental line in 1869. This federal land grant regime drew settlers, extractive industries, and capital into the trans-Mississippi West and created the long-haul freight market that fed Chicago meatpacking, Pittsburgh steel, and eastern finance.

(b) Farmers responded to falling crop prices, high freight rates, and mortgage debt by building the Southern and Northwestern Farmers' Alliances, which in 1892 became the People's (Populist) Party. At its Omaha convention the party demanded the free coinage of silver, a graduated income tax, direct election of senators, and government ownership of the railroads — an agrarian challenge to the Gilded Age industrial order.

(c) In Plessy v. Ferguson (1896) the Supreme Court upheld a Louisiana statute requiring "separate but equal" railway cars, constitutionalizing state-mandated segregation. Combined with Mississippi's 1890 constitution, which used poll taxes and literacy tests to strip Black men of the vote, the ruling cemented Jim Crow and rolled back the political citizenship won by Black Americans during Reconstruction.

Short DBQ. Using the three excerpts below and your knowledge of U.S. history, write a thesis-driven response to the following prompt. Take roughly 25 minutes.

Evaluate the extent to which industrial capitalism in the late nineteenth century (1865–1898) transformed the relationship between workers and employers in the United States.

Document A. "The great inventions of the nineteenth century — the steam engine, the Bessemer converter, the dynamo — have placed in the hands of the employer a productive power so enormous that the old relation of master and journeyman, founded on personal acquaintance, is gone forever. In its place stands the wage contract, impersonal and universal."

— Adapted from an 1886 economic treatise

Document B. "What does labor want? We want more schoolhouses and less jails; more books and less arsenals; more leisure and less greed; more justice and less revenge. We want more of the opportunities to cultivate our better natures."

— Adapted from Samuel Gompers, address to the American Federation of Labor, 1893

Document C. "The strike at Pullman is a revolt of the people against conditions that are intolerable. … An injunction has been issued forbidding even peaceful persuasion. The federal courts have made themselves, in effect, an arm of the railway corporations."

— Adapted from Eugene V. Debs, statement during the Pullman Strike, 1894

Rubric (abbreviated)

  • Thesis (1 pt): A defensible, historically nuanced claim about the extent of transformation, going beyond restating the prompt.
  • Contextualization (1 pt): Situate in post-Civil War industrialization: Second Industrial Revolution, immigration, urbanization, Reconstruction's end.
  • Document use (2 pts): Use at least two of the three documents to support the argument, describing content accurately.
  • Evidence beyond documents (1 pt): Cite specific outside evidence — Homestead 1892, Haymarket 1886, Sherman Antitrust Act (1890) and its early use against unions (In re Debs, 1895), Knights of Labor, scientific management, tenements, wage data.
  • Sourcing (1 pt): For at least one document, explain how POV, purpose, audience, or situation shapes the evidence (e.g., Debs's statement is advocacy from a strike leader facing federal injunction).
  • Complexity (1 pt): Show nuance — e.g., continuity as well as change, regional or racial variation, or distinction between skilled-trade AFL members and the industrial/immigrant workforce.

Model thesis

Industrial capitalism fundamentally transformed the worker-employer relationship between 1865 and 1898 by replacing the older artisanal bond with an impersonal national wage labor market and by pulling the federal government into the role of strikebreaker, but the transformation was uneven — skilled craft workers organized in the AFL retained significant bargaining power, while unskilled, immigrant, and Black workers often faced conditions closer to unfree labor, revealing that the Gilded Age produced not one new labor relationship but a stratified system.

Model paragraph sketch

The Second Industrial Revolution, as Document A notes, destroyed the master-journeyman workshop by scaling production around the Bessemer converter, the dynamo, and the transcontinental railroad. In vertically integrated firms like Carnegie Steel, workers became interchangeable operators of capital-intensive machines, and employers like Frick treated wage contracts as commodities to be cut during downturns — the 1892 Homestead lockout and the destruction of the Amalgamated Association are the emblem. Workers responded collectively. The Knights of Labor briefly organized across skill and race lines in the mid-1880s before Haymarket (1886) discredited them, and Samuel Gompers's AFL (Document B) consolidated a narrower strategy of skilled-trade bread-and-butter unionism. Gompers's address, delivered to a labor audience, is a programmatic statement and frames workers as citizens demanding civic goods. Document C, Debs's 1894 statement from the Pullman Strike, must be read as partisan advocacy — but it accurately describes a new reality: the Cleveland administration's use of federal injunctions and troops under the Sherman Antitrust Act, upheld in In re Debs (1895), inserted the federal government decisively on the side of capital. At the same time, continuity persisted: Southern Black sharecroppers locked into debt peonage after Reconstruction's collapse, Chinese laborers excluded in 1882, and immigrant women in tenement sweatshops all experienced a labor regime closer to dependency than to the "universal wage contract." The transformation was real but stratified — the Gilded Age created a modern industrial labor market and preserved older forms of coerced and racialized work beneath it.
