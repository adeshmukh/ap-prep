Multiple choice

"The price which society pays for the law of competition, like the price it pays for cheap comforts and luxuries, is… great; but the advantages of this law are also greater still, for it is to this law that we owe our wonderful material development… While the law may be sometimes hard for the individual, it is best for the race, because it insures the survival of the fittest in every department. We accept and welcome, therefore… great inequality of environment, the concentration of business, industrial and commercial, in the hands of a few… as being not only beneficial, but essential for the future progress of the race."

— Adapted from Andrew Carnegie, "Wealth" (the "Gospel of Wealth"), North American Review, 1889

Carnegie's argument in the excerpt most directly draws upon which of the following late-nineteenth-century intellectual traditions?

  1. The Social Gospel theology of Walter Rauschenbusch, which called for Christian reconstruction of industrial society.
  2. Social Darwinism, associated with William Graham Sumner and Herbert Spencer, which applied "survival of the fittest" to economic competition.
  3. Henry George's single-tax doctrine, which blamed inequality on unearned rent on land.
  4. Edward Bellamy's nationalist socialism outlined in Looking Backward.

Correct: Social Darwinism (Sumner/Spencer).

Carnegie borrows Herbert Spencer's phrase "survival of the fittest" and treats economic concentration as both natural and beneficial — textbook Social Darwinism as popularized by William Graham Sumner. The Social Gospel, by contrast, was the opposite intellectual current: Rauschenbusch and Washington Gladden indicted industrial inequality as sinful. Henry George's Progress and Poverty (1879) and Bellamy's Looking Backward (1888) were both critiques of Gilded Age capitalism, not celebrations of it. Carnegie's own "Gospel of Wealth" softened this view with a duty of elite philanthropy (Carnegie Libraries), but the underlying logic of competition in the excerpt is Spencerian.

Which of Carnegie's own business practices best illustrates his contribution to the organizational transformation of American industry?

  1. Horizontal integration — combining competing oil refineries into a single trust.
  2. Vertical integration — controlling iron ore mines, coke fields, railroads, and steel mills within one firm to cut costs and dependence on suppliers.
  3. Interlocking directorates across banks and insurance companies to centralize finance capital.
  4. Franchising a retail chain of low-price general stores across the rural South.

Correct: Vertical integration.

Carnegie Steel controlled every stage of production from Mesabi iron ore and Connellsville coke through rail transport to finished steel — the paradigmatic example of vertical integration, alongside Bessemer-process innovations. Horizontal integration via trust is Rockefeller and Standard Oil. Interlocking directorates and finance capital describe J.P. Morgan's domain (Morgan in fact bought out Carnegie in 1901 to form U.S. Steel). Rural general-store franchising is an anachronistic distractor that does not describe any major Gilded Age firm.

The business structure that allowed John D. Rockefeller's Standard Oil to achieve the dominance depicted in the cartoon is best described as which of the following?

  1. Horizontal integration culminating in the Standard Oil Trust (1882), which placed the stock of competing refining firms under a single board of trustees.
  2. A federally chartered monopoly modeled on the Second Bank of the United States.
  3. A worker-owned cooperative modeled on the Knights of Labor's producer-cooperatives.
  4. A public-private partnership with state governments similar to those that built the Erie Canal.

Correct: Horizontal integration via the Standard Oil Trust.

Rockefeller combined or drove out competing refiners — by 1879 Standard controlled about 90% of U.S. refining — and in 1882 organized the Standard Oil Trust, a legal device holding stock of once-independent firms under a single board of trustees. There was no federal charter; on the contrary, Standard used railroad rebates and drawbacks rather than governmental privilege. It was the opposite of a worker-owned cooperative, and it was private, not a canal-style public improvement.

Which piece of federal legislation was most directly enacted in response to concerns of the sort expressed in the cartoon?

  1. The Pendleton Civil Service Act of 1883.
  2. The Dawes Severalty Act of 1887.
  3. The Sherman Antitrust Act of 1890.
  4. The Gold Standard Act of 1900.

Correct: The Sherman Antitrust Act (1890).

The Sherman Act declared "every contract, combination in the form of trust or otherwise, or conspiracy, in restraint of trade" illegal, targeting the kind of concentration the cartoon depicts. The Pendleton Act reformed federal hiring. The Dawes Act concerned allotment of Indian reservations. The Gold Standard Act settled the currency question after the 1896 election. Initially, however, the Sherman Act was used far more often against labor unions (e.g., In re Debs, 1895) and narrowed in U.S. v. E.C. Knight (1895) than used effectively against trusts.

"If the roads in proper duty to the public can reduce wages, then by what right do they prohibit their employees from organizing for the purpose of reducing rents, or reducing the prices of the necessities of life?… The public interest requires of the corporations that they shall give fair wages — the same public interest requires of the government that it shall not allow great combinations of capital to use the machinery of injunction and of federal troops as a club against working men."

— Adapted from a Chicago newspaper editorial in defense of the American Railway Union during the Pullman Strike, 1894

The editorial's complaint about "the machinery of injunction and of federal troops" most directly refers to which of the following developments?

  1. President Rutherford B. Hayes's deployment of troops during the Great Railroad Strike of 1877.
  2. President Grover Cleveland's use of a federal injunction (obtained under the Sherman Antitrust Act) and army troops to break the Pullman Strike led by Eugene V. Debs, later upheld in In re Debs (1895).
  3. President Benjamin Harrison's direct command of the Pinkerton force at the Homestead Strike of 1892.
  4. President William McKinley's dispatch of the National Guard to the anthracite fields in 1902.

Correct: Cleveland's injunction and troops against the Pullman Strike, upheld in In re Debs.

During the 1894 Pullman boycott, Attorney General Richard Olney obtained a federal injunction (citing interference with the mails and interstate commerce) and President Cleveland sent federal troops over the objections of Illinois Governor John Altgeld. The Supreme Court upheld the injunction in In re Debs (1895), setting the template for anti-labor use of antitrust law. The 1877 strike did involve federal troops under Hayes but without the injunction framework the editorial is protesting. Homestead (1892) was fought by Pinkertons hired by Carnegie/Frick and by Pennsylvania militia, not federal forces under Harrison. The 1902 anthracite strike post-dates this editorial and ended in arbitration, not strike-breaking.

Compared with Terence Powderly's Knights of Labor, Samuel Gompers's American Federation of Labor (founded 1886) differed in which of the following ways?

  1. The AFL sought to abolish the wage system entirely through producer cooperatives.
  2. The AFL organized skilled craft workers in separate national unions and focused on "bread and butter" goals — higher wages, shorter hours, and union recognition — rather than sweeping political transformation.
  3. The AFL welcomed unskilled, Black, and women workers on equal terms from its founding.
  4. The AFL allied formally with the Socialist Party of America after 1901.

Correct: Skilled craft unionism focused on bread-and-butter goals.

Gompers's AFL was a federation of autonomous craft unions of skilled workers pursuing contracts, wages, and hours — the model that survived after Haymarket (1886) destroyed the Knights. Abolishing the wage system was closer to the Knights' cooperative ideal. The AFL did not organize unskilled workers, African Americans, or women on equal footing — its exclusions are well documented. It never affiliated with the Socialist Party; Gompers was hostile to socialism.

The Haymarket affair of 1886 most directly affected the American labor movement by

  1. forcing Congress to pass the first federal eight-hour-day law for private employees.
  2. discrediting the Knights of Labor in public opinion by linking organized labor with anarchist violence, accelerating the Knights' collapse and clearing the way for the AFL.
  3. prompting the Supreme Court to legalize the secondary boycott as a legitimate labor weapon.
  4. producing the first recognized collective bargaining agreement in American heavy industry.

Correct: It discredited the Knights and linked labor with anarchism.

A bomb thrown at police during a Chicago rally on May 4, 1886, killed several officers and led to the conviction (and hanging) of anarchist organizers. Although the Knights had no role in planting the bomb, public opinion conflated them with the anarchists, and Knights membership collapsed afterward — the AFL's craft unionism took over. Congress passed no general eight-hour-day law in this period. The Court narrowed, not expanded, labor's use of boycotts. No major industry recognized collective bargaining until the 1930s.

"In the tenements all the influences make for evil; because they are the hot-beds of the epidemics that carry death to rich and poor alike; the nurseries of pauperism and crime that fill our jails and police courts… The boundary line of the Other Half lies through the tenements. It is there that the sanitary reformer, the social reformer, the moral reformer have their work cut out for them."

— Adapted from Jacob Riis, How the Other Half Lives, 1890

Riis's exposé was most directly a response to which late-nineteenth-century development?

  1. The westward migration of sharecroppers into the Great Plains following the Homestead Act.
  2. The concentration of "new immigrants" from southern and eastern Europe in dense, unregulated "dumbbell" tenements in American cities — especially New York, where most arrivals after 1892 came through Ellis Island.
  3. The growth of Mormon settlements in Utah following the end of federal prosecution of plural marriage.
  4. The migration of freedpeople to Black towns such as Nicodemus, Kansas (the Exoduster movement).

Correct: New immigrants crowded into urban tenements.

Riis's photographs and prose documented the Lower East Side and similar districts where Italian, Russian-Jewish, Polish, and other "new immigrants" lived in "dumbbell" tenements with minimal light and ventilation. The Homestead movement concerned rural settlement, not urban tenements. The Mormon and Exoduster options describe genuine Gilded Age migrations but neither is the subject of How the Other Half Lives.

Which of the following represents the most significant contemporaneous reformist response to the urban conditions Riis described?

  1. Jane Addams and Ellen Gates Starr's founding of Hull House in Chicago (1889), a settlement house that offered kindergartens, English classes, health clinics, and political advocacy in immigrant neighborhoods.
  2. The Freedmen's Bureau, which provided schools and food relief in the urban North.
  3. Dorothea Dix's nationwide campaign to replace tenement housing with state asylums.
  4. The Federal Housing Administration's program of mortgage insurance for working-class homebuyers.

Correct: Hull House and the settlement-house movement.

Settlement houses brought middle-class reformers (often college-educated women like Addams, Florence Kelley, and Lillian Wald) to live among immigrants and provide services and political advocacy — a signature Gilded Age response to urban poverty. The Freedmen's Bureau was a Reconstruction-era southern agency, shut down in 1872. Dix's asylum reform dates to the 1840s and concerned mental illness, not tenements. The FHA is a 1934 New Deal agency, far too late.

Urban political machines such as New York's Tammany Hall maintained immigrant loyalty most directly by

  1. enforcing strict civil service rules that guaranteed merit-based hiring for new arrivals.
  2. providing jobs, emergency aid, help with naturalization, and informal social services in exchange for bloc voting at the polls.
  3. campaigning for racially restrictive zoning laws that protected immigrant neighborhoods from African American migration.
  4. founding settlement houses in immigrant neighborhoods as an official arm of the Democratic Party.

Correct: Jobs, aid, naturalization help, and informal services in exchange for votes.

Machines like Tammany (infamously under William "Boss" Tweed, later George Washington Plunkitt) traded patronage, coal deliveries, funeral costs, and help naturalizing newcomers for reliable immigrant votes. Civil service merit hiring was the opposite of the machine system — it was imposed over the machines by the 1883 Pendleton Act after Garfield's assassination. Racial zoning and settlement houses are misattributions; Hull House was independent of machine politics and often challenged it.

"Be it enacted… That from and after the expiration of ninety days next after the passage of this act, and until the expiration of ten years next after the passage of this act, the coming of Chinese laborers to the United States be… suspended; and during such suspension it shall not be lawful for any Chinese laborer to come… into the United States."

— Adapted from the Chinese Exclusion Act, United States Statutes at Large, 1882

The Chinese Exclusion Act is historically significant in United States immigration law primarily because it

  1. created the first federal agency to screen immigrants for infectious disease.
  2. was the first major federal law to bar immigration on the basis of nationality/race, responding to pressure from West Coast white labor (Kearney's Workingmen's Party of California) and setting a precedent for later national-origin restrictions.
  3. required that all immigrants pass a literacy test in English before entering the country.
  4. established Ellis Island as the processing station for Pacific arrivals.

Correct: First major federal law barring immigration on the basis of nationality/race.

The 1882 act singled out Chinese laborers for exclusion and was extended repeatedly (made permanent in 1902), establishing the legal and ideological precedent for the Immigration Act of 1891, the literacy-test push, and eventually the 1924 National Origins Act. It was driven by West Coast workingmen blaming Chinese laborers for wage competition. A literacy test was proposed in the 1890s but not enacted until 1917; disease screening came later; Ellis Island (opened 1892) processed Atlantic arrivals, while Chinese and Japanese arrivals were processed at Angel Island beginning in 1910.

Document A. "We demand a national currency, safe, sound, and flexible, issued by the general government only… We demand free and unlimited coinage of silver and gold at the present legal ratio of 16 to 1… We demand a graduated income tax… We believe that the telegraph and telephone, like the post-office system, being a necessity for the transmission of news, should be owned and operated by the government in the interest of the people. The time has come when the railroad corporations will either own the people or the people must own the railroads."
— Adapted from the Omaha Platform of the People's (Populist) Party, 1892

Document B. "You shall not press down upon the brow of labor this crown of thorns, you shall not crucify mankind upon a cross of gold."
— William Jennings Bryan, Democratic National Convention, Chicago, 1896

The Omaha Platform's demand for "free and unlimited coinage of silver… at… 16 to 1" was most directly a response to which economic condition?

  1. Runaway wage inflation that wiped out urban workers' purchasing power during the 1880s.
  2. Decades of deflation that increased the real burden of farm mortgages and crop-lien debts, which farmers and their organizations (the Grange, the Farmers' Alliances) blamed on the post–Civil War contraction of the money supply and the demonetization of silver in 1873.
  3. A shortage of banks in the urban Northeast that prevented railroad financing.
  4. A sharp increase in commodity prices caused by overseas competition from Argentine and Russian wheat.

Correct: Long deflation that increased the real burden of farmers' debts.

Between 1865 and 1896 wholesale prices fell by roughly half. Farmers who had borrowed in the 1870s had to repay in steadily more valuable dollars. Coining silver freely at 16:1 would have expanded the money supply and raised commodity prices — relieving debtors. The Grange (1867) and the Northern/Southern Farmers' Alliances built the organizational base that became the People's Party. The wage-inflation option inverts reality. Banking concentration was a complaint but not what 16:1 addressed. Cheap foreign wheat was real but the 16:1 demand was about the currency, not trade.

Which of the following best describes the political consequence of Bryan's 1896 campaign and the election that followed?

  1. Populist demands were almost entirely ignored by the two major parties for the next two decades.
  2. Bryan's defeat by William McKinley (who rode high protective-tariff politics and the votes of urban workers anxious about silver) helped complete a partisan realignment that made the Republicans the majority party until the New Deal, while many Populist reforms (income tax, direct election of senators, railroad regulation) were later absorbed into the Progressive agenda.
  3. The Populist Party won the presidency outright when its candidate fused with the Democrats.
  4. The Supreme Court immediately invalidated the Gold Standard Act as unconstitutional, restoring bimetallism.

Correct: Republican realignment plus later absorption of Populist ideas by Progressives.

McKinley's 1896 victory inaugurated a Republican majority era lasting (outside Wilson's two terms) until 1932. Yet key Populist demands reached the Constitution: the 16th (income tax) and 17th (direct election of senators) Amendments in 1913, plus Progressive railroad regulation (Hepburn Act, 1906). Populist ideas were not "ignored." Populists fused with Democrats to back Bryan but did not win the presidency. The Gold Standard Act (1900) was not invalidated.

The Pendleton Civil Service Act of 1883 was most directly prompted by which of the following events, and it signaled what kind of shift in the federal government?

  1. The Credit Mobilier scandal of the Grant administration, pushing Congress to nationalize the transcontinental railroads.
  2. The assassination of President James A. Garfield in 1881 by a disappointed office-seeker, Charles Guiteau, pushing Congress to replace the spoils system with competitive examination and merit-based hiring for a growing share of federal jobs.
  3. The Tweed Ring's conviction in New York, pushing Congress to abolish all urban political machines by statute.
  4. The Haymarket bombing in Chicago, pushing Congress to authorize federal employment of anti-anarchist informants.

Correct: Garfield's assassination by Guiteau produced the merit-exam civil service.

Garfield's shooting by a spurned patronage-seeker crystallized public demand for merit-based federal hiring. The Pendleton Act created the Civil Service Commission and competitive examinations for a limited but growing set of federal positions, beginning the slow displacement of the spoils system. Credit Mobilier (1872) and the Tweed Ring (early 1870s) exposed Gilded Age corruption generally but did not produce federal civil service reform. Haymarket (1886) was after Pendleton and concerns labor, not patronage.

The Dawes Severalty Act of 1887 attempted to transform Native American life primarily by

  1. creating reservations in the Indian Territory (present-day Oklahoma) for the Five Civilized Tribes.
  2. dividing collectively held tribal lands into individual family allotments and selling the "surplus" to white settlers, with full U.S. citizenship contingent on accepting allotment and assimilationist schooling (e.g., the Carlisle Indian Industrial School).
  3. granting full tribal sovereignty and recognizing treaties as the supreme law of the land.
  4. establishing the Bureau of Indian Affairs as an independent agency under Native control.

Correct: Allotment of tribal land to individuals plus sale of "surplus" to whites.

The Dawes Act broke up communal landholding, assigned 160-acre allotments to heads of household, and opened the "surplus" to non-Indian purchase — reducing tribal land from roughly 138 million acres in 1887 to 48 million by 1934. It was part of the same assimilationist package that included Carlisle (founded 1879, motto "Kill the Indian, and save the man"). Indian Territory reservations predate Dawes. Far from affirming sovereignty, Dawes dismantled it. The BIA was not reorganized under Native control in this period.

The Wounded Knee Massacre of December 1890 is best understood as

  1. an opening battle of the Plains Indian Wars that began the long military campaign against the Sioux.
  2. a massacre of roughly 250–300 Lakota, many unarmed women and children, by the U.S. 7th Cavalry amid federal attempts to suppress the Ghost Dance religious revival — conventionally treated as the symbolic end of armed Native resistance in the West after earlier defeats at Little Bighorn (1876) and Chief Joseph's 1877 surrender.
  3. a negotiated treaty that restored the Black Hills to the Sioux following a Supreme Court ruling.
  4. a minor Apache skirmish in Arizona Territory led by Geronimo.

Correct: Massacre during suppression of the Ghost Dance; symbolic end of Plains resistance.

Wounded Knee came at the end, not the beginning, of the Plains Wars; the Ghost Dance movement led by Wovoka had alarmed federal officials, who moved to disarm Lakota at Pine Ridge. The Black Hills were not returned. Geronimo's Apache campaigns ended with his 1886 surrender, well before Wounded Knee. Helen Hunt Jackson's A Century of Dishonor (1881) had already shaped elite opinion against federal Indian policy.

Frederick Jackson Turner's 1893 "frontier thesis," delivered at the Chicago World's Fair, argued that

  1. the frontier had made American politics permanently hostile to democracy and nurtured aristocratic values.
  2. the availability of "free land" on the frontier had shaped American democracy and individualism, and that the Census Bureau's 1890 declaration that the frontier was "closed" marked the end of a defining era in American development.
  3. Native Americans would eventually assimilate and form a new nationality fused with European settlers.
  4. the overseas empire in the Caribbean and Pacific was a natural continuation of western expansion and a moral duty.

Correct: Frontier "free land" shaped American democracy; its 1890 closing ended a defining era.

Turner made the frontier rather than European inheritance the engine of American democracy, individualism, and egalitarianism — papering over Native dispossession, which his "free land" framing obscured. Turner's thesis was celebratory of democracy, not critical. He did not focus on Native assimilation or overseas empire — though later imperialists borrowed his anxiety about the "closed" frontier to justify expansion abroad.

The Mississippi Constitution of 1890, together with devices such as the grandfather clause, white primary, and felony disfranchisement, was designed primarily to do which of the following?

  1. Evade the 15th Amendment's prohibition on racial voting restrictions by using ostensibly race-neutral requirements — poll taxes, literacy/"understanding" tests, and grandfather clauses — that in practice disfranchised African American voters.
  2. Encourage European immigration to the Deep South by rewarding newcomers with automatic citizenship.
  3. Reinforce Reconstruction-era Republican political control of the state.
  4. Enfranchise women ahead of the rest of the South as a reward for Confederate service.

Correct: It evaded the 15th Amendment using ostensibly race-neutral devices.

Poll taxes, "understanding" clauses, literacy tests administered by white registrars, and grandfather clauses were designed to strip Black voters from the rolls without naming race — and they worked: Black registration in Mississippi fell from a majority of adult men in the 1870s to a fraction of one percent by 1900. The other options invert reality: disfranchisement discouraged immigration and cemented the all-white "Solid South," not Republican control. Women of any race could not vote in Mississippi until 1920.

Taken together with the lynching wave exposed by Ida B. Wells, Plessy v. Ferguson established which of the following constitutional doctrines?

  1. That the 14th Amendment's equal protection clause barred all state-mandated racial classifications in public accommodations.
  2. That state-mandated racial segregation was constitutional so long as the separate facilities were nominally "equal" — the "separate but equal" doctrine, which effectively blessed the Jim Crow order emerging across the South.
  3. That the federal government could prosecute private lynchings under a general federal conspiracy statute.
  4. That poll taxes violated the 15th Amendment and could no longer be enforced.

Correct: The "separate but equal" doctrine.

Plessy upheld Louisiana's Separate Car Act 7–1, articulating "separate but equal" as compatible with the 14th Amendment. Ida B. Wells's Southern Horrors (1892) used investigation and statistics to expose lynching as an instrument of racial and economic terror aimed especially at successful Black businessmen and voters — the violent counterpart to the legal regime Plessy blessed. The first option is what Brown v. Board (1954) would later hold. The federal-conspiracy option describes what the Court had already rejected in U.S. v. Cruikshank (1876). Poll taxes were not struck down until the 24th Amendment (1964) and Harper v. Virginia Board of Elections (1966).

In the same year as Plessy, Booker T. Washington's 1895 Atlanta Compromise speech — delivered in a New South of Henry Grady's industrial boosterism — proposed that African Americans

  1. demand immediate enforcement of the 14th and 15th Amendments through federal litigation coordinated by a new national organization.
  2. set aside immediate political and civil rights agitation, accept segregation temporarily ("in all things that are purely social we can be as separate as the fingers"), and focus on industrial education (Tuskegee) and economic self-improvement as a foundation for later equality.
  3. migrate en masse to Liberia under the American Colonization Society's sponsorship.
  4. form a fully integrated third political party with white Populists to contest Southern elections on equal terms.

Correct: Accept segregation for now and focus on industrial education and economic self-help.

Washington's "cast down your bucket where you are" speech at the Atlanta Cotton States Exposition urged accommodation to the racial status quo of Grady's "New South" while Black Southerners built vocational skills (Tuskegee Institute) and economic capital — a position W.E.B. Du Bois would attack in The Souls of Black Folk (1903) and via the Niagara Movement and NAACP. The federal-litigation answer describes the later NAACP strategy. Mass emigration and an integrated third-party strategy were floated but were not Washington's program.

Free response

SAQ. Using your knowledge of the period 1865–1898, answer (a), (b), and (c).

(a) Identify ONE specific federal action between 1865 and 1898 that promoted the growth of American industrial capitalism.

(b) Explain ONE specific way in which industrial workers OR agrarian farmers organized to respond to the conditions of industrial capitalism in this period.

(c) Explain ONE specific way in which the political or civil rights of African Americans OR Native Americans were rolled back or restricted between 1877 and 1898.

Rubric

  • (a) 1 point for a specific federal action promoting industrial capitalism with explanation: Pacific Railway Acts and federal land grants enabling the transcontinental railroad (completed 1869); high protective tariffs (e.g., McKinley Tariff 1890); federal troops in the Great Railroad Strike of 1877; federal injunction and troops in the Pullman Strike (1894), upheld in In re Debs (1895); U.S. v. E.C. Knight (1895) narrowing Sherman Antitrust enforcement against trusts.
  • (b) 1 point for a specific labor or farmer organizing effort with explanation: Knights of Labor (Powderly); AFL under Gompers (1886, skilled craft); Haymarket (1886); Homestead (1892, Carnegie/Frick/Pinkertons); Pullman (1894, Debs); Grange and Granger laws; Farmers' Alliances and subtreasury plan; People's Party and Omaha Platform (1892); Bryan's 1896 Cross of Gold campaign.
  • (c) 1 point for a specific rollback with explanation: Dawes Severalty Act (1887) breaking up tribal land; Wounded Knee Massacre (1890); Carlisle assimilationist boarding schools; Mississippi Plan disfranchisement (poll tax, literacy/understanding test, grandfather clause, white primary); Jim Crow segregation and Plessy v. Ferguson (1896); the lynching wave and Ida B. Wells's crusade.

Model answer

(a) One federal action that powerfully promoted industrial capitalism was the Pacific Railway Acts, under which Congress granted the Union Pacific and Central Pacific millions of acres of public land plus low-interest federal bonds. Completed in 1869 and joined at Promontory Summit, the transcontinental railroad integrated a national market for raw materials and finished goods, enabled large-scale meatpacking and steel distribution, and stimulated the further railroad construction that became the largest industry in the late-nineteenth-century United States.

(b) Agrarian farmers organized in response to falling crop prices, high railroad freight rates, and crop-lien debt. In the late 1860s the Grange began as a social and educational body but produced "Granger laws" regulating rates. By the late 1880s the Northern and Southern Farmers' Alliances had built a mass movement and proposed the subtreasury plan to warehouse crops and extend federal loans. In 1892 the Alliances launched the People's (Populist) Party and adopted the Omaha Platform, demanding free silver coinage at 16:1, a graduated income tax, direct election of senators, and government ownership of railroads and telegraphs — a reform program that reshaped the 1896 election and was later absorbed into the Progressive agenda.

(c) African Americans' political rights were systematically rolled back after Reconstruction. The Mississippi Constitution of 1890 pioneered the disfranchising package — a poll tax, a literacy/"understanding" test administered at the discretion of white registrars, and eventually a grandfather clause and a white primary — that was copied across the South. These devices, combined with the Supreme Court's retreat in cases like U.S. v. Cruikshank (1876), the Civil Rights Cases (1883), and Plessy v. Ferguson (1896, "separate but equal"), built a Jim Crow regime of de jure segregation and disfranchisement that endured until the 1960s. Black voter registration in Mississippi collapsed from a majority of adult men during Reconstruction to a fraction of one percent by 1900.

"During the years 1880 to 1910, the flood of new immigrants from southern and eastern Europe — Italians, Poles, Hungarians, Slovaks, Russian and Polish Jews — supplied the mass labor for the new industrial economy. In cities like Chicago, Pittsburgh, and New York the foreign-born and their children made up more than three-quarters of the wage-earning population in basic industry. The tariff policy of the Gilded Age (culminating in the McKinley Tariff of 1890) sheltered that industry behind some of the highest rates in American history, while the overseas turn of 1898 — the annexation of Hawai'i, the Spanish-American War, and the acquisition of Puerto Rico, Guam, and the Philippines — opened new markets and naval stations for its products."
— Adapted from a modern synthesis of U.S. economic history, 2002

Rubric (abbreviated, DBQ-style)

  • Thesis (1): A historically defensible claim taking a position on the extent of transformation (e.g., "Industrial capitalism remade American work by concentrating production, recruiting mass immigrant labor, and routinizing jobs — but the benefits were distributed sharply unequally, and workers responded with a wave of organizing that federal power repeatedly crushed").
  • Contextualization (1): Situate the period within the Second Industrial Revolution (Bessemer steel, electrification, railroads), the post–Civil War long deflation, the "new immigration" from southern and eastern Europe, and the Gilded Age tariff/currency debates that led up to 1898.
  • Document use (2): Use at least four of the five documents and connect each accurately to the argument.
  • Sourcing (1): For at least one document, explain how point of view, purpose, audience, or historical situation affects its meaning (e.g., Carnegie as industrialist-booster writing to a transatlantic audience; the Knights' Preamble as a recruitment/agitation text).
  • Evidence beyond the documents (1): At least one specific fact not in the documents — e.g., the 1877 Great Railroad Strike, Haymarket (1886), AFL founding (1886), the Pullman Strike and In re Debs (1895), the Chinese Exclusion Act (1882), Hull House (1889), Tammany Hall, vertical vs. horizontal integration, U.S. v. E.C. Knight (1895).
  • Complex understanding (1): Analyze transformation along multiple dimensions (productivity, living standards, ethnic composition, deskilling, labor conflict, gender, the relationship between industrial capacity and the overseas turn of 1898) or over time, rather than a single-axis claim.

Model answer (condensed)

Between 1865 and 1898 industrial capitalism utterly transformed the lives of American workers — but that transformation cut in opposite directions. It raised total output and national income, yet it also concentrated wealth, deskilled craft labor, redrew the ethnic map of the working class, and was enforced by state violence whenever workers tried to bargain with the new giants. By 1898 that same industrial capacity had outgrown the domestic market and propelled the United States into overseas empire.

The defining fact of the era was the Second Industrial Revolution: Bessemer steel, electrification (Edison, Tesla, Westinghouse), the transcontinental railroad (completed 1869), the telegraph and telephone, and the corporate forms — Carnegie's vertical integration of steel, Rockefeller's Standard Oil Trust (horizontal integration), and J.P. Morgan's finance-capital consolidations — that scaled production. Document 1 is Carnegie's own celebration of the productivity revolution: "Two pounds of steel… sold for one penny." Its purpose — a volume called Triumphant Democracy — is to advertise American industrial success to a transatlantic audience, so it emphasizes output rather than the human costs of the twelve-hour shift at a Homestead blast furnace.

Those costs were real. Document 3, Jacob Riis's tenement scene — one hundred forty children in a single building, cornmeal mush, a guttering candle — documents the urban poverty in which the new industrial workforce lived. Document 5 supplies the demographic fact behind that scene: by the 1890s the "new immigration" from Italy, Poland, Hungary, and the Russian and Polish Jewish Pale made up the overwhelming majority of wage earners in basic industry in cities like Chicago and Pittsburgh. Ellis Island opened in 1892; Angel Island would open in 1910. Chinese workers had already been excluded by the 1882 Chinese Exclusion Act at the demand of West Coast workingmen, a reminder that the industrial working class was segmented by race and nationality from the start, and that urban machines like Tammany Hall mediated immigrant political incorporation in exchange for bloc votes.

Workers responded by organizing. Document 2, the Knights of Labor's 1878 preamble, invited "every branch of honorable toil, without regard to nationality, sex, creed, or color" — an extraordinary (if imperfectly practiced) vision. But the Knights collapsed after the Haymarket bombing (1886) tarred all labor with the brush of anarchism, and Samuel Gompers's AFL (founded 1886) instead built craft unionism around skilled, mostly white male workers pursuing "bread and butter" goals. When workers did fight — the Great Railroad Strike of 1877, Homestead in 1892 (Document 4), the Pullman boycott of 1894 — federal and state power crushed them: Pinkertons and the Pennsylvania militia at Homestead, federal troops and a Sherman Act injunction (upheld in In re Debs, 1895) at Pullman.

Document 4 is particularly revealing because it is a historian's synthesis rather than an immediate source; its analytic distance lets it state plainly that within four months "the Amalgamated Association of Iron and Steel Workers was broken." That outcome is the counterweight to Carnegie's Document 1: the productivity miracle depended on the ability of Carnegie Steel to impose twelve-hour days at the price it chose, and when the state was asked to choose between capital and labor, it chose capital.

Document 5's reference to the McKinley Tariff and the 1898 overseas turn ties the domestic story to American empire. High tariffs sheltered the industries that Carnegie, Rockefeller, and Morgan had built; the Spanish-American War opened overseas markets and naval stations for their products. The transformation of American work, in other words, was not just a domestic phenomenon — by 1898 it was reshaping the American state's posture in the world, launching the themes that define Unit 7.

Beyond the documents, reform impulses from Hull House (Addams, 1889) to the Social Gospel (Rauschenbusch, Gladden) to Henry George's Progress and Poverty and the Populist Omaha Platform (1892) insisted that the new economy had to be regulated on behalf of its workers and debtors. Most of these demands were absorbed into Progressive legislation only after 1900. In the Gilded Age itself, the transformation of American work was enormous in scope, uneven in benefit, and contested at every turn.
