Modern America: Conservative Resurgence, End of the Cold War, Globalization, and Polarization

Twenty-eight stimulus-driven multiple choice questions and three free-response prompts covering the Reagan Revolution, the end of the Cold War, 1990s globalization and culture wars, 9/11 and the War on Terror, the 2008 financial crisis, the Obama years, and the Trump–Biden era.

28 multiple choice 3 free response AP-level

Multiple choice

"In this present crisis, government is not the solution to our problem; government is the problem… It is time to check and reverse the growth of government, which shows signs of having grown beyond the consent of the governed. It is my intention to curb the size and influence of the Federal establishment and to demand recognition of the distinction between the powers granted to the Federal Government and those reserved to the States or to the people."

— Ronald Reagan, First Inaugural Address, January 20, 1981

Reagan's argument in the excerpt most directly drew on which longer-running political tradition that had been gathering momentum in the 1970s?

  1. The Great Society liberalism of Lyndon Johnson, updated for the 1980s.
  2. A New Right coalition of economic libertarians, anti-tax activists, and evangelical Christians that had emerged out of opposition to the welfare state and 1960s cultural change.
  3. The Popular Front left of the 1930s, reconstituted around deindustrialization.
  4. The Progressive Era faith in regulatory expertise expressed by Theodore Roosevelt.

Correct: A New Right coalition of economic libertarians, anti-tax activists, and evangelical Christians.

Reagan's 1980 victory was built on the convergence of supply-side tax-cutters, Sunbelt business conservatives, the Moral Majority (Jerry Falwell, 1979), and anti-tax voters mobilized by episodes like California's Proposition 13 (1978). Great Society liberalism is the political tradition Reagan is explicitly rejecting. The Popular Front of the 1930s was a left/Communist coalition, the opposite of Reagan's base. Progressive Era faith in regulatory expertise is exactly what Reagan is attacking when he calls government "the problem."

Which of the following policy actions taken during Reagan's first term most directly expressed the vision articulated in the excerpt?

  1. The creation of the Department of Education and expansion of federal student aid.
  2. The Economic Recovery Tax Act of 1981, which cut top marginal income tax rates and indexed brackets to inflation.
  3. The ratification of the Equal Rights Amendment as proposed by Congress in 1972.
  4. The nationalization of the savings-and-loan industry to stabilize the housing market.

Correct: The Economic Recovery Tax Act of 1981 (Kemp-Roth).

ERTA embodied supply-side economics — the belief that cutting marginal rates would spur growth and eventually increase revenue — and was Reagan's signature first-year legislative win. The Department of Education was created under Carter in 1979; Reagan in fact proposed (unsuccessfully) to abolish it. The ERA stalled in state legislatures and expired unratified in 1982; Reagan opposed it. The S&L industry was not nationalized — it was deregulated, and its later collapse was an expensive consequence.

Reagan's broader first-term economic program also included which of the following, signaling his administration's posture toward organized labor?

  1. Firing the striking air traffic controllers (PATCO) in August 1981 and decertifying their union, accelerating a long decline in union density.
  2. Invoking the Taft-Hartley Act to force striking auto workers back on the line in Detroit.
  3. Nationalizing U.S. Steel to prevent plant closures in the Rust Belt.
  4. Signing card-check legislation to streamline union recognition.

Correct: Firing the striking PATCO controllers and decertifying their union.

The PATCO firings signaled to employers nationwide that the federal government would no longer shield unions, accelerating a decades-long decline in union density. Reagan did not invoke Taft-Hartley in this way, and no auto strike of that scale occurred in 1981. U.S. Steel was not nationalized — Reagan opposed such interventions on principle. Card-check legislation is a pro-union reform never associated with Reagan.

"Silence = Death. The U.S. government has yet to commit the resources necessary to stop this epidemic. Why? Because those who are dying are presumed not to matter: gay men, IV drug users, poor people, people of color. We demand a coordinated, well-funded national program of research, education, and care. Act up. Fight back. Fight AIDS."

— Adapted from ACT UP (AIDS Coalition to Unleash Power) pamphlet, New York City, 1987

The ACT UP campaign described in the excerpt responded to which feature of the Reagan-era federal response to the AIDS epidemic?

  1. A federally funded universal vaccination program that activists felt was too invasive.
  2. Years of limited federal action and public silence from the administration, reflecting a broader conservative social agenda that stigmatized the communities most affected.
  3. The aggressive early approval of experimental antiretroviral drugs at the expense of safety testing.
  4. A federal mandate that all blood donations be tested for HIV starting in 1981.

Correct: Years of limited federal action and public silence, reflecting a broader conservative social agenda.

Reagan did not deliver a major public address on AIDS until 1987, years after the epidemic had been identified, and his administration resisted sex-education and needle-exchange measures favored by public-health officials. There was no universal vaccination program (none existed). The FDA was actually criticized for slow, not rushed, drug approval — which is exactly what ACT UP protested at the Food and Drug Administration in 1988. HIV testing of the blood supply only began in 1985, after activist pressure, and was not an early federal mandate in 1981.

The same Reagan-era domestic-policy shift also produced which of the following, widely criticized by later historians for creating a long-lasting racial disparity in incarceration?

  1. The decision to legalize recreational marijuana at the federal level in 1986.
  2. The Anti-Drug Abuse Act of 1986's 100-to-1 sentencing disparity between crack cocaine (more common in Black communities) and powder cocaine, part of an expanding "War on Drugs."
  3. The repeal of all federal mandatory minimum sentences.
  4. The ratification of the Twenty-Sixth Amendment lowering the voting age.

Correct: The Anti-Drug Abuse Act of 1986's 100-to-1 crack/powder sentencing disparity.

The 1986 statute treated possession of five grams of crack the same as 500 grams of powder cocaine, producing a severe racial disparity in federal drug sentencing that persisted until partial reform in 2010. Marijuana was not legalized federally; Nancy Reagan's "Just Say No" campaign reinforced the opposite. Mandatory minimums were expanded, not repealed. The 26th Amendment (1971) is from an earlier period and is unrelated to drug sentencing.

"General Secretary Gorbachev, if you seek peace, if you seek prosperity for the Soviet Union and Eastern Europe, if you seek liberalization: Come here to this gate. Mr. Gorbachev, open this gate. Mr. Gorbachev, tear down this wall!"

— Ronald Reagan, Brandenburg Gate speech, West Berlin, June 12, 1987

Reagan's speech came shortly before the signing of which arms-control agreement that marked a turning point in the late Cold War?

  1. SALT I, the first major limitation on strategic arms between the superpowers.
  2. The Intermediate-Range Nuclear Forces (INF) Treaty of December 1987, which eliminated an entire class of U.S. and Soviet missiles in Europe.
  3. The Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty, which opened for signature in 1968.
  4. The Kellogg-Briand Pact renouncing war as an instrument of national policy.

Correct: The INF Treaty of December 1987.

Negotiated after the Reykjavik summit (1986), the INF Treaty abolished land-based intermediate-range missiles — the first superpower agreement to eliminate rather than merely cap weapons. SALT I dates from 1972 (Nixon-Brezhnev) and limited strategic missile launchers. The Non-Proliferation Treaty predates Reagan by nearly two decades. Kellogg-Briand (1928) is from the interwar period and is irrelevant to late-Cold-War arms control.

Which of the following best describes the sequence of events that followed Reagan's Brandenburg Gate speech over the next four and a half years?

  1. Gorbachev ordered a crackdown on Eastern European protest movements, reaffirming the Brezhnev Doctrine.
  2. Solidarity-led elections in Poland, the fall of the Berlin Wall in November 1989, German reunification in 1990, and the dissolution of the Soviet Union in December 1991.
  3. A Warsaw Pact military invasion of West Germany that was repelled by NATO forces.
  4. The expansion of the Berlin Wall into a fortified border stretching to the North Sea.

Correct: Solidarity in Poland, fall of the Berlin Wall (1989), German reunification (1990), dissolution of the USSR (1991).

Gorbachev's "Sinatra Doctrine" abandoned the Brezhnev Doctrine and allowed Eastern Bloc regimes to fall, accelerating 1989's revolutions and leading to formal Soviet dissolution on December 26, 1991. A Warsaw Pact crackdown never materialized under Gorbachev. No Warsaw Pact invasion of West Germany took place. The Berlin Wall was not expanded — it was dismantled beginning the night of November 9, 1989.

In the same year the Berlin Wall fell, the Chinese government violently suppressed pro-democracy demonstrations at which location, a reminder that the collapse of Communism in Europe did not extend to East Asia?

  1. Tiananmen Square, Beijing (June 1989).
  2. Red Square, Moscow (June 1989).
  3. Gwangju, South Korea (June 1989).
  4. Hong Kong Central (June 1989).

Correct: Tiananmen Square, Beijing.

The People's Liberation Army crushed the student-led pro-democracy movement in Tiananmen Square on June 3–4, 1989. Red Square was the site of Soviet parades, not a 1989 crackdown. The Gwangju Uprising occurred in 1980, not 1989, and was in South Korea. Hong Kong saw solidarity protests but was not the site of the central 1989 crackdown.

The Iran-Contra affair, investigated in 1986–1987, is best understood as involving which combination of events?

  1. The CIA's overthrow of the Iranian prime minister Mossadegh and subsequent restoration of the Shah.
  2. Secret U.S. arms sales to Iran despite an embargo, with proceeds illegally diverted to fund the Nicaraguan Contras after Congress had prohibited such aid through the Boland Amendment.
  3. A Soviet plot to assassinate Reagan uncovered by Israeli intelligence.
  4. A joint U.S.-Iranian operation to overthrow Saddam Hussein during the Iran-Iraq War.

Correct: Arms-for-hostages sales to Iran with proceeds diverted to fund the Contras in violation of the Boland Amendment.

NSC staffer Oliver North and others arranged the diversion; several officials were indicted, though many convictions were later overturned or pardoned (by George H.W. Bush in 1992). The Mossadegh coup was in 1953, not the 1980s. No Soviet assassination plot was uncovered. The U.S. did tilt toward Iraq in the Iran-Iraq War at times, but not via a joint U.S.-Iranian operation against Saddam.

"Out of these troubled times… a new world order can emerge: a new era — freer from the threat of terror, stronger in the pursuit of justice, and more secure in the quest for peace… Iraq will not be permitted to annex Kuwait. That's not a threat, that's not a boast, that's just the way it's going to be."

— President George H.W. Bush, Address to a Joint Session of Congress, September 11, 1990

The U.S.-led military response to the crisis Bush described culminated in which of the following?

  1. Operation Desert Storm (January–February 1991), a coalition air and ground campaign that expelled Iraqi forces from Kuwait.
  2. A prolonged U.S. ground occupation of Baghdad that toppled Saddam Hussein.
  3. A NATO air war over Kosovo that forced Serbian withdrawal.
  4. A U.N. trusteeship that placed Kuwait under international administration for a decade.

Correct: Operation Desert Storm (January–February 1991).

The coalition liberated Kuwait in roughly 100 hours of ground combat after a month of air strikes. Bush 41 explicitly stopped short of marching on Baghdad or toppling Saddam, fearing "mission creep" — that would come with the 2003 Iraq War. The Kosovo air war (1999) was a later, different conflict. Kuwait was restored to its government, not placed under U.N. trusteeship.

Also during the Bush 41 presidency, Congress passed which landmark civil rights law extending nondiscrimination protections to a new category of Americans?

  1. The Civil Rights Act of 1991 eliminating all affirmative-action programs in federal hiring.
  2. The Americans with Disabilities Act (1990), prohibiting discrimination on the basis of disability in employment, public services, and accommodations.
  3. The Voting Rights Act of 1990, which added language minorities to federal oversight.
  4. The Immigration Reform and Control Act of 1990, granting amnesty to undocumented workers.

Correct: The Americans with Disabilities Act (1990).

The ADA, signed by Bush 41, is the most sweeping late-twentieth-century civil rights statute. The 1991 Civil Rights Act actually strengthened (not eliminated) protections after Supreme Court rulings. There was no "Voting Rights Act of 1990" of the form described. The major immigration amnesty was IRCA, signed in 1986 under Reagan, not 1990.

Which policy decision of the Clinton administration is most directly connected to the trade trends summarized in the table?

  1. Raising tariffs on Mexican and Canadian goods under the Smoot-Hawley framework.
  2. Ratifying the North American Free Trade Agreement (NAFTA) in 1993 and supporting the creation of the World Trade Organization in 1995.
  3. Withdrawing the United States from the General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade.
  4. Imposing capital controls to prevent U.S. investment abroad.

Correct: Ratifying NAFTA (1993) and supporting the WTO (1995).

NAFTA and the WTO were the Clinton-era institutional pillars of accelerated globalization, helping drive the trade figures shown. Smoot-Hawley is a 1930 protectionist law, not a Clinton-era policy. The United States did not withdraw from GATT — GATT was folded into the WTO. Clinton did not impose capital controls; the trend ran the other way.

The 1994 midterm elections — partly a backlash against Clinton's agenda — produced which result?

  1. A "Republican Revolution" led by Newt Gingrich and organized around a "Contract with America," giving Republicans control of the House for the first time since 1954.
  2. A Democratic supermajority that enabled the passage of Clinton's universal health-care plan.
  3. The election of Ross Perot's Reform Party to majorities in both chambers.
  4. A nonpartisan "national unity" Congress built on the Contract with the World.

Correct: A "Republican Revolution" led by Gingrich and the Contract with America.

Gingrich's Contract promised term limits, a balanced-budget amendment, welfare reform, and tax cuts; Republicans flipped both chambers. Clinton's health-care plan (led by Hillary Clinton) had already failed in 1994 — that was one of the reasons Democrats lost. Perot's party never won congressional majorities. There was no "national unity" Congress or "Contract with the World."

Which 1996 law, signed by Clinton, most clearly reflected the influence of the new Republican majority on domestic policy?

  1. The Affordable Care Act, guaranteeing a public option for health insurance.
  2. The Personal Responsibility and Work Opportunity Reconciliation Act (PRWORA), which ended AFDC as an open-ended federal entitlement and replaced it with TANF block grants and work requirements.
  3. The Family and Medical Leave Act, guaranteeing twelve weeks of paid family leave.
  4. The Glass-Steagall Act, separating commercial and investment banking.

Correct: PRWORA (1996), the welfare reform law.

PRWORA fulfilled Clinton's 1992 pledge to "end welfare as we know it" on terms largely set by congressional Republicans. The ACA passed in 2010, not 1996, and did not contain a public option. FMLA (1993) provided unpaid, not paid, leave. Glass-Steagall dates from 1933 — and in fact it was partially repealed under Clinton (Gramm-Leach-Bliley, 1999), not passed.

Which of the following statements about the culture and politics of Clinton's second term (1997–2001) is most accurate?

  1. The House impeached Clinton in December 1998 on perjury and obstruction charges arising from the Monica Lewinsky investigation, but the Senate acquitted him in February 1999.
  2. Clinton was removed from office by a Senate supermajority in 1999 and replaced by Vice President Gore.
  3. Clinton signed the Religious Freedom Restoration Act and declared same-sex marriage legal by executive order.
  4. Clinton's impeachment arose from his conduct of the Kosovo air war in 1999.

Correct: The House impeached Clinton in December 1998 and the Senate acquitted him in February 1999.

Clinton was only the second president to be impeached (after Andrew Johnson), charged with perjury and obstruction of justice. No Senate supermajority for removal emerged. Clinton signed the Defense of Marriage Act (1996), which defined marriage as between a man and a woman at the federal level — the opposite of legalizing same-sex marriage. The Kosovo air war was contemporaneous but not the basis for impeachment.

The immediate domestic legislative response to 9/11 included which of the following?

  1. The USA PATRIOT Act (October 2001), which expanded federal surveillance authority, and the creation of the Department of Homeland Security (2002).
  2. The repeal of FISA and the dismantling of federal intelligence agencies.
  3. The restoration of the military draft and mandatory national service for all Americans aged 18–26.
  4. The ratification of the Kyoto Protocol to combat terrorism through climate cooperation.

Correct: The USA PATRIOT Act and the creation of the Department of Homeland Security.

Both were central to the Bush-era "War on Terror" state. FISA was not repealed; it was expanded. No draft was restored — the wars were fought by an all-volunteer force. Bush in fact rejected the Kyoto Protocol, which is unrelated to terrorism.

Which 2003 decision by the Bush administration became its most controversial foreign-policy legacy, particularly after U.S. weapons inspectors found no active WMD program?

  1. The U.S. intervention in Kosovo to stop ethnic cleansing.
  2. The invasion of Iraq to topple Saddam Hussein, justified by claims of weapons of mass destruction and ties to al-Qaeda.
  3. The unilateral withdrawal of all U.S. forces from the Korean peninsula.
  4. The recognition of Palestinian statehood over Israeli objections.

Correct: The 2003 invasion of Iraq.

The WMD rationale collapsed by 2004; the subsequent insurgency, the Abu Ghraib torture scandal (2004), and the 2007 "surge" defined the conflict. Kosovo was a Clinton-era operation (1999). U.S. forces did not withdraw from Korea. The Bush administration supported a "two-state solution" rhetorically but did not recognize Palestinian statehood.

The disputed 2000 presidential election was ultimately resolved by which of the following?

  1. A House of Representatives contingent election under the Twelfth Amendment.
  2. The Supreme Court's decision in Bush v. Gore (December 2000), which halted the Florida recount and effectively awarded Florida — and the presidency — to George W. Bush.
  3. A statewide re-vote in Florida supervised by the United Nations.
  4. Al Gore's concession based on his popular-vote victory.

Correct: The Supreme Court's decision in Bush v. Gore (2000).

A 5–4 Court halted the Florida recount on Equal Protection grounds, letting Florida's earlier Bush-certified tally stand. No House contingent election occurred — that mechanism applies only when no candidate wins an Electoral College majority, which was not the case. No U.N. supervision or statewide re-vote took place. Gore did win the national popular vote, but that does not decide presidential elections; he conceded after the Court's ruling.

Hurricane Katrina (August 2005) is most often cited by historians for illustrating which of the following?

  1. Failures of federal, state, and local emergency response, and the disproportionate exposure of poor and Black residents of New Orleans to disaster.
  2. The success of FEMA and the Corps of Engineers in preventing levee breaches.
  3. The beginning of a sustained population boom in New Orleans.
  4. The first use of federal troops to enforce desegregation in a Southern city.

Correct: Failures of federal/state/local response and racialized exposure.

Levees failed, the Superdome became a symbol of chaos, and the slow FEMA response ("heckuva job, Brownie") damaged Bush 43's standing. The Corps/FEMA did not prevent breaches — the levees failed catastrophically. New Orleans lost roughly a third of its population after Katrina. Federal troops had first been used to enforce desegregation in Little Rock (1957) and Ole Miss (1962), decades earlier.

The grassroots "Tea Party" movement that emerged in 2009 was most directly a reaction to which of the following?

  2. The 2008 bank bailout, the 2009 stimulus, and the pending health-care reform, which activists framed as unconstitutional expansions of federal power.
  3. Obama's refusal to raise the federal minimum wage.
  4. The repeal of the Don't Ask, Don't Tell policy in 2011.

Correct: The bailouts, the stimulus, and pending health-care reform.

The Tea Party's rise (starting with Rick Santelli's February 2009 on-air "rant") fused anti-bailout sentiment with opposition to what became the ACA; it fueled the 2010 Republican midterm wave. Obama did not withdraw from NAFTA. Minimum-wage politics were not the Tea Party's core issue. DADT repeal came later and was supported, not opposed, by the broader left — not the Tea Party's driver.

The holding excerpted above did which of the following?

  1. Required every state to license and recognize same-sex marriages under the Fourteenth Amendment.
  2. Upheld the federal Defense of Marriage Act (DOMA) as a permissible exercise of congressional power.
  3. Permitted states to ban same-sex marriage if approved by voter referendum.
  4. Invalidated state antidiscrimination laws protecting LGBTQ employees.

Correct: Required every state to license and recognize same-sex marriages.

Obergefell held that the Fourteenth Amendment's Due Process and Equal Protection guarantees extend to same-sex couples. DOMA's key section had already been struck down in United States v. The Black Lives Matter movement, which coalesced after the 2012 killing of Trayvon Martin and grew after the 2014 events in Ferguson, Missouri, is best understood as part of which longer tradition?

SAQ 1 — The rise of the conservative movement. Using your knowledge of the period 1968–2000, answer (a), (b), and (c).

(a) Identify ONE specific economic cause of the rise of the modern conservative movement in the United States between 1968 and 1980.

(b) Explain ONE specific cultural or religious cause of the rise of the modern conservative movement in the same period.

(c) Explain ONE specific way in which the conservative movement transformed federal policy between 1981 and 2000.

Rubric

  • (a) 1 point for a specific economic cause with explanation: 1970s stagflation discrediting Keynesian liberalism; OPEC-era energy prices and deindustrialization in the Rust Belt; the anti-tax revolt (California Proposition 13, 1978); supply-side theorists (Laffer, Kemp); collapse of faith in Great Society spending.
  • (b) 1 point for a specific cultural/religious cause with explanation: the backlash against 1960s civil-rights and feminist movements; the mobilization of evangelicals against Roe v. Wade (1973); Jerry Falwell's Moral Majority (1979); Phyllis Schlafly's STOP ERA campaign; opposition to busing and affirmative action; Nixon's Southern Strategy.
  • (c) 1 point for a specific policy transformation with explanation: Economic Recovery Tax Act (1981) cutting marginal rates; deregulation (airlines, banking, S&Ls); defense buildup and SDI; the PATCO firings; Reagan-Bush Supreme Court appointments (O'Connor, Scalia, Rehnquist, Thomas); welfare reform (PRWORA 1996); Defense of Marriage Act (1996).

Model answer

(a) The stagflation of the 1970s — simultaneous high inflation and high unemployment after the 1973 OPEC embargo and the 1979 oil shock — discredited the Keynesian economic management that had underpinned Democratic liberalism since the New Deal. Supply-side economists like Arthur Laffer and Jack Kemp argued that high marginal tax rates were choking growth, and the California anti-tax revolt that produced Proposition 13 in 1978 demonstrated the electoral appeal of tax-cutting. Reagan in 1980 turned this economic critique into a national campaign.

(b) The 1970s saw the rapid mobilization of white evangelical Protestants into politics, especially after Roe v. Wade (1973) and IRS challenges to the tax-exempt status of segregated Christian schools. Jerry Falwell founded the Moral Majority in 1979 to organize these voters, and Phyllis Schlafly's STOP ERA campaign rallied conservative women against the Equal Rights Amendment. These activists fused with anti-busing parents, anti-abortion Catholics, and opponents of the gay rights movement into a "religious right" that became a core Republican constituency by 1980.

(c) Between 1981 and 2000 the conservative movement transformed federal tax and welfare policy. The Economic Recovery Tax Act of 1981 slashed top marginal rates and began a long-term shift away from the progressive income tax structure of the postwar era. Fifteen years later, Clinton's signature on the Personal Responsibility and Work Opportunity Reconciliation Act (1996) ended AFDC as an open-ended federal entitlement and replaced it with TANF block grants and work requirements — a direct victory for the conservative critique of the welfare state that Reagan had articulated in the early 1980s.

SAQ 2 — Economic transformation from deindustrialization to post-COVID. Using your knowledge of the period 1980–present, answer (a), (b), and (c).

(a) Identify ONE specific cause of American deindustrialization between 1980 and 2000.

(b) Explain ONE specific cause of the 2008 financial crisis.

(c) Explain ONE specific continuity OR change in federal economic policy from the 2008 crisis through the COVID-19 pandemic (2008–2022).

Rubric

  • (a) 1 point for a specific cause: expansion of global trade (NAFTA 1993, WTO / China accession 2001, the "China shock"); automation; decline in union bargaining power after PATCO; the strong dollar in the early 1980s; offshoring of supply chains.
  • (b) 1 point for a specific cause: subprime mortgage lending and the housing bubble; deregulation (Gramm-Leach-Bliley 1999); leverage and credit-default swaps; ratings-agency failures; the Lehman collapse (September 2008).
  • (c) 1 point for a specific continuity or change, well explained: continuity — willingness of federal government to deploy massive fiscal and monetary intervention (TARP 2008, ARRA 2009, CARES 2020, ARP 2021); change — turn toward industrial policy and climate investment (CHIPS and IRA, 2022) after decades of neoliberal consensus.

Model answer

(a) The expansion of global trade was a central cause. After NAFTA took effect in 1994 and China joined the World Trade Organization in 2001, U.S. manufacturers offshored production to lower-wage economies, and imports surged. U.S. manufacturing employment, roughly stable around 17 million through the 1990s, fell sharply after 2001, a phenomenon economists would later label the "China shock" and that hollowed out Rust Belt communities from Ohio to Michigan.

(b) The housing bubble and subprime mortgage lending were a central cause. Banks and non-bank originators issued mortgages to borrowers with weak credit, bundled them into securities, and sold them as investment-grade through complex structures that the ratings agencies validated. When housing prices peaked in 2006 and began to fall, the value of these securities collapsed; leveraged institutions like Bear Stearns and Lehman Brothers failed, freezing interbank credit and triggering a global recession.

(c) The clearest continuity is the federal government's willingness to deploy massive fiscal and monetary intervention in a crisis. TARP (2008) and ARRA (2009) committed hundreds of billions of dollars to stabilize banks, rescue the auto industry, and boost demand; a dozen years later the CARES Act (March 2020) and American Rescue Plan (2021) delivered trillions in direct payments, expanded unemployment insurance, and aid to state and local governments. Whereas Hoover had resisted comparable action in 1930–32, both Democratic and Republican administrations after 2008 treated large-scale intervention as the default response.

LEQ / Short DBQ — Demographic and cultural change since 1980 and political polarization. Using the documents below and your knowledge of the period, write a thesis-driven response to the following prompt:

Evaluate the extent to which demographic and cultural change since 1980 has contributed to political polarization in the United States.

Document 1. "A free society cannot survive without widely accepted standards of decency and morality… We must not, by omission or commission, allow our values to be ridiculed or the traditional family to be dismantled."
— Adapted from the Moral Majority program statement, 1980

Document 2. "The Hispanic share of the United States population rose from roughly 6.4% in 1980 to about 12.5% in 2000 to about 18.7% in 2020. The share of Americans identifying as non-Hispanic white declined from roughly 79% in 1980 to about 57% in 2020. The foreign-born share of the population rose from about 6% in 1980 to about 14% in 2020."
— Adapted from U.S. Census Bureau summary tables, 1980–2020

Document 3. "Same-sex couples are consigned to an instability many opposite-sex couples would deem intolerable… The Court now holds that same-sex couples may exercise the fundamental right to marry."
— Adapted from Justice Anthony Kennedy, Obergefell v. Hodges, 2015

Document 4. "Make America Great Again. We will build a great, great wall on our southern border. I will have Mexico pay for that wall."
— Adapted from Donald Trump, presidential announcement speech, June 2015

Document 5. "The Constitution does not confer a right to abortion; Roe and Casey are overruled; and the authority to regulate abortion is returned to the people and their elected representatives."
— Adapted from Justice Samuel Alito, majority opinion, Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization, 2022

Rubric (abbreviated, DBQ-style)

  • Thesis (1): A defensible argument that takes a position on how much demographic and cultural change drove polarization (e.g., "Demographic and cultural change significantly contributed to polarization, but they interacted with economic dislocation and elite polarization rather than acting alone").
  • Contextualization (1): Situate the period after the 1960s rights revolution, the 1965 Hart-Celler immigration overhaul, and the late-1970s rise of the New Right.
  • Document use (2): Use at least four documents accurately and connect them to the thesis.
  • Sourcing (1): For at least one document, discuss point of view, purpose, audience, or situation (e.g., Trump's speech is a campaign announcement designed to mobilize a specific demographic).
  • Evidence beyond the documents (1): Cite at least one specific fact not in the documents — e.g., Proposition 187 (1994), the 1994 Republican Revolution and Contract with America, the Tea Party (2009), Obergefell (2015), the 2016 election, Bostock (2020), January 6 (2021).
  • Complex understanding (1): Show complexity — e.g., that demographic change provoked both expanded civil rights and a counter-mobilization, or that polarization had elite-driven media and gerrymandering causes as well as cultural ones.

Model answer (condensed)

Demographic and cultural change since 1980 has been a major driver of American political polarization, but it has not acted alone. The country's transformation into a less white, less Christian, and more rights-based society produced dramatic policy gains for women, LGBTQ Americans, and immigrants — and, in turn, a counter-mobilization of religious conservatives, economic nationalists, and opponents of "identity politics" that reshaped the Republican Party and helped polarize the political system around cultural rather than class lines.

The Moral Majority statement in Document 1 captures the opening of this dynamic. Founded in 1979 as a response to Roe v. Wade (1973), the women's movement, and the gay rights movement after Stonewall (1969), the Moral Majority mobilized white evangelicals into Republican politics on cultural questions. That mobilization underwrote Reagan's 1980 victory and, over four decades, produced the Supreme Court majority that decided Dobbs in 2022 (Document 5), returning abortion to the states and triggering immediate bans across the South and Midwest.

The demographic data in Document 2 show why these cultural politics became so fraught. The Hispanic share of the population nearly tripled between 1980 and 2020, the non-Hispanic white share fell by more than twenty percentage points, and the foreign-born share more than doubled. California Proposition 187 (1994) and the 1996 IIRIRA were early attempts to restrict immigration; Trump's 2015 announcement speech (Document 4) shows how, a generation later, a presidential candidate could make a border wall and mass deportation the center of a major-party campaign. Document 4 is a campaign speech — its purpose is to mobilize a specific coalition of voters anxious about immigration and economic decline, and its "build a wall" line is designed for repetition at rallies rather than as policy text.

Obergefell (Document 3) shows the other side of the cultural story: rapid legal gains for LGBTQ Americans, supported by shifting public opinion among younger and more secular voters. Obergefell (2015), Bostock v. Clayton County (2020, extending Title VII to sexual orientation and gender identity), and the 2011 repeal of Don't Ask, Don't Tell were all products of — and further accelerants of — the generational cultural change reflected in Document 2. They also intensified conservative mobilization around religious-liberty exemptions and state-level restrictions.

Yet cultural change is not the whole story. Economic dislocation — deindustrialization after NAFTA (1993) and China's WTO accession (2001), and the Great Recession (2008–09) — hollowed out communities whose voters became central to the Trump coalition. Elite-driven factors (partisan media from Fox News in 1996 to social media after 2008, gerrymandered districts, congressional polarization tracked in roll-call data) amplified cultural sorting. The Tea Party's 2009 emergence fused economic and cultural grievance; the January 6, 2021 Capitol attack showed how deeply polarization had eroded shared commitment to electoral outcomes.

Demographic and cultural change, then, significantly contributed to polarization by giving each party a distinctive coalition — college-educated, younger, more diverse Democrats and older, white, more religiously conservative Republicans — and by making cultural questions (abortion, immigration, LGBTQ rights, race) the fault lines of national politics. But they did so in combination with economic and institutional forces that would have produced some polarization even without the cultural sorting of the post-1980 era.
