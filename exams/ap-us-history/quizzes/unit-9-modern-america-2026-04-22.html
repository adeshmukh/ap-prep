Unit 9 · 1980–present

Modern America: conservative resurgence to the post-2000 era

Reagan Revolution, the end of the Cold War, globalization and the 1990s boom, 9/11 and the War on Terror, the 2008 financial crisis, and the politics of a polarized, demographically shifting nation.

8 multiple choice 2 free response AP-level

Multiple choice

"In this present crisis, government is not the solution to our problem; government is the problem. From time to time we've been tempted to believe that society has become too complex to be managed by self-rule, that government by an elite group is superior to government for, by, and of the people. But if no one among us is capable of governing himself, then who among us has the capacity to govern someone else?"

— Adapted from Ronald Reagan, First Inaugural Address, January 1981

The rhetoric in this excerpt most directly supported which of the following domestic policy shifts during Reagan's first term?

  1. Expansion of the federal welfare state modeled on the Great Society
  2. Large marginal-income-tax cuts and deregulation of major industries
  3. Wage and price controls to suppress double-digit inflation
  4. A federal jobs guarantee for workers displaced by automation

Correct: Large marginal-income-tax cuts and deregulation of major industries.

Reagan paired his anti-government rhetoric with supply-side ("Kemp-Roth") tax cuts in 1981 and with deregulation across airlines, trucking, banking, and broadcasting. The Great Society answer contradicts the whole thrust of the speech. Wage and price controls had been Nixon's approach in 1971 and were explicitly repudiated by supply-siders. A federal jobs guarantee is a New Deal / progressive position, the opposite of Reagan's program.

Reagan's firing of more than 11,000 striking air-traffic controllers (PATCO) in August 1981 is best understood as a continuation of which trend implied by the excerpt?

  1. A bipartisan revival of New Deal labor protections extending into the public sector
  2. A shift away from the postwar labor-management accord toward a harder line against organized labor
  3. A Cold War policy of matching Soviet treatment of unions inside the Eastern Bloc
  4. A legal response to Taft-Hartley's prohibition on private-sector secondary boycotts

Correct: A shift away from the postwar labor-management accord toward a harder line against organized labor.

PATCO signaled that the federal government would no longer function as a neutral arbiter protecting union rights; private employers followed by hiring permanent replacements more aggressively, contributing to a long-term decline in private-sector union density. The answer citing a revival of New Deal labor protections is backwards. Cold War alignment with Soviet labor policy misreads Reagan's ideological frame. Taft-Hartley concerned secondary boycotts, not public-sector strikes like PATCO's.

"We welcome change and openness; for we believe that freedom and security go together, that the advance of human liberty can only strengthen the cause of world peace. General Secretary Gorbachev, if you seek peace, if you seek prosperity for the Soviet Union and Eastern Europe, if you seek liberalization: come here to this gate. Mr. Gorbachev, open this gate. Mr. Gorbachev, tear down this wall!"

— Adapted from Ronald Reagan, Brandenburg Gate address, June 1987

Which development most directly reflected the easing of superpower tension that Reagan invokes in this passage?

  1. The Helsinki Accords formally recognizing post-WWII European borders
  2. The Intermediate-Range Nuclear Forces (INF) Treaty signed later in 1987
  3. The Strategic Arms Limitation Treaty (SALT I) between Nixon and Brezhnev
  4. U.S. withdrawal from the Anti-Ballistic Missile Treaty under George W. Bush

Correct: The Intermediate-Range Nuclear Forces (INF) Treaty signed later in 1987.

The INF Treaty eliminated an entire class of ground-launched missiles and is the clearest concrete product of Reagan-Gorbachev diplomacy from the period of this speech. Helsinki (1975) predates Reagan entirely. SALT I (1972) was a Nixon-Brezhnev achievement and part of an earlier détente, not the late-1980s thaw. The 2001 ABM withdrawal is a post-Cold War development with opposite valence—it was unilateral and abrogating, not a cooperative easing.

"On the first day of the 104th Congress, the new Republican majority will immediately pass the following major reforms, aimed at restoring the faith and trust of the American people in their government: require all laws that apply to the rest of the country also apply to Congress; cut the number of House committees; implement a balanced budget amendment; and enact welfare reform to discourage illegitimacy and teen pregnancy by prohibiting welfare to minor mothers."

— Adapted from the Republican "Contract with America," 1994

The Contract with America is best understood as evidence of which continuity in late-twentieth-century U.S. politics?

  1. The consolidation of a conservative governing coalition that had been building since the Reagan years
  2. The collapse of the Republican Party's Southern strategy after the 1992 election
  3. A bipartisan embrace of centralized federal economic planning
  4. The dominance of organized labor over the Democratic congressional caucus

Correct: The consolidation of a conservative governing coalition that had been building since the Reagan years.

The Contract extended Reagan-era priorities—smaller federal footprint, tax and regulatory restraint, moral-traditionalist welfare framing—into a legislative program, culminating in the 1994 "Republican Revolution" and 1996 welfare reform (PRWORA). The Southern-strategy collapse answer is wrong: the South was in fact realigning more firmly Republican through the 1990s. Centralized federal planning is the opposite of the document's thesis. Labor dominance over Democrats misreads a period in which the Democratic Leadership Council was moving the party toward free trade and market-friendly reforms.

President Clinton's 1996 signing of the Personal Responsibility and Work Opportunity Reconciliation Act (PRWORA) is best interpreted as:

  1. A repudiation of the Contract with America's welfare priorities
  2. A triangulation strategy that absorbed key conservative welfare goals into Democratic policy
  3. A direct extension of Great Society entitlement expansion
  4. A response to a Supreme Court ruling striking down Aid to Families with Dependent Children

Correct: A triangulation strategy that absorbed key conservative welfare goals into Democratic policy.

Clinton explicitly pledged to "end welfare as we know it," and PRWORA replaced AFDC with the block-granted TANF program, imposed work requirements, and capped lifetime benefits—conservative aims enacted by a Democratic president. The "repudiation" answer inverts the relationship. The Great Society extension misreads a law that cut, rather than expanded, entitlement structure. No such Supreme Court ruling existed; AFDC was ended by statute, not judicial order.

"Our war on terror begins with al Qaeda, but it does not end there. It will not end until every terrorist group of global reach has been found, stopped, and defeated. Americans should not expect one battle, but a lengthy campaign, unlike any other we have ever seen. Every nation, in every region, now has a decision to make. Either you are with us, or you are with the terrorists."

— Adapted from George W. Bush, Address to a Joint Session of Congress, September 20, 2001

Which legislative or military action most directly reflects the doctrine articulated in this passage?

  1. The War Powers Resolution of 1973 limiting presidential deployment of forces
  2. The 2001 Authorization for Use of Military Force and the invasion of Afghanistan
  3. The Weinberger Doctrine requiring clearly defined, achievable objectives before committing troops
  4. The Boland Amendment restricting covert U.S. aid abroad

Correct: The 2001 Authorization for Use of Military Force and the invasion of Afghanistan.

The AUMF (Sept. 18, 2001) authorized force against those responsible for 9/11 and those harboring them—essentially the legal embodiment of "with us or with the terrorists"—and was immediately exercised in Afghanistan. The War Powers Resolution cuts against the executive latitude Bush is claiming. The Weinberger Doctrine's restraint criteria are in tension with an open-ended global war. The Boland Amendment concerned 1980s Contra aid, not post-9/11 operations.

Critics of the 2003 Iraq War most often cited which of the following to argue that the conflict exceeded the logic of the passage above?

  1. Iraq's formal membership in the Warsaw Pact at the time of the invasion
  2. The absence of verified weapons of mass destruction and of a demonstrated operational link between Iraq and al Qaeda
  3. A United Nations Security Council resolution explicitly forbidding regime change in Iraq
  4. A congressional refusal to authorize any use of force against Iraq

Correct: The absence of verified weapons of mass destruction and of a demonstrated operational link between Iraq and al Qaeda.

Postwar investigations (the Iraq Survey Group, the 9/11 Commission) found no WMD stockpiles and no operational al-Qaeda–Iraq partnership, which critics used to argue the war fell outside even a broad 9/11 rationale. The Warsaw Pact answer is factually wrong—the pact dissolved in 1991 and Iraq was never a member. No UN resolution explicitly forbade regime change (Resolution 1441 was ambiguous, not prohibitive). Congress did pass the 2002 Iraq AUMF, so the "refusal to authorize" answer is also factually wrong.

Which of the following best explains why many historians treat the 2008 financial crisis and Great Recession as a turning point in early-twenty-first century U.S. politics?

  1. It ended bipartisan support for financial deregulation that had defined policy since the New Deal
  2. It catalyzed populist movements on both the right (Tea Party) and left (Occupy Wall Street) that reshaped the two major parties
  3. It produced a constitutional amendment limiting executive compensation at publicly traded firms
  4. It marked the federal government's first major intervention in the banking system since Reconstruction

Correct: It catalyzed populist movements on both the right (Tea Party) and left (Occupy Wall Street) that reshaped the two major parties.

The Tea Party drove the 2010 GOP wave and pulled Republicans rightward on spending and immigration; Occupy popularized "the 1% vs. the 99%" framing that pushed Democrats to foreground inequality (ACA, Dodd-Frank enforcement, later Sanders/Warren campaigns). The deregulation answer is wrong: New Deal regulation (Glass-Steagall, SEC) was in place until the bipartisan deregulation of the 1980s–90s, so the era described is inverted. No such constitutional amendment exists. Federal intervention in banking was not new in 2008—compare the New Deal, the S&L bailout of the late 1980s, and the Federal Reserve's century-long existence.

Free response

SAQ. Answer (a), (b), and (c).

(a) Briefly describe ONE specific cause of the rise of the modern conservative movement in the United States between 1968 and 1988.
(b) Briefly explain ONE specific way that U.S. foreign policy between 1989 and 2001 reflected the end of the Cold War.
(c) Briefly explain ONE specific way that political or cultural polarization after 2000 represented a continuity with the conservative movement described in (a).

Rubric

  • (a) 1 pt for a specific cause — e.g., white backlash to civil-rights and Great Society expansion, Religious Right alignment (Moral Majority, 1979), Sun Belt suburban growth, stagflation eroding confidence in Keynesianism, or opposition to court-ordered busing/Roe v. Wade.
  • (b) 1 pt for a specific post–Cold War policy — e.g., NATO expansion into former Warsaw Pact states, NAFTA (1994) and WTO (1995) expanding economic engagement, the 1991 Gulf War as a "new world order" intervention, or humanitarian interventions in Somalia/Bosnia/Kosovo.
  • (c) 1 pt for a plausible continuity — e.g., the Religious Right's continued influence on same-sex marriage and abortion politics, Tea Party anti-tax/anti-government rhetoric echoing Reagan, hawkish national-security politics carrying into the War on Terror, or Southern/rural realignment hardening further after 2000.

Model answer

(a) The 1979 formation of the Moral Majority by Jerry Falwell mobilized evangelical Protestants into a Republican-aligned voting bloc that opposed Roe v. Wade, the Equal Rights Amendment, and secularization of public schools, providing organized grassroots energy behind Reagan's 1980 victory.

(b) The 1994 North American Free Trade Agreement lowered tariff barriers among the United States, Canada, and Mexico, reflecting a post–Cold War consensus that U.S. security interests would be advanced through expanded global markets rather than bloc-based containment—an orientation extended by U.S. support for the 1995 creation of the World Trade Organization.

(c) The Tea Party movement that emerged in 2009 recycled Reagan-era language about reducing taxes, slashing domestic spending, and distrusting federal authority, showing that the small-government ideology mobilized in (a) still structured Republican politics three decades later, even as the party's demographic coalition shifted.

DBQ-style short prompt. Evaluate the extent to which U.S. political and economic life between 1980 and 2020 was transformed by globalization. Use the three documents below plus your own outside knowledge. A strong response makes a defensible thesis, uses at least two documents as evidence, brings in at least one piece of outside evidence, and addresses contingency or counter-argument.

Document 1. "NAFTA means jobs. American jobs, and good-paying American jobs. If I didn't believe that, I wouldn't support this agreement. I believe that NAFTA will create a million jobs in the first five years of its impact."

— Adapted from President Bill Clinton, remarks at the NAFTA signing ceremony, December 1993

Document 2. "Between 1979 and 2019, real hourly wages for workers without a four-year college degree stagnated even as productivity rose sharply. Manufacturing employment fell from roughly 19 million to about 12 million, with especially steep losses in the Midwest. The top 1 percent's share of national income roughly doubled over the same period."

— Adapted from an economic history textbook, 2022

Document 3. "We will no longer surrender this country or its people to the false song of globalism. From this day forward, it's going to be only America first. Every decision on trade, on taxes, on immigration, on foreign affairs will be made to benefit American workers and American families."

— Adapted from Donald J. Trump, Inaugural Address, January 2017

Rubric (abbreviated College Board–style)

  • Thesis/Claim (1 pt): Defensible claim about the extent of transformation, responding to all parts of the prompt.
  • Contextualization (1 pt): Broader context — e.g., post-Cold War triumphalism, the information revolution, deindustrialization beginning in the 1970s.
  • Evidence from documents (up to 2 pts): Accurate use of at least 2 of the 3 documents to support the argument.
  • Outside evidence (1 pt): At least one specific fact beyond the documents (e.g., WTO 1995, China PNTR 2000, 2008 crisis, Tea Party, Rust Belt politics, outsourcing, H-1B visas).
  • Analysis/Complexity (1 pt): Sourcing analysis of at least one document (point of view, purpose, audience, or historical situation) OR a genuine counter-argument / qualification.

Model answer

Between 1980 and 2020, globalization transformed U.S. political and economic life to a substantial extent — reshaping the labor market, ideological coalitions, and even the language of presidential politics — but its effects were uneven and were ultimately politically contested rather than irreversible. The end of the Cold War in 1991 removed the security rationale for a bloc-based economic order, and policymakers in both parties embraced freer trade: NAFTA in 1994, the WTO in 1995, and permanent normal trade relations with China in 2000.

Document 1 shows this bipartisan enthusiasm: Clinton, a Democrat, defended NAFTA in explicitly pro-worker terms, arguing that integration with Mexico would generate "a million jobs." This claim is important for sourcing—Clinton is speaking at a signing ceremony designed to build political support, so the projection is optimistic and rhetorical rather than forecast-neutral. Document 2's long-run data qualify that optimism: manufacturing employment fell by roughly a third after 1979, non-college wages stagnated, and the top 1 percent's income share roughly doubled. The Rust Belt losses documented here were not a side effect but a central political fact; outside the documents, the 2008 financial crisis — itself rooted in globally mobile capital — intensified the sense that elite economic assumptions had failed ordinary Americans.

Document 3 captures the backlash: Trump's 2017 inaugural explicitly renounces "globalism," and his administration's tariffs on steel, aluminum, and Chinese imports, along with renegotiation of NAFTA as USMCA, marked the most serious policy reversal of the post-1980 consensus. The backlash was bipartisan in mood — Bernie Sanders ran on similar critiques of trade deals — suggesting that globalization's political legitimacy had eroded across ideological lines.

Still, transformation was not total. The United States remained deeply integrated in global finance and supply chains; the tech sector that drove post-1990s growth was itself a product of global talent flows and global markets. Thus globalization reshaped American life profoundly between 1980 and 2020, but by the end of the period it was being actively renegotiated rather than simply accepted — a continuity-and-change pattern consistent with earlier U.S. debates over tariffs, immigration, and the terms of integration with the world economy.
