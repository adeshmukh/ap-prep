Multiple choice

"There was never any clean-up of the meat because there was no place to clean it. The packers used everything of the hog except the squeal. Men would go to work in rooms where the stench was so overpowering that a visitor could not stay five minutes; the carcasses were dragged out to be cut up by men who had just come from handling tubercular beef. The government inspectors, stationed at the plants to satisfy foreign buyers, paid no attention to what was sold at home."

— Adapted from Upton Sinclair, The Jungle, 1906

The exposé above most directly contributed to the passage of which pair of federal laws in the same year it was published?

  1. The Sherman Antitrust Act and the Interstate Commerce Act
  2. The Pure Food and Drug Act and the Meat Inspection Act
  3. The Clayton Antitrust Act and the Federal Reserve Act
  4. The Underwood Tariff and the Federal Trade Commission Act

Correct: The Pure Food and Drug Act and the Meat Inspection Act.

Sinclair's 1906 novel, though intended to expose the exploitation of immigrant laborers, famously prompted Theodore Roosevelt to push through consumer-safety legislation that same year. The Sherman (1890) and ICC (1887) laws predate the novel. The Clayton Act, Federal Reserve Act, Underwood Tariff, and FTC were Wilson-era reforms from 1913–1914.

Sinclair, Ida Tarbell, and Jacob Riis are most commonly grouped together as part of which broader phenomenon?

  1. Social Darwinists defending laissez-faire capitalism
  2. Populist agrarian reformers advocating free silver
  3. Muckraking journalists whose investigative reporting fueled Progressive reform
  4. Harlem Renaissance writers documenting Black cultural life

Correct: Muckraking journalists whose investigative reporting fueled Progressive reform.

Tarbell exposed Standard Oil, Riis documented tenement poverty in How the Other Half Lives, and Sinclair targeted meatpacking. The Social Darwinist answer is the opposite of their position. The Populist movement peaked in the 1890s and focused on monetary policy. Harlem Renaissance writers belong to the 1920s and addressed a very different subject.

Which constitutional amendment ratified during the Progressive Era is most closely associated with the movement's goal of making government more directly responsive to voters?

  1. The Sixteenth Amendment, authorizing a federal income tax
  2. The Seventeenth Amendment, providing direct election of U.S. senators
  3. The Eighteenth Amendment, establishing Prohibition
  4. The Nineteenth Amendment, granting women the right to vote

Correct: The Seventeenth Amendment, providing direct election of U.S. senators.

The prompt emphasizes "direct responsiveness to voters," which points to the Seventeenth Amendment (1913) that replaced appointment by state legislatures with popular election. The income-tax amendment expanded federal revenue but did not change who picked officials. Prohibition regulated private behavior. The women's suffrage amendment expanded the electorate but is better described as expanding who could vote, not as changing how an office was filled — though on the real exam this is a defensible second choice, direct-election of senators is the textbook answer here.

"The world must be made safe for democracy. Its peace must be planted upon the tested foundations of political liberty. We have no selfish ends to serve. We desire no conquest, no dominion. We seek no indemnities for ourselves, no material compensation for the sacrifices we shall freely make. We are but one of the champions of the rights of mankind."

— Adapted from Woodrow Wilson, War Message to Congress, April 1917

Wilson's immediate justification for the shift from neutrality to war in April 1917 most directly rested on which combination of events?

  1. Germany's resumption of unrestricted submarine warfare and the interception of the Zimmermann Telegram
  2. The sinking of the Maine in Havana harbor and yellow journalism in the Hearst press
  3. The Japanese attack on Pearl Harbor and Germany's subsequent declaration of war on the United States
  4. The Russian Revolution and the collapse of the Eastern Front

Correct: Germany's resumption of unrestricted submarine warfare and the interception of the Zimmermann Telegram.

In early 1917 Germany resumed sinking neutral merchant ships and was caught proposing a military alliance with Mexico if the United States entered the war. The Maine and yellow journalism answer belongs to the Spanish-American War (1898). Pearl Harbor is 1941. The Russian Revolution affected the Eastern Front but was not Wilson's stated cause for U.S. entry.

The idealism expressed in the excerpt was most fully articulated in which postwar proposal — and how did the United States ultimately respond to it?

  1. Wilson's Fourteen Points and the League of Nations, which the U.S. Senate rejected out of concern for entanglement in collective security obligations
  2. The Atlantic Charter, which the Senate ratified as the framework for the postwar United Nations
  3. The Open Door Notes, which the Senate approved by a two-thirds vote to protect U.S. trade in China
  4. The Dawes Plan, which Congress adopted to restructure European reparations

Correct: Wilson's Fourteen Points and the League of Nations, which the U.S. Senate rejected out of concern for entanglement in collective security obligations.

Wilson's Fourteen Points framed a liberal internationalist peace; the centerpiece — the League — was defeated in the Senate led by Henry Cabot Lodge's reservationists and "irreconcilables" who feared Article X would draw the U.S. into future wars. The Atlantic Charter is a 1941 Roosevelt-Churchill document. The Open Door Notes were executive declarations by Hay (1899–1900), not treaties. The Dawes Plan (1924) dealt with German reparations payments but was not Wilson's postwar blueprint.

"I can assure you that it is safer to keep your money in a reopened bank than under the mattress. Confidence and courage are the essentials of success in carrying out our plan. You people must have faith; you must not be stampeded by rumors or guesses. Let us unite in banishing fear. Together we cannot fail."

— Adapted from Franklin D. Roosevelt, First Fireside Chat, March 1933

Which early New Deal measure is most directly reflected in the passage above?

  1. The Social Security Act, which created old-age pensions funded by payroll taxes
  2. The Emergency Banking Act and the creation of the FDIC to restore trust in the banking system
  3. The Wagner Act, which guaranteed the right of workers to organize and bargain collectively
  4. The Agricultural Adjustment Act, which paid farmers to reduce production

Correct: The Emergency Banking Act and the creation of the FDIC to restore trust in the banking system.

The fireside chat accompanied the Hundred Days reopening of solvent banks under federal oversight; the FDIC followed under the Glass-Steagall Act. Social Security (1935) addresses pensions, not bank runs. Wagner (1935) deals with labor, and the AAA deals with farm production — neither addresses the message about deposit safety in the excerpt.

Critics of the later New Deal from both the left and the right included figures such as Huey Long and Father Charles Coughlin. Their criticisms shared which underlying theme?

  1. That the New Deal had violated the gold standard and caused hyperinflation
  2. That the New Deal had either not gone far enough in redistributing wealth or had concentrated too much power in the federal executive
  3. That the New Deal had abandoned isolationism and entangled the United States in European affairs
  4. That the New Deal had dismantled Jim Crow and overreached on civil rights

Correct: That the New Deal had either not gone far enough in redistributing wealth or had concentrated too much power in the federal executive.

Long's "Share Our Wealth" argued FDR was too timid in redistribution; Coughlin's radio broadcasts turned sharply against FDR as a tool of bankers and later became overtly authoritarian. Roosevelt's 1937 court-packing plan further energized charges of executive overreach. Hyperinflation was not a defining Depression-era problem. Foreign policy and civil rights were not central to the Long/Coughlin critiques of the New Deal itself.

In Korematsu v. United States (1944), the Supreme Court upheld Executive Order 9066, which authorized the forced relocation and internment of approximately 120,000 people of Japanese ancestry, most of them U.S. citizens. Which of the following best represents the Court's majority reasoning?

  1. Japanese Americans had forfeited their citizenship by maintaining cultural ties to Japan.
  2. Military necessity in wartime could justify a race-based restriction on civil liberties despite strict scrutiny of such classifications.
  3. The Fourteenth Amendment's equal protection clause did not apply to federal actions, only to the states.
  4. Congress had declared martial law on the West Coast, suspending the writ of habeas corpus.

Correct: Military necessity in wartime could justify a race-based restriction on civil liberties despite strict scrutiny of such classifications.

Justice Black's majority opinion conceded that racial classifications were "immediately suspect" but accepted the government's claim of military necessity after Pearl Harbor. The Court did not strip citizenship or hold that equal protection was inapplicable to the federal government. No general martial law or habeas suspension applied on the West Coast; the relocation rested on EO 9066 and congressional enforcement legislation. The ruling has since been widely repudiated, and the federal government issued an official apology and reparations through the Civil Liberties Act of 1988.

Free response

SAQ. Answer parts (a), (b), and (c).

(a) Briefly describe ONE specific way in which U.S. foreign policy between 1890 and 1917 reflected a turn toward overseas imperialism.

(b) Briefly explain ONE specific way in which U.S. involvement in World War I (1917–1918) affected life on the home front.

(c) Briefly explain ONE specific way in which debates over the proper role of the federal government during the New Deal marked a continuity with or departure from Progressive-Era reform.

Rubric

  • (a) 1 point — identifies and briefly describes a concrete example of overseas imperial expansion (e.g., the annexation of Hawaii in 1898, acquisition of the Philippines and Puerto Rico after the Spanish-American War, Open Door notes in China, the Roosevelt Corollary, "big stick" diplomacy in the Caribbean, construction of the Panama Canal).
  • (b) 1 point — identifies and briefly explains a specific home front effect (e.g., Espionage Act (1917) and Sedition Act (1918) restricting dissent; the Great Migration of African Americans into Northern industrial cities; women entering wartime industry; Committee on Public Information propaganda; anti- German hysteria; Liberty Bonds and War Industries Board economic mobilization).
  • (c) 1 point — identifies a specific continuity OR departure and explains it (e.g., continuity: Progressives had already legitimized federal regulation of industry — Meat Inspection, Pure Food and Drug, FTC, Federal Reserve — and the New Deal extended that logic through NRA codes and securities regulation; departure: the New Deal's welfare state — Social Security, Wagner Act, direct federal employment through CCC/WPA — went beyond Progressive regulation into permanent federal responsibility for individual economic security).

Model answer

(a) Following the Spanish-American War of 1898, the United States annexed the Philippines, Puerto Rico, and Guam under the Treaty of Paris. This marked a decisive turn toward overseas imperialism, since the federal government acquired non-contiguous territories populated by people to whom it refused to extend full citizenship, and it fought a brutal counterinsurgency (the Philippine-American War, 1899–1902) to hold the Philippines against Filipino independence forces under Aguinaldo.

(b) The war drastically reshaped labor and migration on the home front. As white workers were drafted and European immigration collapsed, Northern factories actively recruited African American workers, triggering the first phase of the Great Migration. More than 500,000 Black Americans moved from the rural South to cities like Chicago, Detroit, and New York during and after the war, transforming Northern demography, politics, and culture, while also sparking race riots such as the 1919 "Red Summer."

(c) The New Deal represented both continuity and departure. Progressives like Theodore Roosevelt and Woodrow Wilson had already established that the federal government could regulate industry, tax incomes (16th Amendment), and create permanent administrative agencies (FTC, Federal Reserve), so the NRA and SEC extended a familiar regulatory logic. However, the New Deal departed sharply from the Progressive model by committing the federal government to direct relief and guaranteed social insurance: Social Security created a permanent old-age pension, the Wagner Act federalized labor relations, and the WPA and CCC made Washington the nation's employer of last resort — roles no Progressive reformer had seriously proposed.

Short DBQ-style prompt. Using the two excerpts below and your knowledge of U.S. history, evaluate the extent to which World War II represented a turning point in the economic and social status of African Americans and women in the United States.

"We, as colored Americans, are determined to protect our country, our form of government, and the freedoms which we cherish for ourselves and for the rest of the world. We adopt the Double V for a double victory: the first V for victory over our enemies from without; the second V for victory over our enemies from within. For surely those who perpetrate these ugly prejudices here are seeking to destroy our democratic form of government just as surely as the Axis forces."

— Adapted from James G. Thompson, letter to the Pittsburgh Courier, 1942

"All the day long, whether rain or shine, she's a part of the assembly line. She's making history, working for victory — Rosie the Riveter. Keeps a sharp lookout for sabotage, sitting up there on the fuselage. That little girl will do more than a male will do. Rosie is protecting America, doing her bit for the USA."

— Adapted from Redd Evans and John Jacob Loeb, "Rosie the Riveter," song lyric, 1942

Rubric (simplified DBQ-style)

  • Thesis (1 pt) — Historically defensible claim that evaluates the extent of change. A strong answer argues that WWII was a significant but limited turning point: it produced major economic openings for African Americans and women but left structural inequalities largely intact.
  • Contextualization (1 pt) — Situates WWII within longer trends: Great Migration, New Deal labor reforms, the NAACP's legal strategy, prewar women's limited entry into professions, and the Great Depression's impact on women's employment.
  • Document use (2 pts) — Uses both excerpts to support the argument: Document 1 shows that Black Americans linked victory abroad to civil rights at home; Document 2 shows mobilization of women into industrial jobs previously reserved for men.
  • Evidence beyond the documents (1 pt) — Cites specific outside evidence, e.g., A. Philip Randolph's threatened March on Washington (1941) and FDR's Executive Order 8802 creating the Fair Employment Practices Committee; women holding roughly 36% of the civilian labor force by 1945; the desegregation of the armed forces under Truman's EO 9981 (1948); Smith v. Allwright (1944) striking down the white primary; postwar rollback of women's industrial employment as veterans returned.
  • Analysis / complexity (1–2 pts) — Acknowledges counterevidence: continued segregation in the military until 1948, Detroit race riot of 1943, internment of Japanese Americans, the gendered postwar push back into domesticity, and unequal pay that persisted.

Model thesis and outline

Thesis. World War II was a real but incomplete turning point for African Americans and women: wartime labor demands opened industrial and federal jobs long denied to both groups and strengthened the moral case against segregation, yet Jim Crow, unequal pay, and the postwar expectation that women would return to the home meant that the structural transformation waited until the civil rights and feminist movements of the 1950s–1970s.

Body 1 — Genuine turning point. The Double V campaign (Doc 1) reframed the war as a struggle for democracy at home as well as abroad. Under pressure from A. Philip Randolph's threatened 1941 March on Washington, FDR issued EO 8802 banning discrimination in defense industries and creating the FEPC. The Great Migration accelerated; Black membership in the NAACP soared; Smith v. Allwright (1944) struck down the white primary. Rosie the Riveter (Doc 2) symbolized roughly six million women entering the workforce, many in heavy industry, changing cultural expectations of female competence.

Body 2 — Limits of change. The armed forces remained segregated until Truman's EO 9981 in 1948. The 1943 Detroit race riot and the simultaneous internment of Japanese Americans under Korematsu showed the persistence of racial hierarchy. When veterans returned, women were pushed out of industrial jobs; the GI Bill's benefits flowed overwhelmingly to white men. Wages for Black workers and women remained below white male wages.

Conclusion. WWII did not end racial or gender inequality, but it fatally weakened the ideological defenses of both. The contradictions it exposed — fighting fascism abroad while tolerating Jim Crow and excluding women from postwar prosperity — set the moral and political stage for the civil rights and women's movements of the second half of the century.
