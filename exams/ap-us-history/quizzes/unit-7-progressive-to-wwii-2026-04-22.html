Multiple choice

"God has not been preparing the English-speaking and Teutonic peoples for a thousand years for nothing but vain and idle self-contemplation… He has made us the master organizers of the world to establish system where chaos reigns… And of all our race He has marked the American people as His chosen nation to finally lead in the regeneration of the world. This is the divine mission of America."

— Senator Albert J. Beveridge, speech on the Philippines, U.S. Senate, 1900

"We have pacified some thousands of the islanders and buried them; destroyed their fields; burned their villages… subjugated the remaining ten millions by Benevolent Assimilation… And so, by these Providences of God — and the phrase is the government's, not mine — we are a World Power."

— Mark Twain, "To the Person Sitting in Darkness," North American Review, 1901

Twain's sarcasm in the second excerpt most directly supports which of the following organizations or causes?

  1. The Republican Party's platform in the election of 1900.
  2. The Anti-Imperialist League, which opposed U.S. annexation of the Philippines and the conduct of the Philippine-American War.
  3. The Committee on Public Information (CPI) under George Creel.
  4. The Roosevelt Corollary to the Monroe Doctrine.

Correct: The Anti-Imperialist League.

Twain, along with Andrew Carnegie, William Jennings Bryan, Jane Addams, and others, was a leading voice of the Anti-Imperialist League, which denounced the conquest of the Philippines and the brutal counterinsurgency against Aguinaldo's forces. The Republican Party of 1900 ran on McKinley's imperial victory, the opposite of Twain's view. The CPI is a World War I propaganda agency (1917–19). The Roosevelt Corollary (1904) asserted U.S. "police power" in the Caribbean — the kind of intervention Twain mocks.

Beveridge's argument most directly reflects which late-nineteenth-century ideological current?

  1. The Social Gospel's call to redistribute wealth to the urban poor at home.
  2. A fusion of Anglo-Saxonism, racial Darwinism, and Protestant providential destiny that justified overseas empire.
  3. Jeffersonian anti-imperialism rooted in the Monroe Doctrine.
  4. The Populist demand for free silver and agrarian reform.

Correct: Anglo-Saxonism, racial Darwinism, and providential destiny.

Beveridge's "English-speaking and Teutonic peoples," "master organizers," and "divine mission" rhetoric is the classic imperialist synthesis of Josiah Strong, Alfred Thayer Mahan, and Kipling's "White Man's Burden." The Social Gospel targeted domestic poverty; the Monroe Doctrine was anti-European rather than anti-empire; Populism addressed currency and agriculture.

Which of the following resulted most directly from the war referenced by both Beveridge and Twain?

  1. The annexation of Hawaii by joint congressional resolution.
  2. U.S. acquisition of the Philippines, Puerto Rico, and Guam, and a Platt-Amendment protectorate over Cuba.
  3. The opening of Japan to U.S. trade under Commodore Perry's treaty.
  4. The establishment of the League of Nations as an instrument of U.S. foreign policy.

Correct: Acquisition of the Philippines, Puerto Rico, and Guam, plus the Platt Amendment over Cuba.

The 1898 Treaty of Paris transferred Spain's remaining overseas empire to the United States, and the Platt Amendment (1901) made Cuba a de facto U.S. protectorate despite the Teller Amendment's earlier disclaimer. Hawaii was annexed by joint resolution in July 1898 as a separate process, not as a result of the war. Perry's Japan treaty was 1854. The League of Nations is post-WWI and the Senate rejected it.

"Chronic wrongdoing, or an impotence which results in a general loosening of the ties of civilized society, may in America, as elsewhere, ultimately require intervention by some civilized nation, and in the Western Hemisphere the adherence of the United States to the Monroe Doctrine may force the United States, however reluctantly, in flagrant cases of such wrongdoing or impotence, to the exercise of an international police power."

— President Theodore Roosevelt, Annual Message to Congress, December 1904

Roosevelt's statement above is most directly known as which of the following, and it most directly justified which kind of action?

  1. The Open Door Notes — equal commercial access in China.
  2. The Roosevelt Corollary to the Monroe Doctrine — U.S. intervention in Caribbean and Central American states, as when the U.S. took over Dominican customs collections (1905) and backed Panamanian independence from Colombia (1903).
  3. Dollar Diplomacy — substituting "dollars for bullets" in China and Central America.
  4. The Good Neighbor Policy — FDR's pledge to renounce overt Caribbean intervention.

Correct: The Roosevelt Corollary — intervention in the Dominican Republic and support for Panama.

Roosevelt's 1904 message extended the Monroe Doctrine by asserting a U.S. "police power" in the hemisphere; it legitimized the 1905 Dominican customs receivership and was consistent with his 1903 backing of Panamanian independence to get the canal zone. The Open Door Notes concerned China. Dollar Diplomacy is Taft. The Good Neighbor Policy is FDR's later repudiation of exactly this approach.

Wilson's "moral diplomacy" differed from Roosevelt's approach chiefly in that Wilson

  1. Withdrew all U.S. troops from the Caribbean and ended intervention for the duration of his presidency.
  2. Framed U.S. intervention in moral and democratic terms while still sending troops repeatedly — as with the occupation of Veracruz (1914) and the Pershing expedition into Mexico against Pancho Villa (1916).
  3. Sought to acquire Mexican territory from Baja California to the Yucatán.
  4. Consistently refused to intervene anywhere in Latin America.

Correct: Moral/democratic rhetoric combined with actual repeated intervention in Mexico.

Wilson rhetorically rejected Roosevelt's and Taft's approaches but sent marines into Haiti, the Dominican Republic, and Mexico; he occupied Veracruz in 1914 and sent Pershing in 1916 to chase Villa. He did not withdraw from the Caribbean, did not seek Mexican territorial annexation, and did intervene.

"There were men who worked in the cooking-rooms… with aprons that once were white but had long since become filthy with blood and grease. It was the custom, whenever a hog died, to throw it into the vats — and so they would make the lard, and the canned hams, and the sausage."

— Upton Sinclair, The Jungle, 1906

— Ida M. Tarbell, The History of the Standard Oil Company, 1904

Sinclair's novel most directly contributed to the passage of which pair of federal laws?

  1. The Sherman Antitrust Act (1890) and the Interstate Commerce Act (1887).
  2. The Pure Food and Drug Act and the Meat Inspection Act, both passed in 1906.
  3. The Social Security Act (1935) and Fair Labor Standards Act (1938).
  4. The Federal Reserve Act (1913) and Clayton Antitrust Act (1914).

Correct: The Pure Food and Drug Act and the Meat Inspection Act of 1906.

Public disgust over Sinclair's packinghouse descriptions pushed TR and Congress to pass both 1906 statutes. Sherman and the ICA predate Sinclair. Social Security and FLSA address old-age insurance and wages/hours. The Federal Reserve and Clayton Acts are Wilson-era financial and antitrust reforms — not food safety.

Tarbell's investigation contributed most directly to which of the following outcomes?

  1. The nationalization of the petroleum industry under federal ownership.
  2. The 1911 Supreme Court decision ordering the breakup of Standard Oil under the Sherman Antitrust Act.
  3. The creation of the Securities and Exchange Commission in 1934.
  4. The repeal of the Interstate Commerce Act.

Correct: The 1911 Supreme Court breakup of Standard Oil.

Tarbell's 19-part serial in McClure's built the case that culminated in Standard Oil v. United States (1911), dissolving the trust into 34 companies. The U.S. did not nationalize oil. The SEC is a New Deal agency. The ICC was actually strengthened (Hepburn Act, 1906) in this period.

Sinclair, Tarbell, and Lincoln Steffens (The Shame of the Cities) are most often grouped under which label?

  1. The Lost Generation.
  2. Muckrakers — investigative journalists who exposed corporate abuses, political corruption, and social ills in mass-circulation magazines such as McClure's.
  3. The Mugwumps.
  4. The Fireside Chat authors.

Correct: Muckrakers.

Theodore Roosevelt popularized the term — borrowed from Bunyan — for journalists who raked through civic filth. The Lost Generation is 1920s expatriate writers. Mugwumps were 1880s civil-service reformers. Fireside chats are FDR's 1930s radio addresses.

"The man who wrongly holds that every human right is secondary to his profit must now give way to the advocate of human welfare, who rightly maintains that every man holds his property subject to the general right of the community to regulate its use to whatever degree the public welfare may require it… I stand for the square deal."

— Theodore Roosevelt, "The New Nationalism" speech, Osawatomie, Kansas, 1910

Roosevelt's "New Nationalism" vision is best characterized by which of the following?

  1. A return to laissez-faire economics and the gold standard.
  2. An activist federal government regulating large corporations in the public interest rather than trying to break them all up.
  3. A commitment to states' rights and narrow construction of the Constitution.
  4. A program of rapid deflation to aid creditors and bondholders.

Correct: An activist federal government regulating large corporations.

Roosevelt accepted concentrated industry as efficient but insisted the federal government must police it ("good" vs. "bad" trusts), consistent with his 1902 coal-strike arbitration, Northern Securities suit, Hepburn Act, and conservation work with Gifford Pinchot. Laissez-faire is what Roosevelt rejected. States' rights fits Wilson's "New Freedom" better. Neither candidate proposed deflation.

In the four-way election of 1912, the candidate who ran under the Progressive ("Bull Moose") banner against Wilson, Taft, and Debs was

  1. Theodore Roosevelt, who bolted the Republican Party after Taft won its nomination.
  2. Robert La Follette, leader of the Wisconsin Idea.
  3. William Jennings Bryan, running a fourth campaign for the presidency.
  4. Warren G. Harding, promising a "return to normalcy."

Correct: Theodore Roosevelt.

After Taft's renomination, TR formed the Progressive ("Bull Moose") Party, won 27% of the popular vote, and split the Republican base so Wilson could win with 42%. Eugene Debs ran as a Socialist that year, winning roughly 6%. La Follette lost the Republican contest before the split. Bryan ran his last campaign in 1908. Harding's "normalcy" slogan is from 1920.

Which of the following was a Wilsonian "New Freedom" achievement of Wilson's first term?

  1. The Federal Reserve Act (1913), the Clayton Antitrust Act (1914), and the Federal Trade Commission (1914).
  2. The Social Security Act and the Works Progress Administration.
  3. The Pendleton Civil Service Act and the Sherman Silver Purchase Act.
  4. The Wagner Act and the Fair Labor Standards Act.

Correct: The Federal Reserve Act, the Clayton Antitrust Act, and the FTC.

All three are cornerstones of Wilson's first-term "New Freedom" domestic program. Social Security and WPA are New Deal (1935). Pendleton (1883) and the Sherman Silver Purchase Act (1890) are Gilded Age. Wagner and FLSA are Second New Deal labor laws.

One carries a banner reading, "Mr. President, How Long Must Women Wait for Liberty?" A row of police officers looks on. The caption reads: "The Silent Sentinels."

— Adapted from The Suffragist, 1917

The "Silent Sentinels" pictured above are most closely associated with which leader and strategy?

  1. Jane Addams, tying suffrage to settlement-house "municipal housekeeping."
  2. Alice Paul and the National Woman's Party, using confrontational White House pickets to demand a federal suffrage amendment.
  3. Carrie Chapman Catt and NAWSA, focusing exclusively on state-by-state referenda.
  4. Ida B. Wells-Barnett, leading an anti-lynching campaign that rejected federal reform.

Correct: Alice Paul and the National Woman's Party.

Paul's NWP staged the Silent Sentinel pickets (1917–19); many were arrested and force-fed in prison, generating publicity that pressured Wilson toward a federal amendment. Addams led Hull House. Catt's "Winning Plan" combined state campaigns with a federal amendment push, not state-only. Wells-Barnett's primary cause was anti-lynching.

Which list correctly matches each Progressive Era amendment to its subject?

  1. 16th — federal income tax; 17th — direct election of senators; 18th — Prohibition of alcohol; 19th — woman suffrage.
  2. 16th — Prohibition; 17th — woman suffrage; 18th — income tax; 19th — direct election of senators.
  3. 16th — abolition of slavery; 17th — birthright citizenship; 18th — Black male suffrage; 19th — Prohibition.
  4. 16th — equal protection; 17th — poll taxes abolished; 18th — presidential term limits; 19th — income tax.

Correct: 16th income tax; 17th direct election of senators; 18th Prohibition; 19th woman suffrage.

These four Progressive amendments show up regularly on the exam and must be memorized. The Reconstruction amendments (13, 14, 15) concern slavery, citizenship/equal protection, and Black male suffrage — different era. Poll taxes were abolished by the 24th Amendment (1964); presidential term limits are the 22nd (1951).

Which of the following best describes W.E.B. Du Bois's position relative to Booker T. Washington in the early 1900s?

  1. Du Bois accepted Washington's Atlanta Compromise and urged Black Americans to postpone political rights for vocational training.
  2. Du Bois rejected gradualism, launched the Niagara Movement (1905), and helped found the NAACP (1909) to press for immediate civil and political rights.
  3. Du Bois embraced Marcus Garvey's Back-to-Africa plan and renounced U.S. citizenship.
  4. Du Bois led a farmers' alliance focused on silver coinage and railroad regulation.

Correct: Du Bois rejected gradualism and helped found the Niagara Movement and NAACP.

Du Bois and the "Talented Tenth" pressed for immediate civil and political equality, against both Washington's gradualism and Garvey's separatism. The farmers' alliance describes 1890s Populism, not Du Bois.

"We intend to begin unrestricted submarine warfare on the first of February… In the event of this not succeeding, we make Mexico a proposal of alliance on the following basis: Make war together, make peace together, generous financial support, and an understanding on our part that Mexico is to reconquer the lost territory in Texas, New Mexico, and Arizona."

— Arthur Zimmermann, German Foreign Secretary, to the German Minister in Mexico, January 1917 (intercepted by British intelligence)

The publication of the telegram above, combined with Germany's February 1917 resumption of unrestricted submarine warfare, most directly contributed to which of the following?

  1. Wilson's decision to maintain neutrality throughout the war.
  2. Wilson's April 1917 request that Congress declare war on Germany to make "the world safe for democracy."
  3. The passage of the Neutrality Acts of the mid-1930s.
  4. Mexico's entry into the war on the side of the Central Powers.

Correct: Wilson's April 1917 war request.

Together with the sinking of American merchant ships, the Zimmermann Telegram made neutrality politically untenable. The Neutrality Acts were a 1930s response to a different war. Mexico, under Carranza, rejected the proposal and stayed neutral.

The federal government's wartime suppression of antiwar speech culminated in which landmark Supreme Court decision?

  1. Plessy v. Ferguson (1896).
  2. Schenck v. United States (1919), upholding the Espionage Act under a "clear and present danger" standard.
  3. Korematsu v. United States (1944).
  4. Brown v. Board of Education (1954).

Correct: Schenck v. United States (1919).

Justice Holmes's opinion upheld the Espionage Act conviction of a Socialist who mailed antidraft leaflets, articulating the "clear and present danger" test. The Sedition Act (1918) further criminalized criticism of the government. Plessy is about segregation (1896); Korematsu is WWII (1944); Brown is civil-rights era (1954).

Which domestic development during World War I most directly transformed the demography of northern U.S. cities?

  1. The First Great Migration of African Americans from the rural South to northern industrial cities seeking wartime factory jobs.
  2. Mass return migration of European-born workers to serve in the armies of their home countries.
  3. Federal resettlement of Native Americans to Chicago and Detroit.
  4. Large-scale Chinese migration permitted by the lifting of exclusion laws.

Correct: The First Great Migration.

Roughly half a million African Americans moved North during WWI as European immigration collapsed and war industries recruited labor; Chicago's Black population doubled and the 1919 Chicago race riot followed. Federal relocation of Native peoples is a 1950s Termination-era policy. Chinese exclusion remained in force and was actually extended in this period.

The Senate's rejection of the Treaty of Versailles in 1919–1920 is best explained by which of the following?

  1. A constitutional amendment requiring a referendum on all foreign treaties.
  2. Wilson's refusal to compromise with Senator Henry Cabot Lodge's "Reservationists" plus Irreconcilable opposition to League of Nations membership.
  3. Unanimous House Republican demands that the U.S. annex Alsace-Lorraine.
  4. A Supreme Court ruling that the League violated the Tenth Amendment.

Correct: Wilson's refusal to compromise with Lodge plus Irreconcilable opposition.

Lodge's reservations centered on preserving congressional authority to declare war under Article X of the League Covenant; Wilson ordered Democrats to vote against the reservation-amended treaty, and the Irreconcilables blocked any version. Treaties do not require popular referenda. Alsace-Lorraine went back to France. The Supreme Court did not rule on the treaty.

Hughes's poem is a defining text of which 1920s cultural movement, and which leader of the decade promoted a rival vision of Black nationalism and "Back to Africa" through the Universal Negro Improvement Association?

Hughes, Zora Neale Hurston, Countee Cullen, Claude McKay, Duke Ellington, and Louis Armstrong anchored the Harlem Renaissance. Garvey's Jamaican-born UNIA claimed hundreds of thousands of U.S. members before Garvey's conviction for mail fraud and deportation. "It shall be unlawful for any teacher in any of the Universities, Normals and all other public schools of the State… to teach any theory that denies the story of the Divine Creation of man as taught in the Bible, and to teach instead that man has descended from a lower order of animals."

— Tennessee Butler Act, 1925 (law at issue in the Scopes "Monkey" Trial)

The 1925 Scopes Trial is best understood as a flashpoint in which broader 1920s conflict?

  1. A struggle between New Deal liberals and conservative Supreme Court justices.
  2. A cultural conflict between urban modernism and rural Protestant fundamentalism over science, religion, and public education.
  3. A partisan dispute over free silver and the gold standard.
  4. A debate about U.S. entry into the League of Nations.

Correct: The urban-modernist vs. rural-fundamentalist cultural conflict.

Clarence Darrow (defense, modernist) and William Jennings Bryan (prosecution, fundamentalist) dramatized the tension over evolution, biblical literalism, and cosmopolitan vs. small-town values. The court-packing struggle is a decade later. Free silver and the League are earlier controversies.

The National Origins Act of 1924 (Johnson-Reed) most directly did which of the following, and which movement of the same decade thrived in the same nativist climate?

  1. Abolished national-origin quotas; Anti-Saloon League's rise.
  2. Imposed strict quotas favoring northern and western European immigrants (using the 1890 census) and effectively barred most Asian immigrants; the Second Ku Klux Klan surged on an anti-Black, anti-Catholic, anti-Jewish, and anti-immigrant platform with a national following.
  3. Guaranteed unrestricted Mexican migration; the Niagara Movement.
  4. Extended citizenship to U.S. territorial possessions; the UNIA.

Correct: Strict quotas favoring northern/western Europe, largely barring Asians; Second KKK.

Johnson-Reed capped each nationality at 2% of its 1890 U.S. population — deliberately tilting toward "old stock" Europeans and barring Japanese (ending the 1907 Gentlemen's Agreement). The revived Klan of the 1920s was national — strong in Indiana, Oregon, and Colorado — and targeted Catholics, Jews, and immigrants as well as African Americans. Hart-Celler (1965) later abolished the quotas.

Which pair most accurately describes the economy and politics of the 1920s?

  1. Federal deficit spending and strong labor-union growth.
  2. A mass-consumer economy built on the assembly line (Ford Model T, the $5 day), consumer credit, advertising, and radio, alongside pro-business Republican administrations scarred by scandals like Teapot Dome and Prohibition-era organized crime (Capone).
  3. Integrated prosperity that reached farmers and textile workers equally with urban industrial workers.
  4. Strong federal antitrust enforcement that broke up the automobile industry.

Correct: Mass-consumer economy plus pro-business Republican rule, Teapot Dome, and Capone.

Ford's assembly line and the $5 day enabled mass automobile ownership; installment credit, advertising (Bruce Barton), radio, and movies produced a genuine consumer boom. Harding's Teapot Dome scandal (Secretary Fall's bribery over naval oil reserves) and Al Capone's bootlegging during Prohibition were the shadow side. Farmers and textile workers lagged badly, so the prosperity was uneven; antitrust enforcement was weak, not aggressive.

"Economic depression cannot be cured by legislative action or executive pronouncement. Economic wounds must be healed by the action of the cells of the economic body — the producers and consumers themselves… The attempt to cure them by the legislative action is likely to lengthen and deepen them."

— President Herbert Hoover, May 1930

"The only thing we have to fear is fear itself — nameless, unreasoning, unjustified terror which paralyzes needed efforts to convert retreat into advance… This nation asks for action, and action now… I shall ask the Congress for… broad Executive power to wage a war against the emergency, as great as the power that would be given to me if we were in fact invaded by a foreign foe."

— Franklin D. Roosevelt, First Inaugural Address, March 1933

Hoover's statement is most consistent with which response to the Depression?

  1. Immediate federal deficit spending to create millions of public-works jobs.
  2. Voluntary cooperation with business, limited federal intervention such as the Reconstruction Finance Corporation lending to banks and railroads, reliance on private and local relief, and protective measures like the Hawley-Smoot Tariff (1930).
  3. Nationalization of heavy industry under a federal commissariat.
  4. A universal basic income paid from payroll taxes.

Correct: Voluntary business cooperation, RFC, local relief, Hawley-Smoot.

Hoover believed in "rugged individualism" and "associationalism" — federal nudges rather than deficit spending. The RFC (1932) was his major top-down intervention; Hawley-Smoot was his protectionist misstep that worsened world trade. Mass public-works deficit spending is the New Deal / Keynes. Nationalization and UBI are not Hoover policies.

The dramatic "fear itself" urgency of FDR's 1933 inaugural reflected the accumulated crises of 1929–33, which included all of the following EXCEPT

  1. A wave of bank failures that forced state governors to declare "bank holidays."
  2. An unemployment rate that had reached roughly 25%.
  3. Hoover's use of the U.S. Army to disperse the Bonus Army of World War I veterans in 1932.
  4. A Dust Bowl famine that had already halved the U.S. population of the Great Plains by 1930.

Correct: The Dust Bowl did NOT halve the Plains population by 1930.

The Dust Bowl's worst years and the "Okie" migration to California came in the mid-1930s; it was not a 1930 event. The other three — bank failures, 25% unemployment, and Hoover's violent dispersal of the Bonus Army — were real drivers of the 1933 emergency.

"Every man a king, but no one wears a crown. I contend… that we limit the size of the big man's fortune… and guarantee that every family shall have an estate of at least one-third of the average family wealth, with a minimum home, an automobile, a radio, and enough to send the kids to school and college."

— Senator Huey Long of Louisiana, "Share Our Wealth" radio address, 1934

Which pair correctly matches a New Deal agency with its purpose?

  1. WPA — old-age pensions funded by payroll taxes; AAA — federal antitrust enforcement.
  2. TVA — federal dams for electricity and flood control in the Tennessee basin; CCC — wage-work for unemployed young men in conservation projects.
  3. SEC — crop-price supports by acreage reduction; FDIC — union organizing rights.
  4. Wagner Act — federal deposit insurance; Social Security — national public-art program.

Correct: TVA → dams/electricity; CCC → conservation employment.

WPA was employment and public works (not pensions); AAA was crop-price supports by acreage reduction (not antitrust); SEC regulated securities markets; FDIC insured bank deposits; the Wagner Act protected union organizing; Social Security created old-age pensions and unemployment insurance. Keep each pairing straight — the exam loves to mismatch them.

Long's "Share Our Wealth" proposal is best understood as

  1. A business-conservative critique that FDR had taken the country toward socialism.
  2. A populist critique from the political left charging that the New Deal had not gone far enough to redistribute wealth, similar in spirit to Francis Townsend's old-age pension plan and Father Coughlin's early pro-New Deal radio broadcasts that turned critical.
  3. A Supreme Court ruling that the New Deal violated the interstate commerce clause.
  4. A Republican platform plank for the election of 1936.

Correct: A left-populist critique, grouped with Townsend and the later Coughlin.

Long, Townsend, and (by 1935) Coughlin attacked FDR for being too friendly to big business and pressed him from the left/populist side, pushing FDR toward the Second New Deal. The American Liberty League was the business-conservative critic; Schechter and Butler were the judicial critiques; the Republican platform rejected, rather than embraced, wealth caps.

FDR's 1937 court-packing plan is best understood as a reaction to which of the following?

  1. The Supreme Court's refusal to strike down the Social Security Act.
  2. The Supreme Court's striking down of major First New Deal programs such as the NRA (Schechter, 1935) and the original AAA (Butler, 1936).
  3. Congressional Republican demands for judicial expansion.
  4. A constitutional crisis caused by the Twentieth Amendment's lame-duck provisions.

Correct: The Court's striking down of the NRA and AAA.

After winning a landslide in 1936, FDR proposed adding a new justice for every sitting justice over 70 (up to 15 total) to neutralize the "nine old men." The plan failed in Congress and damaged FDR politically, but the Court's subsequent "switch in time that saved nine" preserved later New Deal statutes. Social Security was upheld. Republicans opposed expansion. The Twentieth Amendment was a technical fix to inauguration dates.

Which development of the mid-1930s most directly expanded the organizing strength of American industrial workers?

  1. The striking down of the NRA in Schechter v. United States.
  2. The Wagner Act (1935) and the formation of the CIO under John L. Lewis, culminating in sit-down strikes that won recognition at General Motors in Flint (1936–37).
  3. The passage of the Smoot-Hawley Tariff.
  4. The Taft-Hartley Act.

Correct: The Wagner Act and the CIO, plus the Flint sit-down strikes.

The Wagner Act (National Labor Relations Act) guaranteed private-sector workers the right to organize and bargain collectively and created the NLRB; the CIO then organized mass-production industries the AFL had ignored. Schechter destroyed the NRA's weak labor protections. Smoot-Hawley is a 1930 tariff, not labor law. Taft-Hartley (1947) actually restricted union power.

The "New Deal coalition" that secured FDR's landslide in 1936 and dominated presidential politics for a generation included all of the following EXCEPT

  1. Urban working-class voters, including many Catholic and Jewish immigrants.
  2. White Southerners voting as "Solid South" Democrats.
  3. A majority of African Americans in the North, who shifted from the Republican Party to the Democratic Party.
  4. Midwestern business elites and large Wall Street financiers, who overwhelmingly backed FDR.

Correct: Midwestern business elites and Wall Street were NOT part of the coalition.

The New Deal coalition joined urban ethnic workers, African Americans (a historic shift from the party of Lincoln), organized labor, the Solid South, and liberal intellectuals. Big business largely backed Alf Landon in 1936 and formed the American Liberty League against FDR.

"Suppose my neighbor's home catches fire, and I have a length of garden hose four or five hundred feet away… If he can take my garden hose and connect it up with his hydrant, I may help him to put out his fire… I don't want $15 — I want my garden hose back after the fire is over."

— President Franklin D. Roosevelt, press conference on Lend-Lease, December 1940

FDR's "garden hose" analogy is a defense of which 1941 policy, and which 1940 organization most vocally opposed it?

  1. The Neutrality Act of 1935; opposed by the Committee on Public Information.
  2. The Lend-Lease Act, under which the U.S. supplied Britain, the USSR, and other Allies with war materiel without immediate payment; opposed by the America First Committee, which featured Charles Lindbergh.
  3. The Marshall Plan's aid to postwar Western Europe; opposed by the Congress of Industrial Organizations.
  4. The declaration of war on Germany in December 1941; opposed by the National Woman's Party.

Correct: Lend-Lease, opposed by America First.

Lend-Lease (March 1941), foreshadowed by Cash-and-Carry (1939) and the Destroyers-for-Bases deal (1940), authorized the President to transfer arms to any country whose defense he deemed vital to U.S. security, effectively ending the Neutrality Acts' logic. America First led isolationist opposition; Lindbergh was its most famous speaker. The CPI is a WWI agency; the Marshall Plan is postwar; the National Woman's Party focused on the ERA.

The immediate U.S. entry into World War II in December 1941 followed which event?

  1. The German invasion of Poland in September 1939.
  2. The Japanese attack on Pearl Harbor on December 7, 1941, and Germany's declaration of war on the United States days later.
  3. The Battle of the Bulge in the Ardennes.
  4. The signing of the Atlantic Charter.

Correct: The attack on Pearl Harbor and Germany's subsequent declaration of war.

Pearl Harbor brought Congress's near-unanimous declaration of war on Japan (December 8, 1941). Germany and Italy then declared war on the U.S. (December 11). Poland's invasion began the European war in 1939 but the U.S. stayed neutral. The Battle of the Bulge was December 1944. The Atlantic Charter (August 1941) preceded, but did not itself trigger, U.S. entry.

We cannot — by availing ourselves of the calm perspective of hindsight — now say that at that time these actions were unjustified."

The policy upheld in Korematsu was authorized by

  1. The Smith Act of 1940.
  2. Executive Order 9066 (1942), which authorized the forced relocation of roughly 120,000 Japanese Americans from the West Coast to internment camps such as Manzanar and Tule Lake.
  3. The GI Bill of 1944.
  4. The War Powers Resolution of 1973.

Correct: Executive Order 9066 (1942).

FDR's EO 9066 empowered military commanders to designate exclusion zones; in practice it targeted Japanese Americans, two-thirds of whom were U.S. citizens. The Smith Act criminalized advocacy of the government's violent overthrow. The GI Bill is veterans' benefits. The War Powers Resolution is a Vietnam-era statute.

The wartime experience of African Americans was shaped by which of the following developments?

  1. The full desegregation of the U.S. armed forces by FDR in 1941.
  2. A. Philip Randolph's threatened March on Washington, leading to FDR's Executive Order 8802 (1941) banning racial discrimination in defense industries and creating the FEPC, alongside the "Double V" campaign and the service of the Tuskegee Airmen.
  3. The abolition of poll taxes across the South by federal statute.
  4. The immediate enforcement of Brown v. Board of Education.

Correct: Randolph's March on Washington threat, EO 8802 / FEPC, Double V, and the Tuskegee Airmen.

The armed forces remained segregated until Truman's 1948 executive order; poll taxes were not federally abolished until the 24th Amendment (1964) and VRA (1965); Brown was a decade away.

Which of the following best describes Mexican American and women's experiences on the WWII home front?

  1. Mexican immigration was suspended entirely, and women were barred from industrial employment by a wartime federal statute.
  2. The Bracero Program (1942) brought hundreds of thousands of Mexican contract laborers to U.S. agriculture and railroads, even as the 1943 Los Angeles Zoot Suit Riots exposed racial tensions, while roughly six million women entered paid work — "Rosie the Riveter" — and WACs/WAVES auxiliaries incorporated women into the armed forces.
  3. The Chicano Moratorium and Title IX both date from this period.
  4. Federal daycare was prohibited by Congress for the duration of the war.

Correct: Bracero Program + Zoot Suit Riots; Rosie the Riveter + WACs/WAVES.

The Bracero Program (1942–64) supplied contract labor; the 1943 Zoot Suit Riots in Los Angeles saw white servicemen attack Mexican American youths with police complicity. Roughly six million women entered paid wartime work, and federally funded daycare briefly appeared (the Lanham Act centers). The Chicano Moratorium (1970) and Title IX (1972) are from Unit 8.

Which of the following correctly pairs a European Theater event with a Pacific Theater event, both widely regarded as turning points of World War II?

  1. Battle of the Bulge (Pacific) / D-Day (Europe).
  2. D-Day and the Yalta Conference (Europe, 1944–45) / Battle of Midway and the Manhattan Project's atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki (Pacific, 1942 and 1945).
  3. Gettysburg (Europe) / Manila Bay (Pacific).
  4. Fall of Saigon (Europe) / Tet Offensive (Pacific).

Correct: D-Day and Yalta (Europe) / Midway and Hiroshima-Nagasaki (Pacific).

D-Day (June 1944) opened the Western Front in Europe; Yalta (February 1945) shaped the postwar settlement. Midway (June 1942) crippled Japanese carrier power; the Manhattan Project (Oppenheimer, Los Alamos) produced the atomic bombs dropped in August 1945. The other pairs either swap theaters (the Battle of the Bulge is European) or invoke unrelated wars (Gettysburg is the Civil War; Saigon/Tet are Vietnam).

SAQ 1. Using your knowledge of the period 1900–1920, answer (a), (b), and (c).

(a) Identify ONE specific Progressive Era reform at the federal level and explain its immediate goal.

(b) Explain ONE specific way Progressive reformers sought to expand American democracy during this period.

(c) Explain ONE significant limitation of the Progressive Era for African Americans OR for women of color.

Rubric

  • (a) 1 point for a specific federal reform with an immediate goal — e.g., Pure Food and Drug Act (1906, ban adulterated food/drugs), Hepburn Act (1906, ICC rate-setting), Federal Reserve Act (1913, stable currency/banking), Clayton Antitrust Act (1914, strengthen Sherman, protect unions), 16th Amendment (income tax).
  • (b) 1 point for a specific democratizing reform with explanation — e.g., 17th Amendment (direct election of senators), 19th Amendment (woman suffrage), initiative/referendum/recall at the state level, direct primaries.
  • (c) 1 point for a specific limitation — e.g., Wilson's segregation of federal agencies, continued lynching (Red Summer 1919), unchecked Southern disfranchisement, NAWSA's strategic exclusion of Black women, Asian exclusion, denial of organizing rights in most industries.

Model answer

(a) The Federal Reserve Act of 1913 created twelve regional reserve banks under a central Federal Reserve Board. Its immediate goal was to stabilize the nation's currency and banking system after the Panic of 1907 had exposed the absence of a lender of last resort, giving the federal government tools to expand credit in downturns and restrain speculation in booms.

(b) The Seventeenth Amendment (1913) provided for the direct election of U.S. senators, replacing selection by state legislatures. Progressives argued that legislative selection produced corrupt bargains with railroad and trust money; popular election was meant to make the Senate responsive to voters rather than political machines.

(c) Progressivism largely excluded African Americans. Wilson's administration segregated federal workplaces and screened The Birth of a Nation at the White House; lynching continued, with no federal anti-lynching law passed; and Southern disfranchisement under poll taxes and literacy tests went unchallenged. The Niagara Movement (1905) and NAACP (1909) emerged partly in response to this failure.

SAQ 2. Using your knowledge of the period 1933–1945, answer (a), (b), and (c).

(a) Identify ONE specific New Deal program and explain how it addressed the Great Depression.

(b) Explain ONE way the New Deal changed the relationship between the federal government and the American economy or American citizens.

(c) Explain ONE specific limitation of the New Deal — a group or problem it did not reach.

Rubric

  • (a) 1 point for a specific program (CCC, AAA, TVA, WPA, NRA, Social Security, Wagner Act, FLSA, FDIC/Glass-Steagall, SEC, HOLC) with a clear explanation of how it addressed the Depression.
  • (b) 1 point for a specific structural change — regulatory state (SEC, FDIC), right to organize (Wagner Act), old-age pensions and unemployment insurance (Social Security), federal minimum wage/maximum hours (FLSA), deficit spending.
  • (c) 1 point for a specific limitation — domestic and agricultural workers excluded from Social Security/Wagner (affecting mostly Black and Latino workers), AAA payments going to landowners rather than sharecroppers, segregation within the CCC, the "Roosevelt Recession" of 1937–38, persistent double-digit unemployment until WWII, failure to pass anti-lynching legislation.

Model answer

(a) The Civilian Conservation Corps (CCC, 1933) put roughly three million unemployed young men into work camps, planting trees, fighting fires, and building trails and park facilities. It attacked both youth unemployment and the soil erosion that produced the Dust Bowl, with most of each worker's small wage sent home to his family.

(b) The Social Security Act (1935) fundamentally changed the federal government's relationship to citizens by creating the first national system of old-age pensions and unemployment insurance, financed by a payroll tax. For the first time, ordinary working Americans had a direct, lifelong economic relationship with Washington, independent of state-level poor relief.

(c) A major limitation was the exclusion of domestic workers and agricultural laborers from both Social Security and the Wagner Act. Because roughly two-thirds of employed African Americans worked in those two sectors, the core protections of the Second New Deal effectively bypassed the Black working class — a compromise demanded by Southern Democrats as the price of passage.

LEQ. Write a thesis-driven essay responding to the following prompt.

Evaluate the extent to which World War II (1941–1945) transformed the status and experience of women AND minority groups (African Americans, Mexican Americans, and/or Japanese Americans) on the U.S. home front.

Rubric (abbreviated LEQ)

  • Thesis (1): A defensible claim that takes a position on the extent of transformation — e.g., the war substantially expanded economic and political opportunity for women and African Americans yet delivered severe repression to Japanese Americans, and many changes were partial.
  • Contextualization (1): Situate the home front within the interwar Depression economy, pre-war racial regime (Jim Crow, segregated military), and mobilization for a two-front war.
  • Evidence (2): At least two specific examples per group — Rosie the Riveter, WACs/WAVES for women; EO 8802, Double V, Tuskegee Airmen, Detroit 1943 for African Americans; Bracero Program and Zoot Suit Riots for Mexican Americans; EO 9066, Manzanar, Korematsu, and the 442nd RCT for Japanese Americans.
  • Analysis & reasoning (2): Use causation and continuity/change — why mobilization pushed open some doors (labor shortages, international scrutiny) while simultaneously justifying mass internment; how change was partial and partly rolled back after 1945.

Model answer (condensed)

World War II transformed the home front unevenly: mobilization dramatically expanded economic and political opportunity for white women and, more cautiously, for African Americans and Mexican Americans, even as the federal government imposed the most severe racial repression in modern U.S. history on Japanese Americans. The war's legacies therefore included both the seeds of the postwar civil-rights movement and a constitutional low point in Korematsu.

For women, roughly six million entered wartime paid work, many in industrial jobs previously closed to them; "Rosie the Riveter" became iconic, the WAVES and WACs incorporated women into military auxiliaries, and federally funded daycare briefly appeared. Yet most industrial jobs were surrendered to returning veterans by 1946, and the cult of domesticity largely reasserted itself in the postwar baby boom.

For African Americans, A. Philip Randolph's threatened March on Washington forced FDR's Executive Order 8802 (1941), banning discrimination in defense industries and creating the FEPC. The Second Great Migration accelerated as 1.5 million Black southerners moved to northern and western defense centers. The "Double V" campaign framed the war as simultaneous struggles against fascism abroad and Jim Crow at home; the Tuskegee Airmen dramatized the contradictions of segregated service. Yet the Detroit race riot of 1943 and continued military segregation underscored the limits.

For Mexican Americans, the Bracero Program (1942) recruited hundreds of thousands of contract laborers whose cheap labor became embedded in southwestern agriculture. At the same time, the Los Angeles Zoot Suit Riots (1943) saw white servicemen attack Mexican American youths with police complicity — exposing wartime racial tensions within the "arsenal of democracy."

The sharpest rupture was for Japanese Americans. Executive Order 9066 (1942) authorized the forced removal of roughly 120,000 people, two-thirds U.S. citizens, to internment camps like Manzanar and Tule Lake. The Supreme Court upheld the policy in Korematsu v. United States (1944). Even so, the all-Nisei 442nd Regimental Combat Team became one of the most decorated units in U.S. military history, embodying the contradictions of loyalty under coercion.

On balance, the war opened real but partial doors for women, African Americans, and Mexican Americans — doors the civil-rights and feminist movements of the next generation would push wider. For Japanese Americans the war was a near-total rupture. Transformation is therefore the right word, but the transformations were not uniform and were sometimes devastating.
