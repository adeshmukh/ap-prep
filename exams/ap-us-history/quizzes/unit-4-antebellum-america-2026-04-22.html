Antebellum America: Jefferson to Seneca Falls

Twenty stimulus-driven MCQs and two free-response prompts covering the Jeffersonian era, Marshall Court, War of 1812, American System, Market Revolution, Jacksonian democracy, slavery's expansion, the Second Great Awakening, antebellum reform, and the emergence of abolitionism and women's rights.

20 multiple choice 2 free response AP-level

Multiple choice

"The Constitution has made no provision for our holding foreign territory, still less for incorporating foreign nations into our Union. The executive, in seizing the fugitive occurrence which so much advances the good of their country, have done an act beyond the Constitution. The Legislature, in casting behind them metaphysical subtleties, and risking themselves like faithful servants, must ratify and pay for it, and throw themselves on their country for doing for them, unauthorized, what we know they would have done for themselves had they been in a situation to do it."

— Adapted from Thomas Jefferson, letter to John C. Breckinridge, August 12, 1803

Jefferson's anxiety in the letter most directly reflects which tension within his political philosophy?

  1. His desire to preserve Federalist economic institutions such as the Bank of the United States.
  2. His commitment to strict construction of the Constitution in conflict with the practical advantage of acquiring the Louisiana Territory.
  3. His belief that the federal government should actively subsidize manufacturing over agriculture.
  4. His view that foreign policy should be directed primarily by the Senate rather than the executive.

Correct: The tension between strict construction and the practical advantage of acquiring Louisiana.

Jefferson had long insisted the federal government could do only what the Constitution expressly authorized, but acquiring foreign territory was nowhere enumerated. He admits here that he has acted "beyond the Constitution" and asks Congress to ratify after the fact. The Bank option is wrong — Jefferson opposed Hamilton's Bank on precisely these strict-construction grounds. Subsidies for manufacturing describe Hamilton and later Clay, not Jefferson, who idealized the yeoman farmer. The Senate-directs-foreign-policy reading misreads the letter, which is about constitutional authority, not interbranch foreign-policy turf.

Which expedition most directly followed from the acquisition Jefferson is defending?

  1. John C. Frémont's mapping of California in the 1840s.
  2. The Lewis and Clark Expedition (1804–1806), which explored the new territory and sought a water route to the Pacific.
  3. Zebulon Pike's expedition to the Great Lakes.
  4. The Wilkes Expedition to the South Pacific.

Correct: The Lewis and Clark Expedition.

Jefferson commissioned Lewis, Clark, and the Corps of Discovery to explore the Louisiana Purchase, establish relations with Native nations, catalog natural resources, and search for a Northwest Passage. Frémont's California work is a later 1840s Mexican-Cession-era enterprise. Pike did explore the southwestern portion of Louisiana and the Rockies — not the Great Lakes — so that option is factually wrong. The Wilkes Expedition was a Pacific oceanographic voyage, not continental exploration of Louisiana.

Which event during Jefferson's second term most directly exposed the limits of his efforts to keep the United States out of European conflicts?

  1. The Whiskey Rebellion, which required federal military intervention in Pennsylvania.
  2. The Embargo Act of 1807, which devastated New England commerce without forcing Britain or France to respect American neutral rights.
  3. Shays' Rebellion, which discredited the Articles of Confederation.
  4. The XYZ Affair, which nearly led to war with France.

Correct: The Embargo Act of 1807.

Jefferson's Embargo banned virtually all American exports in response to British and French interference with neutral shipping; it collapsed New England trade, spawned widespread smuggling, and failed to change European policy. The Whiskey Rebellion (1794) and Shays' Rebellion (1786–87) both predate Jefferson's presidency. The XYZ Affair occurred under Adams in the late 1790s.

"Let the end be legitimate, let it be within the scope of the Constitution, and all means which are appropriate, which are plainly adapted to that end, which are not prohibited, but consist with the letter and spirit of the Constitution, are constitutional… The power to tax involves the power to destroy… If the States may tax one instrument employed by the Government in the execution of its powers, they may tax any and every other instrument."

— Adapted from Chief Justice John Marshall, McCulloch v. Maryland, 1819

The excerpt most directly supports which of the following constitutional doctrines?

  1. A broad reading of the necessary and proper clause that sustained implied federal powers and federal supremacy over state taxation.
  2. The doctrine of nullification, which allowed states to void federal laws they judged unconstitutional.
  3. The compact theory of the Union articulated in the Virginia and Kentucky Resolutions.
  4. Strict construction of the Constitution limiting Congress to its enumerated powers.

Correct: A broad reading of the necessary and proper clause and federal supremacy over state taxation.

McCulloch v. Maryland upheld the Second Bank of the United States under the necessary and proper clause and struck down Maryland's tax on the Bank under the supremacy clause. The nullification and compact-theory options describe Calhoun, Jefferson, and Madison's 1798–99 Resolutions — the opposite of what Marshall argues here. Strict construction is what Marshall rejects.

Which later Marshall Court decision most clearly extended the nationalist logic expressed in the excerpt?

  1. Dred Scott v. Sandford, which restricted the power of Congress to regulate slavery in the territories.
  2. Gibbons v. Ogden (1824), which interpreted the commerce clause broadly to strike down a New York steamboat monopoly.
  3. Marbury v. Madison, which had been decided in 1803 and established judicial review.
  4. Plessy v. Ferguson, which upheld "separate but equal" public accommodations.

Correct: Gibbons v. Ogden (1824).

Gibbons used a sweeping reading of the commerce clause to assert federal authority over interstate navigation — a direct continuation of McCulloch's nationalist method. Dred Scott (1857) and Plessy (1896) are post-Marshall and concern slavery and segregation. Marbury predates McCulloch — the question asks for a later case — and, while important, concerns judicial review rather than the scope of federal power vis-à-vis the states.

In Worcester v. Georgia (1832) the Marshall Court ruled that the Cherokee Nation constituted a "distinct community" whose laws were not subject to Georgia. President Andrew Jackson's response to that ruling most directly illustrated which of the following?

  1. The Supreme Court's ability to enforce its own decisions against a reluctant executive.
  2. The executive's willingness to disregard the Court when Indian removal served the political interests of his base among southern and western whites.
  3. A broad northern consensus in favor of Cherokee sovereignty.
  4. The Cherokee Nation's military defeat in open warfare with the United States.

Correct: Jackson's willingness to disregard the Court to facilitate Indian removal.

Jackson declined to enforce Worcester, clearing the way for the 1830 Indian Removal Act to proceed, culminating in the 1838 Trail of Tears under Van Buren. The Court had no independent enforcement mechanism — it depends on executive compliance, and Jackson withheld it. A "northern consensus" favoring the Cherokee is overstated; many northerners were sympathetic but not organized to override removal. The Cherokee were not defeated militarily; they were forcibly removed under treaty pressure and federal troops.

"Resolved, That it be and hereby is recommended to the legislatures of the several states… to adopt all such measures as may be necessary effectually to protect the citizens of said states from the operation and effects of all acts which have been or may be passed by the Congress of the United States, which shall contain provisions, subjecting the militia or other citizens to forcible drafts, conscriptions, or impressments, not authorized by the Constitution of the United States."

— Adapted from the Report of the Hartford Convention, January 1815

The Hartford Convention's proposals most directly reflected which of the following?

  1. New England Federalist opposition to the War of 1812 and to the growing political power of southern and western Republicans.
  2. A demand by southern planters for the protection of slavery in newly acquired territories.
  3. A plan by western settlers to secede from the United States in alliance with Britain.
  4. Jeffersonian Republican support for conscription to win the War of 1812.

Correct: New England Federalist opposition to the War of 1812 and Republican dominance.

The Hartford Convention gathered New England Federalists frustrated by the embargo, "Mr. Madison's War," and a perceived "Virginia dynasty" tilting the Union southward. The southern-planter option inverts the geography. Western settlers overwhelmingly supported the war — the "War Hawks" came from the West and South. Jeffersonian Republicans were the war's sponsors, not the convention's organizers.

The political fate of the Federalist Party after this convention was most directly shaped by which subsequent event?

  1. The Dred Scott decision, which discredited Federalist constitutional views.
  2. News of Andrew Jackson's victory at the Battle of New Orleans (January 1815) and of the Treaty of Ghent, which made the Convention look disloyal.
  3. The Alien and Sedition Acts, which turned public opinion against Federalists.
  4. The Nullification Crisis of 1832, which absorbed Federalist voters into the Democratic Party.

Correct: Jackson's victory at New Orleans and news of the Treaty of Ghent.

The Hartford Report reached Washington just as news of Jackson's lopsided New Orleans victory and the Ghent peace arrived; the Federalists' timing made them look seditious, and the party never recovered nationally. Dred Scott is from 1857 and concerns slavery, not Federalism. The Alien and Sedition Acts damaged Federalists in 1798 — well before Hartford. The Nullification Crisis (1832) postdates the party's collapse and concerned South Carolina Democrats, not the absorption of Federalists.

In the decade after the War of 1812, American foreign policy most notably turned to which of the following?

  1. The acquisition of new Caribbean colonies by force.
  2. The assertion in the Monroe Doctrine (1823) that the Western Hemisphere was closed to further European colonization.
  3. A permanent military alliance with Great Britain against France.
  4. A neutrality treaty with Japan that opened Asian markets.

Correct: The Monroe Doctrine (1823).

Secretary of State John Quincy Adams drafted what President Monroe delivered: a declaration that European powers should not recolonize the newly independent republics of Latin America, paired with a pledge of non-interference in Europe. The Caribbean-colonies option is anachronistic — U.S. overseas colonialism follows 1898. No formal alliance with Britain occurred; the Rush-Bagot Agreement demilitarized the Great Lakes but was not a military pact. Perry's opening of Japan came in 1853–54, a generation later.

Map description (paraphrased): A shaded map of the United States in 1840 shows the heaviest cotton-producing counties concentrated in a belt running from South Carolina and Georgia across central Alabama and Mississippi into eastern Louisiana and Arkansas. An inset line graph labeled "U.S. cotton output, 1790–1840" climbs from roughly 3,000 bales in 1790 to approximately 1,350,000 bales in 1840. A second inset shows the enslaved population of Mississippi, Alabama, and Louisiana rising from about 40,000 in 1810 to over 700,000 in 1840.

— Adapted from data in the 1840 U.S. Census and Gavin Wright, Slavery and American Economic Development, 2006

The trends shown in the map and insets are most directly explained by which combination of developments?

  1. The gradual abolition of slavery in the South and the mechanization of cotton picking.
  2. Eli Whitney's cotton gin (1793), the opening of "new" southwestern lands after Indian removal, and a massive internal slave trade from the Upper South.
  3. A federal subsidy program for cotton planters enacted by the Jackson administration.
  4. The Embargo Act, which redirected planters from tobacco to cotton.

Correct: The cotton gin, southwestern expansion after Indian removal, and the internal slave trade.

The cotton gin made short-staple cotton profitable, the forced removal of the "Five Civilized Tribes" opened the Deep South interior, and the domestic slave trade moved roughly a million enslaved people from the Chesapeake to the Cotton Belt. Slavery was not being abolished in the South — it was expanding rapidly. Jackson signed no federal subsidy for cotton planters. The Embargo Act (1807) does not explain a four-decade cotton boom; the gin, geography, and the slave trade do.

Which of the following best describes the northern counterpart to the southern cotton economy shown above during the same period?

  1. A resurgence of subsistence farming and the decline of long-distance trade.
  2. The rise of factory textile production using the Lowell/Waltham system, an extensive canal and railroad network, and wage labor supplied initially by young New England women and later by Irish immigrants.
  3. The replacement of free labor with indentured servitude across the Northeast.
  4. A shift by northeastern states toward large plantation agriculture modeled on Virginia tobacco estates.

Correct: Lowell/Waltham textile mills, canals and railroads, wage labor from Yankee women and later Irish immigrants.

The Market Revolution integrated northern manufacturing (Lowell's textile mills using Whitney-style interchangeable parts and water power), transportation (Erie Canal 1825, proliferating railroads in the 1830s–40s), and a new wage-earning workforce. Subsistence farming was in relative decline as farmers produced for distant markets. Indentured servitude was fading, not reviving. Large plantations did not develop in New England or the Mid-Atlantic; climate, soil, and state emancipation laws prevented it.

The Missouri Compromise of 1820 most directly attempted to manage which political consequence of the cotton boom depicted above?

  1. The secession of South Carolina from the Union.
  2. The sectional balance between free and slave states as enslaved people and planters moved into new western territories.
  3. The abolition of the international slave trade, not yet prohibited by Congress.
  4. The introduction of the cotton gin into Missouri's agricultural economy.

Correct: The sectional balance between free and slave states.

The 1820 compromise admitted Missouri as a slave state and Maine as a free state and drew the 36°30′ line across the remaining Louisiana Purchase. It did not resolve South Carolina secession (that crisis came in 1832 and 1860). Congress had already banned the international slave trade in 1808. Missouri's economy was not transformed by the cotton gin; it produced hemp, tobacco, and grain.

Political cartoon description (paraphrased): A lithograph titled "King Andrew the First" shows President Jackson dressed in royal robes and crown, trampling a torn copy of the Constitution and the Bank of the United States charter beneath his feet. He holds a scepter in one hand and a scroll labeled "VETO" in the other. A caption reads, "Born to Command."

— Adapted from an anti-Jackson political cartoon, c. 1832

The cartoon was most likely produced by supporters of which emerging political coalition?

  1. The Democratic Party of Martin Van Buren.
  2. The Whig Party, organized in opposition to Jackson's use of executive power.
  3. The Liberty Party, focused on immediate abolition of slavery.
  4. The Know-Nothing (American) Party, focused on immigration restriction.

Correct: The Whig Party.

The Whigs coalesced in the early 1830s out of National Republicans, anti-Jackson Democrats, and Anti-Masons, taking their name from British opponents of monarchy to brand Jackson as "King Andrew." Van Buren was Jackson's political heir, not his opponent. The Liberty Party (1840) and the Know-Nothings (1850s) come later and focus on different issues.

Jackson's 1832 veto of the recharter of the Second Bank of the United States most directly reflected which of the following convictions?

  1. That the Bank was unconstitutional because the Supreme Court had struck it down.
  2. That the Bank concentrated financial privilege in the hands of a "monied aristocracy" at the expense of ordinary farmers and mechanics.
  3. That a strong national bank was essential to the federal government's credit during the War of 1812.
  4. That the Bank's charter violated the Missouri Compromise.

Correct: That the Bank benefited a "monied aristocracy" at the expense of ordinary Americans.

Jackson's veto message framed the Bank as a corrupt monopoly that privileged eastern and foreign investors over producers. The Supreme Court had upheld the Bank in McCulloch v. Maryland, not struck it down. Supporting the Bank for wartime credit describes Madison's reasoning in 1816, not Jackson's. The Missouri Compromise (1820) concerned slavery in the territories and has no bearing on a bank charter.

The Nullification Crisis of 1832–33, in which South Carolina declared federal tariff laws void within its borders, was most directly resolved by which combination of measures?

  1. Henry Clay's Compromise Tariff, which gradually lowered rates, paired with the Force Bill authorizing Jackson to use the military to enforce federal law.
  2. Immediate secession of South Carolina and its readmission on new terms in 1835.
  3. A Supreme Court ruling that federal tariffs were unconstitutional.
  4. Jackson's decision to veto the Tariff of Abominations outright.

Correct: Clay's Compromise Tariff paired with the Force Bill.

The crisis ended with a face-saving package: lower tariffs over nine years plus a Force Bill that reaffirmed federal supremacy. South Carolina did not secede in 1832 — it rescinded its nullification ordinance, though it symbolically "nullified" the Force Bill. No Supreme Court ruling invalidated the tariffs; tariffs are an enumerated congressional power. Jackson did not veto the Tariff of Abominations; he enforced federal law.

"I am aware that many object to the severity of my language; but is there not cause for severity? I will be as harsh as truth, and as uncompromising as justice. On this subject, I do not wish to think, or speak, or write, with moderation… I am in earnest — I will not equivocate — I will not excuse — I will not retreat a single inch — AND I WILL BE HEARD."

— William Lloyd Garrison, The Liberator, January 1, 1831

"I hold then, that there never has yet existed a wealthy and civilized society in which one portion of the community did not, in point of fact, live on the labor of the other… I fearlessly assert that the existing relation between the two races in the South, against which these blind fanatics are waging war, forms the most solid and durable foundation on which to rear free and stable political institutions."

— John C. Calhoun, Speech on the Reception of Abolition Petitions, 1837

Garrison's tone and content in the first excerpt most clearly distinguished the "immediatist" wing of abolitionism from which earlier anti-slavery approach?

  1. The Nat Turner Rebellion of 1831, which Garrison personally organized.
  2. The gradualist and colonizationist approach of the American Colonization Society, which proposed to resettle free Black Americans in Liberia.
  3. The Fugitive Slave Act of 1850, which Garrison defended.
  4. The Compromise of 1820, which Garrison helped to draft.

Correct: The gradualist and colonizationist approach of the American Colonization Society.

Garrison, Frederick Douglass, and David Walker's Appeal demanded immediate, uncompensated emancipation and rejected colonization as a racist evasion. Garrison did not organize Nat Turner's revolt (Turner was a lay preacher in Virginia; Garrison was in Boston). The Fugitive Slave Act (1850) was loathed by abolitionists, not defended by them — Garrison publicly burned a copy. Garrison was a child in 1820; he did not help draft the Missouri Compromise.

Calhoun's argument in the second excerpt is most accurately described as which of the following?

  1. A moderate defense of slavery as a "necessary evil" that would gradually disappear.
  2. A "positive good" pro-slavery argument that recast slavery as the foundation of a free and stable republic.
  3. A theological case derived from the Second Great Awakening for the immediate abolition of slavery.
  4. A transcendentalist critique of commercial society typified by Emerson and Thoreau.

Correct: A "positive good" pro-slavery argument.

Calhoun (and later George Fitzhugh) abandoned the earlier Jeffersonian framing of slavery as a "necessary evil" and argued affirmatively that slavery was morally justified and socially superior to northern "wage slavery." The "necessary evil" option describes the older view Calhoun rejected. Second Great Awakening theology (Finney, perfectionism) actually fueled abolitionism, not pro-slavery thought. Transcendentalism is Emerson's and Thoreau's literary and philosophical movement — unrelated to Calhoun's speech.

Which of the following best describes the relationship between the Second Great Awakening and antebellum reform movements such as temperance, common schools, and asylum reform?

  1. The Awakening discouraged social reform by insisting on predestination and the inability of humans to improve society.
  2. Revivalists like Charles Grandison Finney preached perfectionism — the idea that individuals and society could be purged of sin — which inspired reformers including Horace Mann (common schools) and Dorothea Dix (asylum and prison reform).
  3. Reform movements grew entirely out of Enlightenment deism and had no religious roots.
  4. The Awakening's revivals were confined to the South and produced no reform movements in the North.

Correct: Perfectionist revivalism inspired reformers like Horace Mann and Dorothea Dix.

Finney's "burned-over district" revivals in upstate New York rejected Calvinist predestination and insisted people could choose salvation and build a righteous society. That moral optimism underwrote temperance (American Temperance Society), common-school advocacy (Mann), asylum reform (Dix), Sabbatarianism, and abolition. The Enlightenment-only reading misses the revivalist core. The Awakening was national but especially powerful in the North and West, and its reform energy was overwhelmingly northern.

"The civilized man has built a coach, but has lost the use of his feet… His note-books impair his memory; his libraries overload his wit." This observation from Ralph Waldo Emerson's "Self-Reliance" is most characteristic of which antebellum intellectual movement?

  1. Scottish Common Sense realism, which celebrated technological progress without reservation.
  2. Transcendentalism, which emphasized individual intuition, nature, and skepticism toward materialism and conformity — and which inspired utopian experiments such as Brook Farm.
  3. Social Darwinism, which justified industrial hierarchy as natural selection.
  4. The Gospel of Wealth, which tied philanthropy to industrial fortunes.

Correct: Transcendentalism.

Emerson, Thoreau (Walden, "Civil Disobedience"), and Margaret Fuller championed individual intuition, communion with nature, and wariness of market society — inspiring utopian experiments like Brook Farm and loosely resonating with the Shakers and Oneida. Scottish Common Sense realism shaped earlier American religious and moral philosophy but lacks Emerson's romantic skepticism of modern life. Social Darwinism (Sumner, Spencer) and the Gospel of Wealth (Carnegie 1889) are Gilded Age, not antebellum.

"We hold these truths to be self-evident: that all men and women are created equal…" The 1848 Declaration of Sentiments from which this passage comes is best understood as which of the following?

  1. A formal secession of American women from the Union following the Missouri Compromise.
  2. A foundational statement of the American women's rights movement, produced at Seneca Falls by Elizabeth Cady Stanton, Lucretia Mott, and others and modeled on the Declaration of Independence.
  3. A pro-slavery tract written by southern women in defense of the "cult of domesticity."
  4. A document authored by Horace Mann to justify compulsory public education for girls.

Correct: The foundational statement of the American women's rights movement at Seneca Falls.

Stanton and Mott, both active in abolition (and barred from full participation at the 1840 World Anti-Slavery Convention in London), organized the Seneca Falls Convention and drafted the Declaration of Sentiments, cataloguing grievances including the denial of the vote. No "secession of women" occurred. The cult of domesticity was the ideology the Declaration challenged, not an expression of it. Mann authored common-school arguments but not this document.

Free response

SAQ. Using your knowledge of the period 1824–1840, answer (a), (b), and (c).

(a) Briefly describe ONE specific way in which the rise of Jacksonian democracy expanded political participation in the United States.

(b) Briefly explain ONE specific way in which Andrew Jackson's presidency was criticized by his political opponents as an abuse of executive power.

(c) Briefly explain ONE specific consequence of Jacksonian-era federal Indian policy for Native American peoples.

Rubric

  • (a) 1 point for a specific, accurate expansion: elimination of most property qualifications for white male voting; rise of national nominating conventions in place of congressional caucuses; near-universal use of popular election (not legislative appointment) of presidential electors by 1832; the emergence of mass-party organization and the "spoils system."
  • (b) 1 point for a specific, well-explained Whig/National Republican critique: the 1832 Bank veto as exceeding the president's constitutional role; removal of federal deposits to "pet banks"; use of the spoils system to politicize the civil service; defiance of Worcester v. Georgia; overall depiction as "King Andrew I."
  • (c) 1 point for a specific consequence: the Indian Removal Act (1830) and the forced relocation of the "Five Civilized Tribes"; the 1838 Trail of Tears (thousands of Cherokee deaths); destruction of tribal sovereignty in the Southeast; Second Seminole War; dispossession opening Deep South land to cotton and slavery.

Model answer

(a) Between roughly 1820 and 1840, most states eliminated property-holding qualifications for white male voting and shifted from legislative to popular election of presidential electors. By 1828, about 1.1 million white men voted for president, more than triple the 1824 total, and by 1840 the figure topped 2.4 million as party organizations mobilized them through rallies, torchlight parades, and partisan newspapers.

(b) Jackson's opponents — who would form the Whig Party — accused him of abusing executive power when he vetoed the recharter of the Second Bank of the United States in 1832 on policy rather than constitutional grounds and then ordered federal deposits removed to Democratic-friendly "pet banks" over the objections of two Treasury Secretaries. Whigs caricatured him as "King Andrew I," a monarch trampling the Constitution, and built their party around congressional supremacy and internal improvements in reaction.

(c) The Indian Removal Act of 1830 authorized the forced relocation of southeastern Native nations to territory west of the Mississippi. Even after the Supreme Court ruled in Worcester v. Georgia (1832) that Georgia could not extend its laws over Cherokee lands, Jackson refused to enforce the ruling. In 1838 federal troops under Van Buren forced roughly 16,000 Cherokee to march west on what became known as the Trail of Tears; approximately a quarter died of exposure, disease, and starvation, and the southeastern Native homelands were opened to cotton planters.

Short DBQ. Using the four excerpts below and your knowledge of the period, write a thesis-driven response to the following prompt:

Evaluate the extent to which reform movements in the United States from 1820 to 1848 challenged existing social and political hierarchies.

Document 1. "Ought women to do more than they are doing? … We have made her the hostess and the head of the household; but all else — the counting-room, the forum, the pulpit, the professor's chair — from all these we have barred her out. I ask whether this is consistent with the avowed principles of our country."
— Adapted from Angelina Grimké, Letters to Catharine Beecher, 1838

Document 2. "I would, at this time, most earnestly recommend that measures be taken to investigate the condition of the insane poor throughout the commonwealth… I have seen them chained, naked, beaten with rods, and lashed into obedience… Men of Massachusetts, I beg, I implore, I demand pity and protection for these of my suffering, outraged sex."
— Adapted from Dorothea Dix, Memorial to the Legislature of Massachusetts, 1843

Document 3. "The common school is the greatest discovery ever made by man… It is the only instrumentality for the universal diffusion of knowledge… a power more than material, to disarm the poor man's envy and the rich man's arrogance."
— Adapted from Horace Mann, Twelfth Annual Report of the Massachusetts Board of Education, 1848

Document 4. "Whereas the Great Governor of the Universe has seen fit to permit… the horrid traffic in our fellow-men… we therefore declare that no man has a right to enslave or imbrute his brother — to hold or acknowledge him, for one moment, as a piece of merchandise — to keep back his hire by fraud — or to brutalize his mind by denying him the means of intellectual, social, and moral improvement."
— Adapted from Declaration of Sentiments of the American Anti-Slavery Society, 1833

Rubric (abbreviated, DBQ-style)

  • Thesis (1): A historically defensible claim that takes a position on the extent of challenge (e.g., "Antebellum reformers challenged hierarchies of gender, class, mental illness, and race, but most accepted the underlying capitalist market order and often reinforced Protestant cultural authority").
  • Contextualization (1): Situate the reform surge in the Second Great Awakening, the Market Revolution, and/or Jacksonian democratization.
  • Document use (2): Use at least three documents to support the argument, accurately describing content.
  • Sourcing (1): For at least one document, explain point of view, purpose, audience, or historical situation (e.g., Dix writes as a middle-class white woman appealing to male legislators, consciously invoking gendered sympathy to secure asylum funding).
  • Evidence beyond the documents (1): Cite at least one specific fact not in the documents — e.g., Seneca Falls Declaration of Sentiments, Finney's revivals in the burned-over district, the American Temperance Society and Maine Law (1851), Brook Farm / Oneida / Shaker communities, Frederick Douglass's Narrative, the Liberator (1831), Nat Turner's rebellion, Mormon migration, or Walker's Appeal.
  • Complex understanding (1): Show nuance — e.g., that women's rights emerged from within abolitionism, that common schools both democratized and standardized Protestant Anglo culture, or that reformers' targets (slavery, patriarchy) were far more radical than their methods.

Model answer (condensed)

Between 1820 and 1848 Americans launched an unprecedented wave of voluntary reform movements — abolition, women's rights, temperance, asylum reform, common schools, and utopian experiments — that sharply challenged hierarchies of race, gender, and access to education, even as most reformers accepted (and some actively advanced) the new market order and Protestant moral authority driving the age.

These movements grew out of three reinforcing contexts: the Second Great Awakening, whose perfectionist revivals in Charles Grandison Finney's "burned-over district" taught that sin — including slavery and drunkenness — could be abolished; the Market Revolution, which created a new urban middle class with leisure time and printed media to support voluntary associations; and Jacksonian democratization, which expanded the rhetoric of equality even as it restricted it to white men.

The clearest challenges to hierarchy came from abolitionism and women's rights. Document 4, the Declaration of the American Anti-Slavery Society (1833), rejects slavery as a violation of divine and natural law and rules out gradualism or colonization — the radical immediatism of William Lloyd Garrison, Frederick Douglass, Harriet Tubman, and David Walker's Appeal. Black autonomous action — from Walker to Gabriel Prosser (1800), Denmark Vesey (1822), and Nat Turner's 1831 rebellion — pushed the movement beyond white sympathy. Abolitionism also radicalized women: when the American Anti-Slavery Society split in 1840 over women's right to speak and hold office, veterans like Elizabeth Cady Stanton and Lucretia Mott carried the argument to Seneca Falls in 1848, where the Declaration of Sentiments explicitly extended the Declaration of Independence to women. Document 1 (Angelina Grimké, 1838) anticipates that trajectory, rejecting the "separate spheres" ideology of the cult of domesticity from within evangelical reform itself — note her audience of Catharine Beecher, a defender of that ideology, which reveals the purpose: to contest gender boundaries among Protestant middle-class women.

Other reforms contested hierarchies more ambiguously. Document 2, Dorothea Dix's 1843 Memorial, reveals horrific conditions for the mentally ill — chained, beaten, confined with criminals — and demanded state-built asylums, winning hospitals across more than a dozen states. Dix's point of view is important: as a middle-class white woman addressing male legislators, she consciously deployed gendered appeals ("my sex") to secure action, working within rather than directly against patriarchal politics. Document 3, Horace Mann's 1848 Report, similarly democratized access to schooling in Massachusetts and beyond, calling the common school the "great equalizer" that could defuse class conflict — but Mann's schools also standardized a Protestant Anglo-American civic culture that excluded Catholic immigrants, and their aim of "disarming the poor man's envy" was as much social control as liberation.

Limits on the reform challenge were real. Temperance (American Temperance Society; Maine Law 1851) and Sabbatarianism often targeted Irish Catholic immigrants and working-class leisure, reinforcing ethnocultural hierarchy. Utopian communities — Brook Farm, Oneida under John Humphrey Noyes, the Shakers, New Harmony — imagined radical alternatives to patriarchal marriage and wage labor, but remained small and short-lived. The Mormons under Joseph Smith and Brigham Young, persecuted for polygamy and communal practice, ultimately trekked to Utah in 1846–47 to pursue their vision outside the republic. Most reformers, Mann included, accepted the Market Revolution's capitalist foundation and sought to moralize rather than overturn it. Transcendentalists like Emerson and Thoreau offered a philosophical critique of conformity and commerce but stood outside the organized movements.

On balance, antebellum reform challenged existing hierarchies substantially — especially the hierarchies of slavery and patriarchy that Documents 1 and 4 attacked directly — while also reshaping those hierarchies along new lines of Protestant moral respectability, middle-class self-discipline, and evangelical anti-Catholicism. The boldest reforms culminated at Seneca Falls and in the expanded abolitionist movement, bequeathing a template that the Civil War era and later suffrage and civil rights movements would inherit.
