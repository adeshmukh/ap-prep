Unit 4 · 1800–1848

Antebellum America: Jefferson to the eve of Seneca Falls

Stimulus-based multiple choice and free-response practice covering the Jeffersonian republic, the Market Revolution, Jacksonian democracy, the Second Great Awakening, and the early reform and abolitionist movements.

8 multiple choice 2 free response AP-level

Multiple choice

"It is to be regretted that the rich and powerful too often bend the acts of government to their selfish purposes. Distinctions in society will always exist under every just government. Equality of talents, of education, or of wealth can not be produced by human institutions… but when the laws undertake to add to these natural and just advantages artificial distinctions, to grant titles, gratuities, and exclusive privileges, to make the rich richer and the potent more powerful, the humble members of society… have a right to complain of the injustice of their Government."

— Adapted from Andrew Jackson, Veto Message Regarding the Bank of the United States, 1832

Jackson's argument in the excerpt most directly reflects which political tradition?

  1. Hamiltonian support for a strong, nationally chartered financial system
  2. Jeffersonian suspicion of concentrated economic privilege tied to the federal government
  3. Whig advocacy of Henry Clay's American System and internal improvements
  4. Federalist insistence on judicial review as a check on majority rule

Correct: Jeffersonian suspicion of concentrated economic privilege tied to the federal government.

Jackson consciously positioned himself as heir to Jefferson's Republican tradition, attacking the Bank as a "monster" of artificial privilege for eastern elites. The Hamiltonian answer describes the Federalist position the Bank itself embodied — the very thing Jackson was rejecting. The Clay/American System option describes Jackson's Whig opponents, who defended the Bank. Federalist judicial review is irrelevant to a presidential veto grounded in popular sovereignty and executive constitutional interpretation.

Which later nineteenth-century political development most directly extended the logic expressed in Jackson's veto?

  1. The Marshall Court's nationalist rulings in McCulloch v. Maryland and Gibbons v. Ogden
  2. The Hartford Convention's proposed amendments to limit federal power
  3. The Democratic Party's opposition to a national bank and to federally funded internal improvements through the 1850s
  4. Henry Clay's Compromise Tariff of 1833 ending the Nullification Crisis

Correct: The Democratic Party's opposition to a national bank and to federally funded internal improvements through the 1850s.

Jackson's veto became foundational for the Second Party System Democrats, who continued to oppose a central bank (Van Buren's Independent Treasury) and reject federally funded internal improvements on strict-constructionist, anti-privilege grounds. The Marshall Court rulings moved in the opposite direction, affirming federal economic power. The Hartford Convention predates the veto and was a Federalist episode. The Compromise Tariff addressed sectional nullification, not Jackson's political economy of banking.

Critics of Jackson in the 1830s most commonly responded to messages like this one by charging that he was

  1. abandoning the Monroe Doctrine's commitment to hemispheric noninterference
  2. acting as "King Andrew I" by expanding executive power beyond its constitutional bounds
  3. secretly sympathizing with the nullifiers of South Carolina
  4. conceding federal authority over Native nations to state governments

Correct: acting as "King Andrew I" by expanding executive power beyond its constitutional bounds.

Whig cartoons famously depicted Jackson as "King Andrew I" trampling the Constitution after his vetoes and removal of federal deposits. The Monroe Doctrine answer is chronologically and topically off. Jackson in fact opposed South Carolina's nullifiers, issuing the Nullification Proclamation and backing the Force Bill. The Native-nations option describes something Jackson actually did in the Indian Removal/Worcester episodes but it was not the typical Whig critique of the Bank Veto itself.

"I am aware that many object to the severity of my language; but is there not cause for severity? I will be as harsh as truth, and as uncompromising as justice. On this subject, I do not wish to think, or speak, or write, with moderation… I am in earnest — I will not equivocate — I will not excuse — I will not retreat a single inch — AND I WILL BE HEARD."

— Adapted from William Lloyd Garrison, The Liberator, 1831

Garrison's stance in the excerpt broke most sharply from earlier antislavery activity because it

  1. called for gradual, compensated emancipation coupled with colonization of freedpeople abroad
  2. demanded the immediate, uncompensated abolition of slavery on moral grounds
  3. urged enslaved people to wait for federal legislation rather than resist
  4. relied on the Three-Fifths Compromise to argue slavery would die out naturally

Correct: demanded the immediate, uncompensated abolition of slavery on moral grounds.

Garrison's "immediatism" rejected both gradualism and the American Colonization Society's scheme to relocate free Black Americans to Liberia. The gradual-and-colonize option describes precisely what Garrison opposed. The "wait for legislation" option contradicts the urgency of the passage. The Three-Fifths answer describes an eighteenth-century compromise, not an antislavery argument.

Which 1831 event most directly hardened Southern white resistance to the kind of abolitionist appeal in the excerpt?

  1. The Hartford Convention
  2. Nat Turner's Rebellion in Southampton County, Virginia
  3. The passage of the Missouri Compromise
  4. Worcester v. Georgia

Correct: Nat Turner's Rebellion in Southampton County, Virginia.

Turner's uprising in August 1831, coming months after Garrison founded The Liberator, was blamed by Southern elites on abolitionist "incendiary" literature and led to tighter slave codes, restrictions on Black literacy, and the gag rule in Congress. The Hartford Convention (1814) and the Missouri Compromise (1820) are earlier and unrelated to Garrison's arrival. Worcester v. Georgia (1832) concerned Cherokee sovereignty, not slavery.

Garrison's tone and rhetoric were most directly shaped by which broader cultural movement of the 1820s and 1830s?

  1. The classical republicanism of the Jeffersonian Republicans
  2. The Second Great Awakening's emphasis on individual moral perfection and conversion
  3. The Transcendentalist embrace of Asian philosophical texts
  4. The nativist response to Irish Catholic immigration

Correct: The Second Great Awakening's emphasis on individual moral perfection and conversion.

Revivalists like Charles Finney taught that sin — including participation in slavery — was a personal choice that could be renounced, fueling immediatist reform. Classical republicanism focused on civic virtue and liberty from monarchy, not moral uplift. Transcendentalism influenced a few reformers (Thoreau, Alcott) but its Eastern-philosophy strand did not drive Garrison's biblical moral absolutism. Nativism cut against reform coalitions rather than fueling abolitionism.

"We hold these truths to be self-evident: that all men and women are created equal; that they are endowed by their Creator with certain inalienable rights… The history of mankind is a history of repeated injuries and usurpations on the part of man toward woman, having in direct object the establishment of an absolute tyranny over her."

— Adapted from the Declaration of Sentiments, Seneca Falls Convention, 1848

The rhetorical structure of the excerpt most clearly demonstrates which historical thinking move?

  1. Sectional compromise by invoking the language of the Missouri agreement of 1820
  2. Contextualization by appropriating the Declaration of Independence to claim the Revolution's universal principles for women
  3. Continuity by reaffirming the "republican motherhood" framework of the 1790s
  4. Comparison by contrasting American women with enslaved women in the South

Correct: Contextualization by appropriating the Declaration of Independence to claim the Revolution's universal principles for women.

Stanton's document deliberately mirrors Jefferson's 1776 text — right down to "self-evident" and the list of grievances — to locate women's rights inside the Revolution's ideological legacy. The Missouri Compromise option is unrelated. "Republican motherhood" located women's civic role inside the household; Seneca Falls explicitly rejected that confinement. While comparison to slavery did appear in abolitionist-feminist rhetoric, the structural move of the excerpt is the appropriation of the Declaration, not a slavery comparison.

Which pairing best illustrates how the Market Revolution and the Marshall Court together reshaped the antebellum United States?

  1. The Erie Canal and the Treaty of Ghent opened the Ohio Valley to federal land sales
  2. Gibbons v. Ogden and the spread of steamboats and canals tied regional economies into a single national market
  3. McCulloch v. Maryland and the Embargo Act of 1807 strengthened domestic manufacturing
  4. Worcester v. Georgia and the Lowell textile mills accelerated Indian Removal

Correct: Gibbons v. Ogden and the spread of steamboats and canals tied regional economies into a single national market.

Gibbons (1824) placed interstate commerce under federal authority and dissolved state-granted monopolies on steamboats, clearing the legal ground for the transportation revolution (Erie Canal, 1825; rail and telegraph in the 1830s–40s) to integrate the Northeast, Old Northwest, and cotton South. The Erie Canal/Treaty of Ghent pairing mixes domestic and diplomatic items that did not jointly produce land-sale policy. The Embargo Act did spur some manufacturing but McCulloch (1819) concerned the Bank, not manufacturing, and the two are not a natural causal pair. Worcester limited state power over the Cherokee but Jackson ignored it; Lowell mills are unrelated to Removal.

Free response

SAQ. Using your knowledge of United States history in the period 1800–1848, answer (a), (b), and (c).

(a) Briefly explain ONE specific way in which the Market Revolution changed the lives of women in the North between 1800 and 1848.

(b) Briefly explain ONE specific way in which the policies of Andrew Jackson's administration (1829–1837) expanded democratic participation for white men.

(c) Briefly explain ONE specific way in which the same Jacksonian era restricted the rights or sovereignty of a group other than white men.

Rubric

  • (a) Identify a specific change (wage work in textile mills like Lowell; the "cult of domesticity" redefining middle-class women's role; greater female participation in reform/benevolent societies) AND tie it to market-revolution forces.
  • (b) Identify a specific expansion of white-male democracy (elimination of property qualifications, spoils system/"rotation in office," rise of national nominating conventions, Jackson's veto as a direct-democratic tool).
  • (c) Identify a specific restriction on a non-white-male group (Indian Removal Act 1830 and Trail of Tears; Jackson's refusal to enforce Worcester v. Georgia; Black disfranchisement in new state constitutions; tightened slave codes after Nat Turner; continued exclusion of women from suffrage).

Model answer

(a) The Market Revolution pulled young women out of the farm household into the new textile economy. At the Lowell mills in Massachusetts, "mill girls" earned their own cash wages, lived in company boardinghouses, and organized the 1834 and 1836 turn-outs against wage cuts — a form of economic independence impossible in the pre-1815 household economy.

(b) Jackson championed "rotation in office" (the spoils system), replacing career federal officeholders with party loyalists on the grounds that any ordinary white man could perform government work. Combined with the elimination of property qualifications for voting in most states by the late 1820s and the rise of national nominating conventions, this reframed federal office as the property of the common voter rather than a gentry class.

(c) The Indian Removal Act of 1830 and Jackson's refusal to enforce the Supreme Court's ruling in Worcester v. Georgia (1832) stripped the Cherokee and other southeastern nations of their treaty-guaranteed sovereignty, producing the forced march of the Trail of Tears in 1838 under Van Buren. Jacksonian "democracy" for white men was built on the contraction of Native sovereignty.

Short DBQ-style prompt. Using the three excerpts below and your knowledge of the period, develop an argument that evaluates the extent to which the reform movements of the 1820s–1840s represented a continuation of, or a break from, the republican ideals of the American Revolution.

Document 1. "Our contest is not merely whether we ourselves shall be free, but whether there shall be left on the globe any portion of the human race susceptible of freedom."

— Adapted from Thomas Jefferson, letter to John Adams, 1821

Document 2. "The great object to be sought is the education of the whole people… In a republic, ignorance is a crime; and private immorality is not less an opprobrium to the state than it is a guilt in the individual. A free government cannot long survive an ignorant and vicious people."

— Adapted from Horace Mann, Twelfth Annual Report of the Massachusetts Board of Education, 1848

Document 3. "The question is not whether the black man's condition would be improved by emancipation… the question is whether a nation conceived in liberty can continue half slave and half free without making a mockery of the Declaration."

— Adapted from a speech by Frederick Douglass, c. 1847

Rubric

  • Thesis (1 pt): Historically defensible claim that evaluates continuity/change, not merely restates the prompt. Example: "Antebellum reform largely extended the Revolution's republican ideals of liberty and self-government, but abolitionism and women's rights also broke from the founders by applying those ideals to groups the Revolution had explicitly excluded."
  • Contextualization (1 pt): Situate in the Second Great Awakening, Market Revolution, and Jacksonian democratization.
  • Document use (2 pts): Use all three documents; explain Jefferson's Revolutionary universalism (Doc 1), Mann's republican argument for public education (Doc 2), and Douglass's radical reapplication of the Declaration (Doc 3).
  • Outside evidence (1 pt): At least one specific fact not in the documents: Seneca Falls, Garrison's Liberator, temperance societies, Dorothea Dix's asylum reform, Nat Turner's Rebellion, utopian communities (Brook Farm, Oneida), Finney's revivals.
  • Complexity (1 pt): Acknowledge tensions — e.g., Mann's common schools sought to "Americanize" Irish Catholic immigrants; abolitionism split the women's movement; Douglass broke with Garrison over the Constitution.

Model answer (condensed)

Thesis. Antebellum reform drew heavily on the Revolution's republican vocabulary of liberty, virtue, and popular government, but movements like abolitionism and women's rights pushed those ideals well beyond what the founders intended, making the 1820s–1840s a period of radical continuity.

Contextualization. The Second Great Awakening (Finney, Beecher) placed moral perfection within reach of ordinary Americans; simultaneously the Market Revolution — Erie Canal (1825), Lowell mills, telegraph (1844) — created new social dislocations (drunkenness, urban poverty, female wage labor) that reformers interpreted through a republican lens of virtue and citizenship.

Evidence + documents. Jefferson's 1821 letter (Doc 1) shows the founders themselves framing American liberty in universal terms, giving reformers a usable inheritance. Mann (Doc 2) argued that public common schools were necessary to sustain republican government — a classic continuity with Revolutionary fears of an ignorant electorate, and matched by Dorothea Dix's campaign for state asylums and temperance societies that sought to produce virtuous citizens. Douglass (Doc 3) invokes the Declaration to indict slavery — the Revolution's unfinished business — and Garrison's 1831 launch of The Liberator, Nat Turner's 1831 rebellion, and the 1848 Declaration of Sentiments at Seneca Falls all show reformers extending "all men are created equal" to groups the founders had excluded.

Complexity. The movement was not monolithic: Mann's common schools aimed to assimilate Irish Catholic immigrants into a Protestant civic order; Douglass eventually split with Garrison over whether the Constitution was a proslavery document; and many white male reformers resisted Seneca Falls. These tensions show reform both fulfilling and straining the Revolution's promise.
